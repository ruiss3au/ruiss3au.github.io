Dalian was the final RAN1 meeting before the RAN#112 plenary freeze. Rel-20
accounted for 93 percent of contributions. Rel-19 had effectively vanished. The
meeting was a 6G specification assembly line, and FS_6G_Radio had become so
dominant that it accounted for more than two-thirds of all contributions. Every
other work item in the meeting combined was smaller than FS_6G_Radio.

The company distribution was the flattest of the series. Twelve companies fell
within a single-digit margin at the top. Samsung, HiSilicon, vivo, Sanechips,
OPPO, Qualcomm, CATT, Apple, Ericsson, NEC, Nokia, and Xiaomi all clustered
within a range narrower than any previous meeting. When the specification freeze
approaches, every company with a stake in the outcome files contributions to
protect its interests. The flat distribution reflected the freeze pressure.

# FS_6G_Radio: The Final Push Before Freeze

FS_6G_Radio at 1,138 contributions was not just the largest work item in the
series. It was larger than any meeting prior to RAN1#124. The volume was driven
by the approaching RAN#112 plenary deadline: every open issue had to be
resolved, every parameter had to be agreed, every company position had to be
recorded.

## Waveform Parameters Finalized

The enhanced OFDM waveform parameters were finalized at this meeting. The
subcarrier spacing set was confirmed at 120, 240, 480, and 960 kHz, with 480
kHz as the baseline for the 7-24 GHz range. The cyclic prefix lengths were
scaled proportionally to subcarrier spacing, maintaining the same absolute
guard time (approximately 0.07 microseconds for normal CP) across all
numerologies. The spectral containment improvement, achieved through time-domain
windowing, was specified as a 10 dB reduction in out-of-band emissions at the
channel edge compared to NR OFDM.

OTFS was confirmed as an optional waveform for high-Doppler operation. The
activation conditions (Doppler shift exceeding 2 kHz, corresponding to 500 km/h
at 3.5 GHz or 60 km/h at 30 GHz) and the fallback procedure (automatic reversion
to enhanced OFDM when Doppler drops below the threshold) were specified.

While OFDM variants dominated the waveform discussion, a parallel thread on
low-PAPR uplink waveforms gained traction at Dalian. The problem: OFDM's high
peak-to-average power ratio forces the UE power amplifier to operate with
significant back-off, reducing transmit power and coverage. For the 7-24 GHz
range where path loss is higher and every dB of transmit power matters, this
back-off penalty is particularly costly.

Companies proposed that 6G should include a mandatory low-PAPR uplink waveform
option alongside enhanced OFDM. The leading candidate was DFT-s-OFDM (already
used in NR for uplink control and coverage-limited scenarios), but proposals
also included new designs with better spectral containment than DFT-s-OFDM
while maintaining similar PAPR reduction. Skyworks contributed on the MPR and
power boosting framework for low-PAPR waveforms [analysis from R4-2605499],
arguing from the RF component perspective that a 3-4 dB reduction in PAPR
translates directly to 3-4 dB better uplink coverage, or equivalently, 3-4 dB
lower PA power consumption for the same coverage. The waveform decision had
direct RF component implications: a low-PAPR waveform reduces the PA
linearity requirements, which reduces PA cost and power consumption, a feedback
loop between RAN1 waveform design and RAN4 RF requirements that was one of the
central coordination challenges of 6G specification.

## 6G KPI Targets Locked

The 6G key performance indicator targets that had been discussed since the
FS_6G_Radio launch in Bangalore were finalized:

- Peak data rate: 100 Gbps downlink, 50 Gbps uplink
- User plane latency: 0.1 ms one-way
- Positioning accuracy: 1 cm horizontal, 1 cm vertical in indoor deployments
- Spectral efficiency: 3x improvement over NR at the same frequency and antenna
configuration
- Energy efficiency: 10x improvement in energy per bit compared to NR
- Connection density: 10 million devices per square kilometer
- Mobility: support for speeds up to 1000 km/h

These targets were ambitious but grounded in the technical work that RAN1 had
conducted over the preceding four meetings. The 100 Gbps peak rate requires 2
GHz of aggregated bandwidth at 64QAM with 8 MIMO layers, achievable with 10
component carriers of 200 MHz each in the 7-24 GHz range. The 0.1 ms latency
requires the 960 kHz subcarrier spacing with 0.0625 ms slot duration and
self-contained slot structure where transmission and acknowledgment occur in the
same slot.

## Coexistence and Spectrum Sharing

A new thread within FS_6G_Radio addressed coexistence between 6G and incumbent
services in the 7-24 GHz range. The frequency range is shared with fixed
satellite service (FSS), fixed service (FS), radio astronomy, and passive
sensing. 6G deployments must protect these services through power limits,
antenna pattern restrictions, and geographic exclusion zones.

FUTUREWEI continued the downlink shared channel design discussion
[R1-2603552]. Apple contributed on downlink control channel and scheduling
for 6G [R1-2603863], Sharp filed parallel control channel work [R1-2604660],
and NEC [R1-2603806] and Ofinno [R1-2604209] discussed contention-based
PUSCH transmission evaluation assumptions. Deutsche Telekom contributed on 6G
ISAC deployment scenarios, evaluation methodology, and performance requirements
[R1-2604580], emphasizing the operator perspective on sensing as a service.
HFCL discussed FWA deployment scenarios, CPE prequalification, and antenna
configurations [R1-2604560]. NICT contributed on downlink wake-up
signal-assisted PDCCH monitoring [R1-2604888].

The coexistence work defined the protection criteria for each incumbent service
and the corresponding 6G transmitter restrictions. For FSS protection in the
14.0-14.5 GHz uplink band, 6G base stations are limited to 40 dBm EIRP per 200
MHz carrier with antenna elevation angles below 30 degrees. For radio astronomy
protection in the 15.35-15.4 GHz band, 6G deployments are prohibited within
coordination zones around radio astronomy sites.

### Single Cell Multi-Carrier (SCMC)

The Gothia-cell concept advanced at Dalian with contributions from multiple
companies. OPPO proposed that the Bandwidth Part (BWP) concept should be
decoupled from numerology and extended to support non-continuous frequency
resources for SCMC scenarios, where a single cell spans multiple carriers
with a unified control plane [R1-2505757 analysis]. The core idea: one
component carrier handles all control (SSB, PDCCH, CSI-RS, SRS), while
additional carriers serve only data (PDSCH/PUSCH), without their own
synchronization or control channels.

Nokia pushed back, arguing that SCMC does not change the fundamental RF
architecture: whether a UE receives data on one carrier or three, the RF
front-end requirements (ACLR, EVM, MPR) are the same. Nokia's position was
that SCMC is a scheduling and RRC optimization, not an RF specification change,
and RAN4 should not create new work items for it. vivo analyzed the RF impacts
and concluded that SCMC imposes no fundamentally new UE RF requirements but
does require specification of how RRM measurements from the anchor carrier
apply to data carriers at different frequencies - the measurement validity
across carriers is the open regulatory and performance question.

The SCMC debate reflected a broader tension in 6G: between network
simplification (fewer cells, fewer SSBs, less overhead) and specification
simplicity (don't create new work items for what CA already solves).

# Ambient IoT Phase 2: From Study to Specification

Ambient IoT Solutions Phase 2 (Ambient_IoT_Solutions_Ph2-Core) transitioned
from the Rel-19/Rel-20 studies to a normative work item. The Phase 2 scope
covered both indoor and outdoor ambient IoT, consolidating the separate study
items into a single specification program.

The indoor work defined short-range ambient IoT for factory and warehouse
applications. The outdoor work defined longer-range ambient IoT.

Spreadtrum contributed on R2D signals and procedures [R1-2603577] and D2R
signals and procedures [R1-2603578]. ASUSTeK discussed D2R multiplexing and
multiple access for active devices [R1-2604795]. Huawei [R1-2605103] and
HiSilicon [R1-2604926] coordinated liaison statements with SA WG2 on the
Rel-20 ambient IoT TR conclusions.

# AI/ML Phase 2: Case 0 Completes

The AI/ML Phase 2 work reached completion for Case 0. The CSI
spatial/frequency compression specification was finalized with standardized
tensor formats (input: [N_ports x N_subbands] complex matrix, output: [K]
real-valued vector where K is the compression dimension), performance
requirements (reconstruction normalized mean squared error below 0.1 at the
90th percentile), and inference latency budgets (0.5 ms for UE encoder, 0.2 ms
for gNB decoder).

The inter-vendor training question was deferred to an industry collaboration
outside 3GPP. The specification defined the inference interface but not the
training framework. This was effectively the proprietary-model position winning:
vendors train their own models, the specification guarantees only that the
compressed CSI format is compatible.

# MIMO Phase 6: Final Codebook Design

MIMO Phase 6 finalized the distributed MIMO codebook at this meeting. The
two-stage approach agreed at Gothenburg was specified in detail: Stage 1 TRP
selection uses a bitmap of length N_TRPs with up to 3 bits set, Stage 2 CSI
quantization uses an enhanced Type II codebook with W1 (spatial basis) and W2
(combining coefficients) for each selected TRP. The feedback overhead for 4-TRP
CSI was bounded at 200 bits per reporting instance, comparable to 2-TRP CSI in
NR.

# IoT NTN Phase 4: GNSS-Resilient and Ka-Band

IoT NTN Phase 4 (IoT_NTN_Ph4-Core) appeared at this meeting, the successor to
the IoT NTN Phase 3 work that had been active throughout the series. Phase 4
incorporated the GNSS-resilient positioning techniques developed in the
FS_NR_NTN_GNSS_resilient study item and extended the NTN IoT work to Ka-band
for higher-capacity satellite IoT links.

THALES maintained TR 38.742 through version 1.1.0 [R1-2605000], consolidating
the study conclusions. Nokia discussed GNSS-resilient operation for NR over
NTN [R1-2604521]. Sony contributed observations and conclusions on NR NTN
GNSS resilience [R1-2604584, R1-2604951]. The European Commission discussed
remaining issues [R1-2604904], providing an EU regulatory perspective.

# Company Dynamics: The Freeze Distribution

The company distribution at Dalian was a perfect illustration of freeze
dynamics. Twelve companies within a narrow band at the top: Samsung, HiSilicon,
vivo, Sanechips, OPPO, Qualcomm, CATT, Apple, Ericsson, NEC, Nokia, Xiaomi.
When the specification freeze approaches, every company files its final
positions. Companies that had been quiet in earlier meetings file contributions
to ensure their interests are recorded. The result is a flat distribution that
reflects strategic positioning rather than technical leadership.

NEC's appearance in the top tier was notable. The Japanese company had been a
mid-tier contributor through the series, but its focus on 6G energy efficiency
and the coverage Phase 3 work item gave it outsized influence at the freeze
meeting. vivo and OPPO, both Chinese smartphone vendors, rose to the top tier
alongside the traditional infrastructure and chipset leaders.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#119 and RAN#112

RAN1#125 was the last RAN1 meeting before the Rel-20 Stage-2 scope was frozen at
RAN#112 in June 2026. The decisions made here, on waveform parameters, KPI
targets, codebook designs, and AI/ML specifications, would become the 6G
physical layer baseline. The RAN#112 plenary would approve the scope, and the
work would shift from design to specification completion.

The next and final meeting in this series is RAN4#119 in Dalian, held
immediately after this RAN1 meeting in the same city. RAN4#119 would be the
last RAN4 meeting before RAN#112. It would show how much of RAN1's 6G design
output had cascaded into RAN4 requirements, and how much would be deferred to
post-freeze specification work.
