Gothenburg in February was the mirror image of Gothenburg in January. RAN1#124
had been the 6G design peak, with FS_6G_Radio consuming half the meeting.
RAN4#118, held two months later in the same city, showed RAN4 beginning to
catch up. Rel-20 grew from 15 percent at Dallas to 27 percent. FS_6G_Radio grew
from 230 to 371 contributions. Rel-20 carrier aggregation work began. The Rel-19
-Perf work continued but was no longer the dominant force.

The 27 percent Rel-20 share was still far from RAN1's 81 percent a month
earlier. The structural lag persisted. But the growth rate was accelerating:
Dallas had been the inflection point, and Gothenburg confirmed the trend.

# FS_6G_Radio Accelerates in RAN4

FS_6G_Radio's growth from Dallas reflected both more contributors engaging with
6G and deeper technical content. Where Dallas had been about specification
efficiency and framework questions, Gothenburg added specific technical studies:
channel bandwidth implications for RF requirements, MPR and ACLR trade-offs
for 6G waveforms, and RRM measurement framework design for the 7-24 GHz range.

The channel bandwidth work was the most practically significant. RAN1 had
selected enhanced OFDM as the baseline 6G waveform and was defining
numerologies up to 960 kHz subcarrier spacing. These decisions translate
directly into RAN4 RF requirements: a 200 MHz channel with 480 kHz subcarrier
spacing has different ACLR characteristics than a 100 MHz channel with 30 kHz
subcarrier spacing, because the wider subcarrier spacing reduces the relative
guard band but increases the absolute occupied bandwidth.

The RAN4 contributions on 6G operation efficiency continued from Dallas, with
companies proposing specific specification structure improvements: parameterized
MPR formulas, unified ACLR limits that scale with channel bandwidth, and RRM
measurement gap designs for 6G slot durations.

vivo contributed on frequency range definitions and spectrum regulatory survey
for 6G [R4-2600669], the foundational spectrum scoping work. Ericsson
contributed on channel and synchronization raster arrangements [R4-2601382]
and provided input to the ECC PT1 request for AAS BS operating below 1 GHz
[R4-2601079], linking RAN4's 6G work to European regulatory processes. Nokia
contributed on frequency ranges and spectrum regulatory survey [R4-2601665]
and on band and band combination definition and simplification [R4-2601666].
Nokia also filed initial simulation results on transmitter EVM relaxation and
its impacts on the BS receiver [R4-2601765], a direct RAN4 contribution to
the waveform-decision evidence base.

The meeting also saw the first RAN4-driven AI/ML use case proposals. CATT
contributed on AI-based SRS power imbalance compensation [R4-2600251] and
AI-based non-linearity compensation [R4-2600250], proposing that AI/ML could
improve RF performance in specific transmitter and receiver scenarios without
requiring changes to the air interface specification. These were RAN4-native
AI proposals, independent of the RAN1 AI/ML work, reflecting RAN4's growing
confidence in defining its own AI/ML scope.

# Rel-20 Carrier Aggregation Begins

The most significant new development at RAN4#118 was the appearance of Rel-20
carrier aggregation work items. NR_CADC_SUL_R20_HPUE and DC_xBLTE_yBNR_R20_HPUE
were the Rel-20 equivalents of the Rel-19 carrier aggregation work that had been
a constant background thread through every RAN4 meeting.

## HPUE for Rel-20 CA

NR_CADC_SUL_R20_HPUE addressed high-power UE operation for Rel-20 carrier
aggregation and supplementary uplink. HPUE (power class 2, 26 dBm) is critical
for the 7-24 GHz range because the higher path loss at these frequencies
requires higher UE transmit power to maintain coverage. The Rel-20 HPUE work
defined MPR and A-MPR values for UE transmissions on 7-24 GHz carriers,
accounting for the intermodulation products that arise when aggregating a 7-24
GHz carrier with a sub-7 GHz anchor carrier.

Samsung discussed adaptive changes with HPUE notes removal in Rel-19 and HPUE
request handling in Rel-20 [R4-2600371]. Apple contributed CSI-RS based
L1-RSRP accuracy requirements for neighbor cells in FR1 [R4-2600558] and FR2
[R4-2600559], the RRM measurement refinement work that underpins RRM Phase 6.

Ericsson filed draft Big CRs for TS 38.101-1 and TS 38.101-3 for Rel-20 HPUE
carrier aggregation [R4-2601776, R4-2601777], defining MPR and A-MPR values
for the first set of Rel-20 band combinations. Nokia filed draft CRs on power
class 2 and power class 1.5 in CA combinations [R4-2601672, R4-2602217].
CATT contributed CRs for TS 38.194 on ambient IoT framework simplification for
co-location and coexistence requirements [R4-2600209, R4-2602935], and LG
Electronics UK filed a draft CR for inclusion of outdoor active device study
outcomes [R4-2601431].

The intermodulation challenge was different from sub-7 GHz CA. When a UE
transmits simultaneously on a 3.5 GHz carrier and a 15 GHz carrier, the
third-order intermodulation products fall at widely separated frequencies
(approximately 8 GHz and 26.5 GHz), which are far from the receiver bands at
either 3.5 GHz or 15 GHz. This is easier than sub-7 GHz CA where
intermodulation products can fall directly in the receiver band. The Rel-20
HPUE MPR values were correspondingly less restrictive than Rel-19 values,
allowing higher transmit power on 7-24 GHz carriers.

## DC R20: Dual Connectivity for the 6G Era

DC_xBLTE_yBNR_R20_HPUE continued the EN-DC and NR-DC dual connectivity work
into Rel-20. The work item defined new band combinations that include 7-24 GHz
NR carriers aggregated with LTE or NR anchor carriers. The dual connectivity
framework was particularly important for the 6G transition: early 6G deployments
would use EN-DC or NR-DC with a sub-7 GHz anchor for coverage and a 7-24 GHz
secondary cell for capacity, mirroring the 5G NSA deployment model.

# AI/ML Mobility: RAN4's Normative Work Expands

NR_AIML_Mob-Core, which had debuted at Dallas, grew to become a significant work
item at Gothenburg. The normative work now covered specific RRM measurement
configurations: how the UE reports an AI/ML-predicted cell quality measurement
alongside the conventional SSB-based measurement, what confidence thresholds the
UE applies before using the predicted measurement for cell reselection or
handover reporting, and how the network configures the trade-off between
prediction accuracy and measurement overhead.

The mobility AI/ML work benefited from the consensus that had emerged in RAN1:
mobility prediction is a vendor-differentiation feature, not a standardized
model. RAN4's job was to define the measurement reporting framework that
accommodates AI/ML-generated measurements without mandating how the AI/ML model
works. This scope was narrower and less contentious than the air interface
AI/ML work, and the specification progressed rapidly.

CATT contributed CRs for TS 38.194 on ambient IoT framework simplification for
co-location and coexistence requirements [R4-2600209, R4-2602935] and filed
a technical report contribution for TS 38.195 on the same topic
[R4-2600210]. LG Electronics UK filed a draft CR for inclusion of outdoor
active device study outcomes [R4-2601431], transitioning the study conclusions
into normative specification text.

# Rel-19 -Perf Work Winds Down

The Rel-19 -Perf work items that had dominated Dallas continued at Gothenburg
but at reduced volume. SBFD performance testing dropped from 70 to 91
contributions (a modest increase, suggesting final issue resolution), MIMO
Phase 5 conformance dropped from 82 to 68, and ATG enhancement performance
dropped from 62 to 22.

The remaining Rel-19 work was issue resolution: closing the last open points,
correcting errors in previously agreed CRs, and finalizing the conformance test
procedures. The work items were not generating new requirements. They were
polishing existing ones.

NR_demod_Ph6-Perf, the Rel-20 demodulation performance Phase 6 work item,
contributed to the SCM-based testing framework. Nokia filed simulation results
for 6Rx UE performance with interference [R4-2600403]. Samsung discussed
FR1 SU-MIMO performance requirements with spatial channel model [R4-2600714],
and Sanechips contributed parallel discussion on demodulation requirements
[R4-2601243].

# Company Dynamics: Ericsson Leads at Home

Ericsson led the contribution count at its home meeting for the second month in
a row, following its RAN1#124 leadership. HiSilicon held second, maintaining its
consistent presence across all RAN4 meetings. Sanechips rose to third place,
driven by UE chipset contributions to the Rel-20 carrier aggregation work and
6G RF specification enhancement.

Nokia dropped to fourth, an unusual position for the company that had been a
consistent top-three contributor throughout the series. The drop reflected
Nokia's relatively lower engagement with the Rel-20 carrier aggregation work,
which was the primary growth area at this meeting. CATT and Apple rounded out
the upper tier.

OPPO rose significantly, joining the upper tier for the first time in the RAN4
series. OPPO's growth was driven by contributions across multiple Rel-20 work
items: FS_6G_Radio, AI/ML mobility, and carrier aggregation.

# Looking Ahead to RAN1#125

RAN4#118 was the meeting where RAN4's Rel-20 program became self-sustaining.
FS_6G_Radio, Rel-20 carrier aggregation, AI/ML mobility, and IoT NTN Phase 2
formed a coherent Rel-20 work program that no longer depended on Rel-19
momentum. The Rel-19 -Perf work was winding down, but the Rel-20 work was
growing fast enough to replace it.

The next meeting in the series is RAN1#125, the final RAN1 meeting before
the RAN#112 plenary that would freeze the Rel-20 Stage-2 scope. RAN1#125 would
be the last chance for companies to influence the 6G physical layer design
before the freeze deadline. The meeting was expected to be nearly entirely
Rel-20, with the final waveform parameters, MIMO configurations, and AI/ML
scope decisions being locked in.
