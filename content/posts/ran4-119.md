Dalian was the meeting where RAN4 tipped. Rel-20 contributions outnumbered
Rel-19 for the first time in the series. The margin, 41 percent to 13 percent,
was not the landslide of RAN1#125's 93 percent a week earlier in the same city.
But the crossing of the threshold, after eight meetings where Rel-19 had
dominated, was the structural event that the entire series had been marching
toward.

The context was the approaching RAN#112 plenary in Singapore, four weeks away.
That plenary would freeze the Rel-20 Stage-2 scope. All feature decisions had to
be locked. The Dalian RAN4 meeting was the last chance for the group to submit
its RF feasibility assessments, its RRM measurement frameworks, and its
conformance test plans to the plenary. The meeting had the urgency of a
deadline.

# FS_6G_Radio: RAN4's 6G Specification Framework

FS_6G_Radio at Dalian was the culmination of RAN4's 6G work that had started at
Dallas five months earlier. The operation efficiency discussions from Dallas and
Gothenburg had produced concrete specification framework proposals. The RAN1
waveform, numerology, and MIMO decisions from Gothenburg and Dalian had been
absorbed into RAN4's RF requirement work.

The 6G specification framework defined at this meeting included: a waveform-
agnostic transmitter requirement structure where MPR, ACLR, and EVM are
functions of waveform parameters rather than hard-coded for OFDM, a channel-
bandwidth-scalable receiver requirement framework where REFSENS, ACS, and
blocking limits are continuous functions of channel bandwidth, and an RRM
measurement framework that accommodates the shorter slot durations and wider
subcarrier spacings of 6G.

The framework was designed to be forward-compatible: if a new waveform or
numerology is added in a future release, the RAN4 requirements automatically
adapt through the parameterized formulas rather than requiring new requirement
definitions. This was the specification efficiency improvement that MediaTek
and Nokia had advocated since Dallas.

Sanechips contributed views on 6G demodulation aspects of receiver assumptions,
interference modeling, and transmitter EVM assumptions [R4-2606297]. Nokia
contributed on 6G SI demodulation covering receiver assumptions, interference
profile modeling, and TxEVM assumptions [R4-2605226]. CATT discussed receiver
assumptions and interference modeling for 6G demodulation [R4-2606352].
These contributions addressed the practical measurement framework: how should
conformance tests model the interference environment at 7-24 GHz where
interference characteristics differ from sub-7 GHz?

RAN4 also provided its RF feasibility assessment for the RAN#112 plenary. The
assessment confirmed that the 6G KPI targets (100 Gbps peak rate, 0.1 ms
latency, 10x energy efficiency) were achievable with the agreed waveform and
MIMO configurations, but with specific caveats: the 100 Gbps peak rate requires
at least 8 MIMO layers and 256QAM modulation, which demands high-SINR
conditions that are only achievable at short range with line-of-sight; the 0.1
ms latency requires integrated access and backhaul or fiber fronthaul to
coordinate multi-TRP transmission; and the 10x energy efficiency improvement
assumes network-level sleep modes that reduce always-on transmission, not just
PHY-level efficiency gains.

# Ambient IoT Phase 2: RAN4's Conformance Framework

Ambient IoT Phase 2 became the second-largest Rel-20 work item at RAN4#119, the
RAN4 counterpart to the RAN1 normative work that had launched at Dalian a week
earlier. RAN4's work defined the conformance testing framework for ambient IoT
devices and readers: OTA test methods for passive and active devices, reader
sensitivity and transmit power conformance tests, and coexistence testing with
NR and LTE in shared spectrum.

The OTA test method debate that had split Apple and vivo at Bangalore was
resolved with a hybrid approach: conducted tests for chipset certification and
OTA tests for device-level certification.

CATT contributed on coexistence between active device 2b and NR/LTE
[R4-2606250] and coexistence between active device C and NR/LTE [R4-2606247],
addressing the practical deployment question of whether ambient IoT devices
interfere with existing NR and LTE UEs in shared spectrum. LG Electronics
filed CRs to TS 38.191 for correcting D2R transmission bandwidth
[R4-2607193, R4-2607195]. HiSilicon filed a CR for TS 38.195 to correct ACLR
requirements [R4-2607275].

# Rel-20 Carrier Aggregation Expands

NR_CADC_SUL_R20_HPUE grew to become the third-largest work item, continuing the
Rel-20 carrier aggregation work that had started at Gothenburg. New band
combinations were added for 7-24 GHz carriers, new MPR and A-MPR values were
defined, and the HPUE framework was extended to support power class 1.5 (29
dBm) for fixed wireless access devices in the 7-24 GHz range.

Ericsson filed CRs to TS 38.101-1, 38.101-2, and 38.101-3 to remove
configuration tables and introduce a band combination database
[R4-2606379, R4-2606380, R4-2606381], a specification structure improvement
that addresses the combinatorial explosion of band combinations. CHTTL filed
a draft CR for power class 1.5 uplink transmission with three transmit chains
[R4-2606554]. T-Mobile USA submitted a further revised WID for Rel-20 NR
carrier aggregation and dual connectivity [R4-2607184].

DC_xBLTE_yBNR_R20_HPUE continued the dual connectivity band combination work.
A new work item, NR_Bands_BW_PC_BC_RedCap_R20, appeared to define the Rel-20
band, bandwidth, power class, and band combination support for RedCap devices.

# AI/ML: Mobility and Air Interface Convergence

NR_AIML_Mob-Core and NR_AIML_air_Ph2-Core appeared together at this meeting,
reflecting the convergence of mobility and air interface AI/ML work. The
mobility AI/ML specification defined RRM measurement configurations for
AI/ML-assisted cell reselection and handover. The air interface AI/ML work
from RAN1 was beginning to produce RAN4 conformance requirements, though the
full conformance framework would be developed after RAN#112.

# RRM Phase 6: Measurement Gaps for 7-24 GHz

NR_RRM_Ph6-Core, the work item that Apple had pitched since Athens in January
2025, was now an active normative work item at the final meeting of the series.
The Dalian contributions focused on measurement gap design for the 7-24 GHz
range. The 6G frame structure with 480 kHz subcarrier spacing and 0.125 ms slot
duration means that measurement gaps must be shorter and more frequent than in
NR. The Dalian RRM Phase 6 work defined measurement gap patterns with 0.5 ms
periodicity and 0.0625 ms duration, allowing RRM measurements to be interleaved
with data transmission at the slot level rather than requiring dedicated
measurement gaps that interrupt data flow.

A distinct sub-thread within RRM Phase 6 was AI-assisted RRM. CMCC contributed
on AI-RRM Sub-Case 1 covering FR2-1 L3 spatial domain beam-level prediction
for intra-cell transmission [R4-2606058], and Sub-Case 2 covering FR1 L3
frequency domain cell-level prediction for inter-cell non-collocated scenarios
[R4-2606059]. CATT filed parallel contributions for both sub-cases
[R4-2605842, R4-2605843]. Apple contributed on AI-RRM same-FR non-collocated
cell prediction with channel models, cross-frequency bounds, and per-site
specialization [R4-2605329]. Samsung discussed FR1 L3 frequency domain
cell-level prediction for non-collocated inter-cell scenarios [R4-2606158].
HiSilicon contributed on the framework for measurement data collection for
parameterized out-of-band correlation models [R4-2605736], a foundational
piece for the AI-RRM training data framework.

# NTN: Bands and Features for Rel-20

A new work item, NTN_bands_Features_R20, consolidated the NTN band and feature
definitions for Rel-20. The work item covered S-band, L-band, Ku-band, and
Ka-band NTN bands, with specific device classes for each band: handheld UE for
S-band and L-band, VSAT for Ku-band, and fixed terminal for Ka-band.

NR_BS_RF_req_evo_Ph2-Core appeared as a new work item for Rel-20 base station
RF requirement evolution. NR_Bands_BW_PC_BC_RedCap_R20 defined the Rel-20
band, bandwidth, power class, and band combination support for RedCap devices.
NR_UAV_req-Core continued the unmanned aerial vehicle requirements work started
in Rel-19. HiSilicon discussed UAV RRM core requirements for Rel-20
[R4-2606132], Sanechips contributed on UE RF requirements for Rel-20 UAV
[R4-2606089], and Nokia discussed the impact of high-power UAV-UEs with
directional antennas in RRM requirements [R4-2607088]. NR_NTN_enh-Core
continued with correction CRs from HiSilicon for TS 38.101-5 on NS_201N and
NS_202N applicabilities [R4-2606405, R4-2606406] and Ofinno contributions
on satellite switching and RACH-less handover corrections
[R4-2607156, R4-2607157, R4-2607154].

## SCMC: The Gothia-Cell Debate Intensifies

The Single Cell Multi-Carrier debate reached RAN4#119 with sharply opposing
positions. vivo filed a comprehensive analysis of SCMC ("Gothia-cell") impacts
on 6G RF requirements, arguing that while SCMC does not require a fundamentally
new UE RF architecture, it imposes specific constraints: cross-carrier
measurement validity rules must be defined, the anchor carrier must provide
coverage at least as good as the data carriers, and UE RF requirements for
simultaneous reception across carriers without independent synchronization
need study [analysis 420037].

Nokia took the hardest opposition line, strongly recommending against RAN4
engaging in the Gothia-cell concept at all. Nokia's analysis argued that
SCMC's scope from RAN1 was unclear and that existing carrier aggregation and
dual connectivity specifications already cover the technical scenario. If CC1
carries control and CC2 carries data, that is functionally equivalent to a
primary cell and secondary cell in CA, and no new specification work is needed.
By denying SCMC a dedicated work item, Nokia ensured the concept would be
implemented through existing CA specifications rather than requiring new RF
requirement definitions.

Ericsson filed a comprehensive analysis of UE RF spectrum aggregation for 6G
[analysis 420719] that included SCMC as one of several spectrum aggregation
scenarios to be addressed within the existing framework. CATT proposed spectrum
aggregation as a foundational 6G feature [analysis 414792], with SCMC as one
implementation option. The debate split three ways: give SCMC its own work item,
fold it into the broader spectrum aggregation study, or keep it as a RAN1
scheduling concept with no new RAN4 work. The resolution would wait for RAN#112.

### Spectrum Aggregation: The Bigger Frame

SCMC was one implementation option within a broader debate at RAN4#119: how
should 6G handle spectrum aggregation in general? Apple opened the discussion
on 6G spectrum aggregation topics [R4-2605279], framing the question as a
choice between three models: keep 5G's carrier aggregation framework with
enhancements, adopt SCMC as a new aggregation paradigm, or define a
generalized spectrum aggregation framework that subsumes both.

Apple also contributed on 6G multi-RAT spectrum sharing [R4-2605330], the
coordination between 5G and 6G in shared spectrum - a separate but related
challenge. KDDI contributed views on 6G UE RF for spectrum aggregation
[R4-2605357], Spreadtrum filed its views [R4-2605363], Qualcomm contributed
its position [R4-2605550], MediaTek added views on 6G spectrum aggregation
[R4-2605581], and Nokia filed a comprehensive general and UE RF spectrum
aggregation contribution [R4-2605529].

The spectrum aggregation debate was important because it determined how 6G
would use the fragmented spectrum that operators own: a mix of low-band
(sub-1 GHz), mid-band (1-7 GHz), and the new 7-24 GHz allocations. The choice
between CA and SCMC affected every aspect of the UE RF design: how many RF
chains the UE needs, whether it can share RF components across carriers, how
RRM measurements work across frequencies, and how power is managed when
transmitting on multiple carriers simultaneously. This was the RAN4 equivalent
of the RAN1 waveform decision: a foundational architecture choice that would
cascade into thousands of requirement clauses.

# The Last of Rel-19

The remaining Rel-19 contributions at Dalian were final corrections and
editorial CRs. SBFD, once the flagship Rel-19 feature that had generated
passionate debate across the industry, was present only in -Perf form at modest
volume. MIMO Phase 5 had essentially closed. LP-WUS was absent. The Rel-19 work
items that had consumed RAN4's attention from Orlando to Bangalore were either
complete or represented by final maintenance contributions.

The transition that had begun with a single Rel-20 TDoc at RAN4#113,
introducing Band 111, was complete. The Band 111 CR was a reminder of how far
the work had come: from routine band additions to a comprehensive 6G
specification framework, from scoping papers to normative work items, from zero
FS_6G_Radio TDocs to 622.

# Company Dynamics: The Pre-Plenary Positioning

HiSilicon retook the top position at Dalian, reversing Ericsson's Gothenburg
lead. The UE chipset vendor led the Rel-20 carrier aggregation work, which
concentrated in the UE RF specifications that are HiSilicon's domain. Ericsson
and Nokia held the next two positions, maintaining the infrastructure vendor
presence at the top.

Apple, now serving as RAN4 Chair, was solidly established in the upper tier. The
RRM Phase 6 work item that Apple had championed was an active normative work
item, and Apple's contributions spanned RRM, AI/ML mobility, and ambient IoT
conformance testing.

CATT, Sanechips, OPPO, vivo, Qualcomm, Samsung, and MediaTek rounded out the
top contributor list. The distribution was less flat than RAN1#125 but showed
the same pattern of broad participation as the plenary deadline approached.

# The Series in Retrospect

RAN4#119 was the last meeting in this series. Eleven meetings, seventeen months,
from November 2024 to May 2026. The series began with RAN4#113 in Orlando, a
meeting where Rel-20 existed as a single Band 111 CR and Rel-19 consumed all of
RAN4's energy. It ended with RAN4#119 in Dalian, where Rel-20 edged past
Rel-19 for the first time, FS_6G_Radio was the dominant work item, and the 6G
specification framework was taking shape.

Between those endpoints, the series traced the structural RAN1-RAN4 lag that
defines how 3GPP works. RAN1 designed the features. RAN4 specified the
conformance tests. The six-month gap between RAN1#122's 6G explosion and
RAN4#117's Rel-20 breakout was not a delay: it was the time required for
physical layer concepts to mature into RF requirements.

The next chapter begins at RAN#112 in Singapore. The Stage-2 freeze will lock
the Rel-20 scope. The work will shift from feature invention to specification
completion. And the cycle will begin again with Rel-21, where the 6G
specifications that RAN1 started designing at RAN1#122 in Bangalore will become
normative requirements that RAN4 will specify in meetings that have not yet been
scheduled. The work continues.
