Athens was not the start of the Rel-20 work. It was the meeting where RAN4 began
to talk about starting. Four Rel-20 TDocs appeared, not one of them a technical
contribution. They were positioning papers: Apple proposing RRM Phase 6
[R4-2500242], Intel offering views on 5G-Advanced topics [R4-2501904], MediaTek
laying out its RAN4 Rel-20 priorities [R4-2501625], and BT proposing a way
forward on 200 MHz channel bandwidth [R4-2500214]. Companies were staking claims
on what RAN4 should work on before anyone had decided what RAN4 would work on.

The rest of the meeting was still overwhelmingly Rel-19, but the character had
shifted from Orlando. Where the flagship Rel-19 features at RAN4#113 had only
discussion papers establishing frameworks (the mature work items like HPUE and
demodulation corrections already had CRs), Athens saw the first draft CRs appear
for the headline Rel-19 features. LP-WUS got its first UE RF requirements CR. SBFD moved from "should we add RRM
measurements?" to specific SRS-RSRP simulation results. Five new work items
debuted, none of which existed in November. The transition was not in volume
but in direction: the group was beginning to point toward Rel-20.

# The Rel-20 Scoping Papers

The four Rel-20 contributions in Athens were not about solving technical
problems. They were about defining the problems. This is the pre-standardization
phase where companies compete to frame the work: if your framing is accepted, the
resulting work item aligns with your product roadmap. The four papers revealed
different visions of what RAN4's Rel-20 should be.

## Apple's RRM Phase 6 Pitch

Apple's motivation paper for Rel-20 RRM Phase 6 [R4-2500242] was the most
consequential of the four. RRM (Radio Resource Management) is the set of
measurement and reporting procedures that govern mobility, and RRM Phase 5 was
already delivering fast SCell activation and FR2-1 measurement optimizations for
Rel-19. Apple's pitch for Phase 6 framed Rel-20 RRM around three axes: AI/ML-assisted
mobility decisions, multi-TRP measurement frameworks for distributed MIMO, and
measurement gap enhancements for the 7-24 GHz range where beam sweeping overhead
becomes the dominant performance bottleneck.

The subtext was competitive positioning. Apple drives RRM specifications because
RRM directly affects the user experience of its devices: how fast a phone finds
a new cell, how reliably it stays connected at cell edges, how seamlessly it
transitions between carriers. By framing Rel-20 RRM early, Apple ensured that
its device priorities would be baked into the work item scope before
infrastructure vendors could define it around gNB-centric KPIs. MediaTek's views
paper [R4-2501625] aligned broadly with Apple's framing while adding emphasis on
power consumption metrics for RRM procedures, reflecting MediaTek's position as
a chipset vendor for mid-range devices where battery life competes with
performance. Intel's paper [R4-2501904] supported the multi-TRP measurement
direction, unsurprising given Intel's investment in vRAN and distributed MIMO
architectures.

## 200 MHz Bandwidth: The 7-24 GHz Enabler

BT's way forward paper on 200 MHz channel bandwidth [R4-2500214] addressed a
specific technical gap. NR Release 15 through 19 supports maximum channel
bandwidths of 100 MHz for FR1 (below 7 GHz) and 400 MHz for FR2 (above 24 GHz).
The 7-24 GHz range sits between these two regimes: carrier bandwidths of 200 MHz
are a natural fit for the wider spectrum blocks available in this range, but no
NR specification currently supports it. BT argued that 200 MHz bandwidth is a
prerequisite for 6G in the 7-24 GHz range and should be a Rel-20 RAN4 work item
covering BS and UE RF requirements, ACLR and EVM targets for 200 MHz carriers,
and receiver reference sensitivity at wider bandwidths.

The proposal was strategic. BT is not a major RAN4 contributor, but by filing
the scoping paper it positioned itself as a thought leader on spectrum strategy
for 6G. The 200 MHz bandwidth question would later become central to the
FS_6G_Radio study item, and BT's early framing influenced the bandwidth options
that were evaluated.

# LP-WUS: The First Big CR

The low-power wake-up signal work item crossed a threshold in Athens. Orlando
had produced only system parameter draft CRs from CATT [R4-2417862, R4-2417863],
adding LP-WUS entries to the specification tables without defining behavioral
requirements. In Athens, vivo submitted the first UE RF requirements CR.

## vivo Submits Draft Specification Text

vivo filed TR 38.774 v0.2.0 [R4-2500753], the technical report consolidating
LP-WUS physical layer parameters, system parameters, and evaluation assumptions.
Alongside the TR update, vivo submitted a "Draft Big CR for LP-WUS UE RF
requirements in Rel-19" [R4-2500754], the first attempt at UE RF requirement
text for LP-WUS. Unlike the system parameter CRs from Orlando, which added rows
to existing specification tables, a Big CR touches multiple clauses across
multiple TS documents with new behavioral requirements. Submitting one signals
that the feature is mature enough for specification text, not just parameter
studies.

The Big CR covered UE reference sensitivity, adjacent channel selectivity,
in-band blocking, and spurious emissions for the wake-up receiver. vivo's choice
to lead the CR drafting was notable: vivo is a smartphone vendor with a strong
interest in power-efficient devices, and LP-WUS is one of the few Rel-19
features that directly improves device battery life rather than network capacity.
By drafting the CR, vivo positioned itself as the specification lead for the UE
side of LP-WUS.

HiSilicon continued its comprehensive approach from Orlando with five updated
TDocs covering general aspects, UE REFSENS and ACS, other Rx requirements,
testability, and BS RF [R4-2500157 through R4-2500161]. Nokia updated its BS RF
proposal [R4-2500075], and Sanechips filed a full set of LP-WUS contributions
covering BS RF, system parameters, REFSENS/ACS, receiver characteristics,
testability, and TR updates on power boosting and regulatory spectrum
requirements [R4-2500550 through R4-2500556]. Samsung entered the LP-WUS
discussion for the first time with UE RF requirements and testability
[R4-2500503, R4-2500504].

## Apple Proposes Simulation Collection Framework

Apple's LP-WUS contributions in Athens were the most structurally significant.
Beyond the ongoing Rx requirements work [R4-2500307] and RRM core requirements
[R4-2500238], Apple submitted simulation assumptions and results for LP-WUR
[R4-2500276] and, critically, a "Proposal for Simulation Results Collection for
LP-SS Performance" [R4-2500277]. The proposal established a formal process for
vendors to submit standardized simulation results for wake-up signal performance,
including defined channel models, deployment scenarios, and reporting formats.

The simulation collection framework matters because it transforms the debate
from qualitative ("our approach works better") to quantitative ("here is the
SINR at 95th percentile coverage for each proposal"). Apple's experience
running similar simulation campaigns for NR RRM in earlier releases gave it the
credibility to establish the methodology. Once a simulation framework is agreed,
every vendor must submit results to the same format, making direct comparison
possible. This shifts power from the companies with the largest delegation
(who can argue longer in the meeting room) to the companies with the best
simulation results (who have the data).

The mandate fight continued in the background. Apple's Rx requirements proposal
[R4-2500307] maintained the tiered approach from Orlando: relaxed sensitivity
for low-cost devices, premium sensitivity for flagship tiers. The simulation
collection framework would determine whether the relaxed tier met operator
coverage requirements, and therefore whether the optional-versus-mandatory
fight would be resolved by data rather than debate.

# SBFD: From Framework to Numbers

SBFD work in Athens shifted decisively from "what should we specify?" to "what
should the numbers be?" Two external inputs drove this transition: Nokia's
SRS-RSRP simulation results and RAN1's liaison statements on CLI scenarios.

## Nokia's SRS-RSRP Simulations

Nokia filed simulation results for L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP measurement
[R4-2500468], the first quantitative performance data for UE-to-UE cross-link
interference measurement in SBFD. L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP is a Layer 1 measurement where
the UE measures the SRS transmitted by neighboring UEs to estimate the CLI power
level at its own receiver. The measurement accuracy determines how well the
gNB can schedule UEs to avoid mutual interference: if the measurement is too
coarse, the scheduler makes wrong decisions and CLI degrades throughput; if the
measurement is too precise, the measurement overhead consumes resources that
could be used for data.

Nokia's results showed that L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP measurement accuracy within plus or
minus 3 dB is achievable with SRS transmission periodicities of 10 ms or less,
but degrades rapidly at longer periodicities due to channel aging. The practical
implication: SBFD CLI management requires relatively frequent SRS transmissions
from all UEs in the cell, adding uplink overhead that partially offsets the
duplexing gain. Nokia's framing of this trade-off favored conservative SRS
configurations, which aligns with the European operator preference for
deployability over theoretical peak gain.

Nokia also updated its RRM requirements for CLI handling [R4-2500466] and SBFD
operation [R4-2500467], now with specific measurement periods and accuracy
targets rather than the framework descriptions from Orlando. The shift was
visible across the TDoc titles: where Orlando had "Discussion on potentially new
requirements," Athens had "RRM requirements for" with concrete parameter
proposals.

## RAN1 Weighs In on CLI Scenarios

RAN1 sent two liaison statements to RAN4 that structured the CLI debate. The
first LS [R4-2500007] clarified the scenarios for UE-to-UE CLI measurement
reporting in SBFD operation, defining three distinct interference cases:
co-channel CLI where two UEs are scheduled on adjacent sub-bands within the same
carrier, adjacent-channel CLI where UEs are on different carriers, and
mixed-numerology CLI where UEs use different subcarrier spacings. Each scenario
requires different measurement configurations and reporting triggers.

The second LS [R4-2500006] addressed the impact of CLI on transmit signal
quality and MPR (maximum power reduction). When a UE detects high CLI, it may
need to increase its transmit power to maintain its uplink link budget, but
increasing power worsens the CLI for neighboring UEs. RAN1's position was that
CLI-aware power control should be specified in RAN1, and RAN4 should define the
corresponding RF requirements (transmit power accuracy, EVM under CLI-aware
power control, and MPR allowances).

These LS responses were significant because they showed RAN1 and RAN4 converging
on a division of labor for SBFD: RAN1 defines the CLI measurement and power
control procedures, RAN4 defines the RF performance and RRM requirements. When
two working groups agree on scope boundaries, the specification work accelerates.
The Athens SBFD contributions reflected this: LG Electronics updated RRM impacts
for CLI handling [R4-2500169] and SBFD operation [R4-2500171]; Apple
contributed UE RRM requirements for CLI handling [R4-2500245]; vivo updated
sub-band configurations and guard-bands [R4-2500737]; CMCC filed four
contributions covering general aspects, new RF requirements, existing Tx, and
existing Rx [R4-2500865 through R4-2500868]; Sanechips added CLI and RRM
impacts [R4-2500911, R4-2500912]; and CATT updated its full set of SBFD
requirements [R4-2501025 through R4-2501028, R4-2501042]. The volume remained
similar to Orlando but the titles had shifted from "Discussion on" to concrete
parameter proposals.

# Ku-Band NTN: A New Spectrum Frontier

The appearance of a Ku-band NTN work item (NR_NTN_Ku_bands-Core) in Athens was a
surprise if you thought NTN was contained to L-band and S-band. Ku-band spans
12 to 18 GHz, well above the current NR NTN frequency range which tops out at
Ka-band (20-30 GHz) for the forward link. Ku-band is interesting for NTN because
it sits in a sweet spot: lower atmospheric attenuation than Ka-band, wider
available bandwidth than S-band, and existing satellite infrastructure from
broadcast and fixed-satellite services that can be repurposed.

## System Parameters and Coexistence

Sanechips led the Ku-band charge with five initial TDocs covering coexistence
studies based on ITU regulations [R4-2500607], system parameters [R4-2500608],
UE RF requirements [R4-2500609], SAN (satellite access node) RF requirements
[R4-2500610], and a general discussion paper [R4-2501175]. The coexistence study
was the critical contribution: Ku-band is heavily used by fixed-satellite
service (FSS) and broadcast-satellite service (BSS), and adding a mobile
service like NR NTN requires demonstrating that mobile terminals will not
interfere with existing services. Sanechips' ITU-based analysis proposed
protection criteria based on the ITU-R P.452 propagation model and FSS
interference thresholds from the Radio Regulations.

Ericsson contributed a full set of five Ku-band TDocs: WI updates impact
[R4-2501354], system parameters [R4-2501355], UE RF requirements
[R4-2501356], SAN requirements [R4-2501357], a draft CR to TS 38.101-5 for
VSAT support [R4-2501358], and coexistence simulation assumptions
[R4-2501398]. Ericsson's draft CR was the first concrete specification text for
Ku-band NTN, adding VSAT (very small aperture terminal) type UE definitions to
the NR NTN specification. A VSAT terminal is a fixed or portable satellite
terminal with a directional antenna, typically 0.6 to 1.2 meters in diameter,
used for broadband satellite internet. Adding VSAT to TS 38.101-5 signaled that
Ku-band NTN was targeting fixed-wireless and backhaul use cases, not handheld
devices.

Xiaomi contributed system parameters [R4-2500642] and VSAT type definitions
for Ku-band [R4-2500643], plus coexistence assumptions and parameters
[R4-2500637]. CATT filed further discussions on system parameters, SAN RF
requirements, and coexistence evaluations [R4-2500988 through R4-2500990].
MediaTek contributed RRM requirements [R4-2500387], LG Electronics added RRM
requirements [R4-2500883], and HiSilicon filed initial RRM requirements
[R4-2501271].

The Ku-band discussion revealed different visions of what NTN beyond L/S-band
should be. Ericsson and Sanechips pushed for VSAT fixed-terminal use cases with
directional antennas, which simplify RF requirements (antenna gain compensates
for path loss, no need for handheld power constraints). Xiaomi's VSAT type
definition [R4-2500643] was more explicit about portable terminals, suggesting
interest in consumer broadband via satellite. The tension between fixed-VSAT
and portable-terminal use cases would shape the Ku-band specification scope.

## VSAT and SAN Requirements

The SAN (satellite access node, the 3GPP term for the satellite payload or
gateway) requirements for Ku-band introduced new challenges. A SAN operating at
Ku-band faces higher free-space path loss than at S-band (roughly 16 dB
additional loss at 14 GHz compared to 2 GHz for the same satellite altitude),
requiring higher transmit power or larger antenna apertures. The SAN RF
requirements proposed by Sanechips [R4-2500610] and Ericsson [R4-2501357]
assumed a GEO satellite architecture (35,786 km altitude) rather than LEO,
which is the opposite of the LEO-focused design that had dominated NTN through
Phase 3. A GEO satellite at Ku-band can serve a continent-scale footprint with
a single beam, but the 250 ms one-way delay makes it unsuitable for real-time
applications.

The choice of GEO as the baseline for Ku-band NTN was pragmatic: existing Ku-band
satellites are predominantly GEO, and reusing them for NR NTN is faster than
launching a new LEO constellation. But it also revealed a split in the NTN
community between the LEO evangelists (SpaceX/AST SpaceMobile aligned, focused
on low-latency direct-to-handset) and the GEO pragmatists (traditional satellite
operators, focused on broadband and backhaul). RAN4's role is specification, not
constellation strategy, but the assumptions embedded in the RF requirements
effectively choose sides.

# IoT NTN Testing Goes Narrowband

The largest work item at RAN4#114 by contribution volume was IoT NTN testing
(NR_IoT_NTN_req_test_enh-Core). This was a shift from Orlando, where general
NTN Phase 3 was the active thread. The IoT NTN testing work item focused on two
specific technical problems: supporting channel bandwidths narrower than 5 MHz
for IoT over NTN, and defining high-power UE requirements for NTN in smartphones.

## The 3 MHz Channel Push

The 3 MHz channel bandwidth for NTN emerged as the central technical thread.
Narrowband IoT devices (NB-IoT and eMTC) use channel bandwidths of 180 kHz to
1.4 MHz. NR NTN was designed around 5 MHz and wider channel bandwidths. The gap
between IoT channel widths and NR NTN channel widths meant that IoT over NTN
required significant specification extensions.

Apple led the 3 MHz push with an extraordinary eight TDocs: less than 5 MHz
NTN RRM [R4-2500241], further considerations on HPUE TX requirements
[R4-2500293], restricted power back-off simulations for satellite band UE TX
[R4-2500294], system aspects of NTN 3 MHz channel [R4-2500297], UE RF
requirements for 3 MHz channel [R4-2500298], power back-off values for the
3 MHz channel in band n254 [R4-2500299] and band n256 [R4-2500300], and
addition of the 3 MHz channel to TS 38.101-5 [R4-2500301] and TR 38.863
[R4-2500302].

The 3 MHz channel was a compromise between IoT requirements (ideally sub-MHz)
and NR NTN waveform constraints (which become inefficient below 3 MHz due to
control channel overhead). Apple's detailed proposals showed that 3 MHz was
technically viable for NTN while narrow enough to serve IoT use cases like
asset tracking and environmental monitoring. Huawei's French subsidiary
contributed remaining system parameters [R4-2500412], and HiSilicon filed
remaining RF issues and a draft CR for 3 MHz on NTN bands [R4-2500413,
R4-2500416].

Sanechips filed draft CRs to TS 38.108 for EVM window length requirements at
3 MHz [R4-2500549] and to TS 38.101-5 and TS 38.181 for less than 5 MHz
channel bandwidth introduction [R4-2500605, R4-2500606]. Xiaomi contributed UE
RF requirements and a draft Big CR for less than 5 MHz NTN [R4-2500625,
R4-2500626], plus RRM requirements [R4-2500633]. China Telecom opened with a
Big CR to TS 38.108 for less than 5 MHz NTN [R4-2500073].

The volume and specificity of these contributions indicated that the 3 MHz
question was being resolved, not debated. Multiple companies filing draft CRs
for the same specification change means the technical direction is agreed and the
work is about text precision, not concept selection. This was the opposite of
SBFD and AI/ML, where draft CRs were still absent.

## NTN HPUE for Smartphones

Skyworks Solutions contributed a candidate transmit power amplifier for NR NTN
high-power UE in smartphones [R4-2500090], a component-level contribution that
revealed the supply chain preparing for satellite-capable phones. The
contribution proposed a specific PA architecture and output power levels for
NTN bands, showing that the RF component industry was already developing parts
for satellite direct-to-handset.

Apple's HPUE contributions [R4-2500293, R4-2500294] addressed the
power back-off requirements for NTN UEs. When a UE transmits at high power
(HPUE, power class 2 at 26 dBm) over a satellite link, the transmitter must
reduce power at certain modulation schemes and resource block allocations to
meet ACLR and EVM requirements. Apple's simulation results proposed specific
power back-off values for NTN bands, calibrated to ensure the UE can maintain
the link budget required for LEO satellite connections while meeting regulatory
spectrum emission limits.

# AI/ML Mobility: A Less Contentious Frontier

The AI/ML for mobility study item (FS_NR_AIML_Mob) debuted in Athens with
a notably different tone from the AI/ML air interface work that had split
the industry in Rel-19. Where the air interface AI/ML debate pitted
standardized architectures against proprietary models, the mobility AI/ML
discussion was practical and uncontroversial: can AI/ML improve handover
decisions, cell selection, and measurement reporting without requiring
standardized model architectures?

## Apple's Trio of Opening Contributions

Apple opened the mobility AI/ML discussion with three TDocs that framed the
entire work item. The first addressed testability and interoperability
[R4-2500231], the now-standard RAN4 question for any AI/ML feature. The
second covered general aspects [R4-2500232], defining the scope: AI/ML for
mobility covers cell prediction (which cell the UE will hand over to before
the measurement confirms it), beam prediction in mobility scenarios (which
beam will be best after handover), and measurement configuration optimization
(which measurements to trigger based on learned mobility patterns). The third
studied impacts on RAN4 requirements [R4-2500233], asking which specific TS
clauses would need modification.

The testability question was simpler for mobility AI/ML than for air interface
AI/ML. A mobility prediction model can be tested against recorded drive-test
data: the model predicts a handover target, the test equipment verifies whether
the prediction matches the actual best cell based on post-handover measurements.
This is fundamentally an offline evaluation problem with defined ground truth,
unlike CSI compression where the "correct" compressed representation is
undefined. Apple's framing emphasized this simplicity to argue that mobility
AI/ML should not be held to the same standardization requirements as air
interface AI/ML.

## Broad Buy-In Across Companies

The striking feature of the mobility AI/ML discussion was the absence of
opposition. Every contributing company filed supporting papers. CMCC
contributed general aspects, requirement impacts, and testability
[R4-2501099 through R4-2501101]. NTT DOCOMO discussed requirement impacts
[R4-2500594]. Sanechips addressed requirements and testability
[R4-2500696, R4-2500697]. MediaTek contributed RRM impact and testability
[R4-2500890, R4-2500891]. Xiaomi filed requirement impacts and testability
[R4-2500619, R4-2500628]. CATT rounded out the discussion with RRM impacts
and testability [R4-2501040, R4-2501041].

No one argued against studying AI/ML mobility. No one demanded standardized
model architectures. The group appeared to agree that mobility prediction is a
vendor-differentiation feature that can be implemented in the UE without
network-side standardization beyond measurement reporting formats. This
consensus was a preview of how AI/ML would likely be handled in Rel-20:
proprietary UE models for mobility, standardized interfaces, no architecture
mandates. The air interface AI/ML fight would continue, but mobility AI/ML
had found a path around it.

# Air-to-Ground Gets Serious

Air-to-ground communications (NR_ATG_enh-Core) provide cellular connectivity to
aircraft, enabling in-flight broadband without satellite links. Rel-17
established the basic ATG framework with dedicated ATG bands and high-Doppler
operation. The Rel-19 enhancement work item that arrived in Athens aimed to
add MIMO, carrier aggregation, and higher-order modulation.

## CMCC Pushes MIMO and CA for ATG

CMCC was the primary driver of ATG enhancements at this meeting. China has one
of the world's largest ATG deployments, providing in-flight connectivity on
domestic routes, and CMCC's contributions reflected operational experience. The
BS RF requirements for ATG with DL MIMO [R4-2500858] proposed 2x2 and 4x4 MIMO
configurations for ATG base stations, addressing the challenge of serving
aircraft at altitudes of 10 km with MIMO where the channel angular spread is
narrow and beamforming gain is essential. The UE RF requirements for ATG with
UL-MIMO [R4-2500859] addressed the aircraft terminal side: an aircraft
terminal can support larger antenna arrays than a handheld UE, enabling 2x2
UL-MIMO, but the terminal must also handle the 1200 km/h Doppler shift at
cruising speed.

CMCC's inter-band CA for ATG [R4-2500860] proposed aggregating the dedicated
ATG band (4.5 GHz in China) with a terrestrial NR band to increase capacity.
The challenge was intermodulation between the ATG and terrestrial bands, which
have different Doppler profiles and propagation characteristics. CMCC also
filed a draft CR for TS 38.104 on ATG BS RF clarification [R4-2500857],
addressing ambiguities in the Rel-17 ATG specification that operators had
encountered in deployment.

HiSilicon filed BS RF requirements for ATG enhancements [R4-2500783] with a
draft CR for TS 38.104 [R4-2500784], aligning with CMCC's direction. Apple
contributed a discussion on ATG UE RF requirement applicability [R4-2500443],
questioning whether ATG UEs (which are aircraft terminals, not handsets) should
be subject to the same RF requirements as handheld UEs or a separate device
category. CATT filed BS RF requirements with draft CRs to TS 38.104 and TS
38.141-1 [R4-2500995 through R4-2500997]. Sanechips addressed RRM aspects and
CA for ATG [R4-2500906, R4-2500937, R4-2500938]. LG Electronics contributed
RRM requirements for ATG CA operation [R4-2500837].

The ATG discussion was a niche but practical work item. It revealed China's
operational lead in ATG deployment translating into specification influence, a
pattern that also appeared in SBFD and NTN where Chinese operators' deployment
experience shaped the technical requirements.

# Spatial Channel Modeling: The Hidden Foundation

The spatial channel model for demodulation study (FS_NR_demod_SCM) was the
least visible but most foundational new work item in Athens. Every NR
demodulation performance requirement in TS 38.101 is defined against a specific
channel model: the UE must achieve a given throughput at a given SNR under a
defined channel profile (EPA, EVA, ETU, TDL, CDL). These channel models define
the multipath environment that the UE receiver must handle. If the models are
wrong, the conformance tests pass but real-world performance suffers.

The spatial channel model study aimed to update these models for the deployment
scenarios that have emerged since the original NR channel models were defined in
Rel-15: massive MIMO with 64 or more antenna elements, distributed MIMO with
multiple TRPs, and high-mobility scenarios with Doppler shifts up to 2 kHz.
Existing models assume spatial stationarity (the channel statistics are constant
across the antenna array), which breaks down for arrays spanning multiple meters
in distributed MIMO deployments.

Apple led the study with three TDocs: spatial channel modeling discussion
[R4-2500262], simulation results [R4-2500263], and a draft technical
report contribution for the CDL (clustered delay line) approach [R4-2500264].
MediaTek contributed discussion [R4-2500335], results [R4-2500336], and
a TDL (tapped delay line) TR contribution [R4-2500337]. Nokia contributed
discussion and simulation results with CDL implementation [R4-2500427,
R4-2500428]. Samsung, Sanechips, and CATT each filed simulation results and
TR contributions [R4-2501428 through R4-2501430, R4-2501503 through
R4-2501504, R4-2500993]. China Telecom opened with TR scope and reference
[R4-2500331].

The CDL versus TDL debate within the study was technical rather than
contentious. CDL models represent the channel as clusters of rays with
specific angles of departure and arrival, capturing the spatial structure of
the channel. TDL models abstract the spatial structure into a tapped delay line
where each tap represents a cluster with a defined delay, power, and Doppler
spectrum but without angular information. CDL is more accurate for MIMO
performance evaluation but computationally heavier; TDL is simpler but loses
spatial information that matters for massive MIMO. The study was evaluating
whether the increased accuracy of CDL justified the additional test complexity.

This work was invisible to anyone not reading the demodulation performance
specifications, but it underpinned every UE conformance test for Rel-20 and
beyond. When a UE's receiver performance is tested, the channel model determines
whether the test reflects real-world conditions or an obsolete abstraction. The
Athens contributions were the first step in a multi-release effort to bring NR
channel models into the massive MIMO and distributed MIMO era.

# Low-Band CA Switching: Study to Specification

Low-band carrier aggregation via switching (NR_LBCA_Sw-Core) had been a Rel-19
study item. In Athens, it began its transition toward normative specification.
The concept: instead of simultaneously transmitting on multiple low-band
carriers (which requires antenna separation that exceeds the UE form factor),
the UE rapidly switches its transmit chain between carriers. The result is
effective carrier aggregation for coverage extension without the RF complexity
of simultaneous transmission.

AT&T filed the work plan [R4-2500089], establishing the timeline and
deliverables for the Rel-19 normative phase. Apple created the skeleton TR
38.768 [R4-2500215, R4-2500322], the technical report that would consolidate
the study conclusions and provide the basis for the normative specification.
Apple also contributed initial views on UE RF requirements
[R4-2500313], proposing switching times, RF retuning requirements, and
intermodulation analysis for the switching-based approach.

HiSilicon contributed a discussion on LB-LB switching [R4-2500139], focusing
on the switching transient: when the UE switches from one low-band carrier to
another, the RF front-end must retune the power amplifier, filters, and
antenna matching network, creating a transient period during which transmission
is not possible. HiSilicon proposed a maximum switching transient of 35
microseconds, short enough to fit within the NR slot structure without losing
an entire OFDM symbol. Nokia contributed considerations [R4-2500405],
addressing the network-side impacts: the gNB must know when the UE is switching
so it does not schedule the UE during the transient. The signaling overhead and
scheduler complexity were Nokia's primary concerns.

Xiaomi and vivo also contributed, with Xiaomi addressing RF and RRM
requirements [R4-2500644, R4-2500645] and vivo contributing UE RF impact and
RRM requirements [R4-2500768, R4-2500828]. The LBCA work was procedural
rather than contentious: the concept was agreed in the Rel-19 study, and Athens
was about parameter proposals for the normative phase.

# Company Dynamics: HiSilicon Takes the Lead

The company rankings shifted in Athens. HiSilicon took the top position,
narrowly ahead of Ericsson. Nokia held steady at third. The most significant
change was Apple's rise into the fourth position, driven by the Rel-20 scoping
papers and the comprehensive IoT NTN testing contributions. Qualcomm rose
noticeably, doubling its output from Orlando, primarily through contributions to
EN-DC RF Phase 4 and carrier aggregation.

The Chinese ecosystem remained dominant in aggregate: HiSilicon, Sanechips,
CATT, vivo, CMCC, OPPO, and Xiaomi together accounted for roughly half of all
contributions. Sanechips maintained its position as the leading UE chipset
contributor, with comprehensive filings across Ku-band NTN, LP-WUS, AI/ML
mobility, ATG, and spatial channel modeling.

The session chair contributions (BDaT Session, RRM Session, Main Session)
grew significantly in Athens, suggesting heavier meeting management overhead
as the number of parallel work items increased. More work items means more
agenda items to manage, more session splits to coordinate, and more meeting
reports to produce.

A notable new entrant: Huawei Technologies France appeared as a distinct
contributor from HiSilicon, filing system parameter work for IoT NTN
[R4-2500412]. The Huawei/HiSilicon split, where HiSilicon handles chipset-level
RF specifications and Huawei handles system-level parameters, has been a
longstanding pattern in RAN4. The French entity's appearance indicated
Huawei's European R&D centers taking a larger role in 3GPP contributions,
possibly reflecting EU research funding requirements.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#115

RAN4#114 was the meeting where Rel-20 stopped being an abstract concept and
became a set of specific proposals. The four scoping papers from Apple, Intel,
MediaTek, and BT established the initial boundaries of what RAN4 would work on
in Rel-20: RRM Phase 6, 200 MHz bandwidth, multi-TRP measurements, and
AI/ML-assisted mobility. The five new work items (Ku-band NTN, AI/ML mobility,
ATG enhancements, spatial channel modeling, and IoT NTN testing) showed where
the growth was happening.

But Rel-20 still represented less than one percent of the contributions. The
group remained a Rel-19 delivery machine. The question for the next meeting,
RAN4#115 in Wuhan, was whether Rel-20 would move from proposals to action. Would
the FS_6G_Radio study item, the centerpiece of the 6G physical layer work,
appear on RAN4's agenda? Would companies start filing Rel-20 CRs rather than
Rel-20 opinion papers? Wuhan would answer whether Athens was a one-off
positioning exercise or the start of a real transition.
