Gothenburg was the meeting where 6G swallowed RAN1. FS_6G_Radio accounted for
more than half of all contributions. Rel-20 contributions outnumbered Rel-19 by
five to one. The Rel-19 work items that had been RAN1's primary activity six
months earlier in Bangalore had either closed or reduced to maintenance levels.
RAN1 was now a 6G design factory, and the Gothenburg meeting was its peak
production run.

The meeting was held in Ericsson's home city, and the company took the
opportunity to lead the contribution count. But the distribution remained
characteristically flat for RAN1, with six companies within a narrow margin at
the top. The 6G work had entered a phase where companies were submitting
detailed technical specifications: waveform parameter sets, MIMO codebook
designs, channel model calibration results. The brainstorming phase was over.
The engineering phase had begun.

# FS_6G_Radio: Half the Meeting

FS_6G_Radio at 931 contributions was not just the largest work item at this
meeting. It was larger than the entire RAN1#123 meeting in Arlington. The
volume reflected both the breadth of the 6G study (waveforms, numerologies,
MIMO, channel models, AI/ML, sensing, energy efficiency, coexistence) and its
depth (each topic had progressed from candidate proposals to detailed
simulation results and parameter trade-off analysis).

## Waveform Down-Select Approaches Decision

The 6G waveform debate reached its decisive phase at Gothenburg. After three
meetings of proposals, simulations, and moderator summaries, the candidate set
had narrowed to a primary waveform and an optional enhancement. OFDM with
enhanced spectral shaping was selected as the baseline 6G waveform, maintaining
backward compatibility with NR signal processing chains while adding spectral
containment improvements that reduce guard band overhead. OTFS was designated as
an optional waveform for high-Doppler scenarios, available when configured by
the network but not required for baseline UE implementation.

The decision represented a victory for the evolutionary approach advocated by
Ericsson, Qualcomm, and Apple over the clean-slate approach advocated by Nokia
and some Asian contributors. The argument that won was practical: a new waveform
requires new silicon, new test equipment, new deployment tools, and new operator
training. An enhanced OFDM leverages the existing NR ecosystem while delivering
most of the spectral efficiency gain that a new waveform would provide.

The enhanced OFDM design included: adaptive cyclic prefix length scaling with
subcarrier spacing to maintain spectral efficiency at wider bandwidths, improved
filtered-OFDM spectral containment using time-domain windowing rather than
sub-band filtering, and a unified frame structure that supports mixed
numerologies within the same carrier for flexible resource allocation.

## Channel Models for Sub-THz

The 7-24 GHz channel model work from earlier meetings was extended to sub-THz
frequencies above 100 GHz, where propagation characteristics change
fundamentally. At these frequencies, atmospheric absorption (particularly from
oxygen and water vapor), molecular scattering, and building penetration loss
become dominant factors rather than secondary corrections.

The sub-THz channel models defined at Gothenburg included: indoor hotspot
scenarios where line-of-sight propagation dominates and data rates above 100
Gbps are achievable at ranges up to 10 meters, outdoor urban micro scenarios
where diffraction and reflection from building surfaces provide coverage but
atmospheric absorption limits range to approximately 100 meters, and fixed
wireless access scenarios where high-gain directional antennas at both ends
extend range to approximately 500 meters.

FUTUREWEI contributed on downlink transmission schemes for the 6G shared
channel [R1-2600054]. CATT discussed interference measurements and UE
reporting for PHY-layer information beyond CSI and HARQ [R1-2600311],
a topic unique to 6G where sensing information shares the feedback channel
with communication CSI. Apple contributed on downlink control channel design
for 6G [R1-2600838], Xiaomi discussed downlink control channel and
scheduling [R1-2600432], TCL contributed parallel work on control channel
design [R1-2600575], and Sharp discussed downlink control channel for
scheduling [R1-2600925]. IITH proposed low-PAPR waveforms and pre-DFT
multiplexing for uplink [R1-2601370]. ETSI ISG MAT provided a liaison
statement on candidate multiple access techniques for 6G [R1-2600579],
coordinating the European research community's input into 3GPP.

## Massive MIMO for 6G

The 6G MIMO work defined antenna configurations that exceed NR's maximum of 64
antenna elements per panel. 6G massive MIMO targets 128 to 256 elements per
panel for the 7-24 GHz range, with hybrid beamforming architectures that
combine analog beamforming (phase shifters) with digital precoding (baseband
processing) to manage the power consumption and cost of fully digital
beamforming at these array sizes.

For sub-THz frequencies above 100 GHz, the MIMO approach shifts to lens-based
beamforming where a dielectric lens focuses the signal onto an antenna array,
reducing the number of active RF chains while maintaining high antenna gain.
This is fundamentally different from the phased-array approach used in NR mmWave
and reflects the different physics at sub-THz frequencies where antenna element
spacing is sub-millimeter and conventional phased arrays become impractical.

# Ambient IoT Outdoor: Active Device Air Interface Detailed

Ambient IoT outdoor active devices became the second-largest work item, growing
significantly from Arlington. The air interface design for Device 2b (active
self-powered) reached the detailed specification phase: modulation scheme
(BPSK for the backscattered signal, OFDM for the carrier wave), coding rate
(1/3 convolutional code for the forward error correction), frame structure
(time-division between energy harvesting and communication phases), and
coexistence mechanisms with NR UEs.

Device 2c (passive backscatter) continued in the study phase. The range
limitations of purely passive devices were being evaluated for specific use
cases rather than the broader outdoor IoT vision.

CATT contributed on R2D signals, channels, waveform and procedure for ambient
IoT enhancement [R1-2600327] and the complementary D2R direction [R1-2600328].
Apple filed a draft CR for inclusion of outdoor active device study outcomes
[R1-2601730] and Huawei coordinated the RAN1 outcome inclusion [R1-2601734,
R1-2601735].

# AI/ML Phase 2: Case 0 Specification Nears Completion

The AI/ML air interface Phase 2 work reached near-completion for Case 0 (CSI
spatial/frequency compression without temporal prediction). The inference
parameters, tensor formats, and performance requirements had been agreed. The
remaining open issue was the training data framework: what data is used to train
the UE and gNB models, and how is interoperability verified when models are
trained independently by different vendors?

The inter-vendor training collaboration question that NTT DOCOMO and FUTUREWEI
had raised at Bangalore remained the unresolved frontier. The working assumption
at Gothenburg was that the specification would define the inference interface
(tensor formats, compression ratios) and leave the training framework to an
industry collaboration outside 3GPP. This was the proprietary-model position
(Ericsson, Qualcomm, Apple) winning by default, since the alternative
(standardized model architectures) had not gained sufficient support to become
a normative requirement.

# MIMO Phase 6: Distributed MIMO Codebooks

MIMO Phase 6 progressed from the SRS capacity and DL CSI acquisition scoping of
Bangalore and Arlington to specific codebook designs for distributed MIMO. The
key challenge was CSI feedback for more than two TRPs: the NR codebook framework
was designed for single-TRP and two-TRP scenarios. Phase 6 needed codebooks that
could represent the channel from three or more TRPs to the UE with manageable
feedback overhead.

The solution adopted at Gothenburg used a two-stage codebook: the first stage
selects the strongest TRPs (typically two or three out of the available set),
and the second stage quantizes the channel from the selected TRPs using an
enhanced version of the NR Type II codebook with additional spatial basis
vectors. The two-stage approach limits the feedback overhead to a level
comparable to two-TRP CSI while capturing most of the multi-TRP gain.

# NR Coverage Phase 3: Filling the Gaps

NR_cov_Ph3-Core was a new Rel-20 work item that appeared for the first time at
Gothenburg. Coverage Phase 3 addressed coverage limitations that had been
identified in NR deployments: uplink coverage at cell edge for the 7-24 GHz
range where higher path loss reduces range, indoor coverage from outdoor cells
where building penetration loss at mid-band frequencies (3.5-6 GHz) limits
indoor service, and coverage for new device classes (RedCap, ambient IoT, NTN
handheld) that have lower transmit power or simpler receivers than full-spec NR
UEs.

Coverage Phase 3 was a practical work item driven by operator deployment
experience rather than technology push. The contributions came predominantly
from operators (NTT DOCOMO, CMCC, China Telecom) and infrastructure vendors,
with device vendors contributing more selectively.

# Sensing Bis: Signal Design for Multi-Static Operation

The sensing bis study made significant progress on signal design for multi-static
sensing. The RAN1#122 and RAN1#123 discussions had established the service
types. Gothenburg defined the specific NR reference signals that would be
reused for sensing: enhanced CSI-RS with wider bandwidth and shorter periodicity
for high-resolution ranging, enhanced SRS with positioning-specific sequences
for multi-static angle-of-arrival estimation, and a new dedicated sensing
reference signal that combines features of both.

The architecture question, monostatic versus distributed, remained open but
the signal designs were written to support both. A monostatic implementation
uses the gNB's own CSI-RS and SRS; a distributed implementation adds the
dedicated sensing reference signal for inter-site coordination.

China Telecom advanced the ISAC technical report TR 38.765 to version 0.3.0
[R1-2601711]. Qualcomm contributed evaluation assumptions and performance
evaluation for UAV gNB-monostatic sensing [R1-2601264]. CATT discussed
evaluation methodology and results for Rel-20 ISAC [R1-2600331]. CEWiT and
Panasonic each contributed evaluation assumptions and performance evaluation
[R1-2601390, R1-2600538].

# GNSS-Resilient NTN: LEO-Based Positioning Emerges

The GNSS-resilient NTN study converged on LEO satellite-based positioning as the
primary alternative to GNSS. The approach uses the same LEO satellites that
provide the communication link as positioning references: the UE measures the
Doppler shift and time of arrival from multiple visible satellites and computes
its position without requiring GNSS signals. The accuracy target was 50 meters
horizontal, sufficient for the UE to pre-compensate Doppler for the
communication link.

Eutelsat Group contributed location uncertainty reduction and mitigation
techniques [R1-2601041], addressing the practical challenge of a UE that knows
its position with degraded accuracy during GNSS denial. THALES, as rapporteur,
advanced TR 38.742 through multiple versions [R1-2601321, R1-2601708,
R1-2601709] and compiled RAN1 agreements on GNSS resilience [R1-2601547].

# Company Dynamics: Ericsson Leads at Home

Ericsson led the contribution count at its home meeting, followed closely by
OPPO, Samsung, CATT, Nokia, HiSilicon, and Apple in a tight pack. The flat
distribution reflected the meeting's character: detailed technical
specifications that required contributions from every company with a stake in
the 6G physical layer design.

OPPO's rise to second place was notable. OPPO had been steadily increasing its
RAN1 presence through the series, and Gothenburg represented its peak. The
company's 6G research investment was paying off in specification influence.

Samsung maintained its strong RAN1 position, consistent with its historical
strength in PHY design. HiSilicon and Sanechips continued their comprehensive
contributions across all work items. Apple's presence grew with the AI/ML
Phase 2 work nearing completion and the RRM Phase 6 work item now active in
RAN4.

InterDigital, Ofinno, Spreadtrum, and NEC maintained their mid-tier presence,
contributing to specific technical areas where they had expertise rather than
across the full work item portfolio.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#118

RAN1#124 was the peak of 6G physical layer design activity. The waveform
decision, the sub-THz channel models, the massive MIMO configurations, the
distributed MIMO codebooks, the sensing signal designs, and the AI/ML Case 0
completion represented the core technical decisions that would define the 6G
air interface.

The next meeting in the series is RAN4#118, back in Gothenburg two months later.
RAN4 would receive the output of this RAN1 meeting as a cascade of liaison
statements, work item descriptions, and specification requirements. The
FS_6G_Radio work in RAN4 would grow. The 6G RF and RRM requirements would begin
their own specification journey, trailing RAN1's design work by the structural
lag that had characterized the entire series.
