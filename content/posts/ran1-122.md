RAN1#122 was the meeting where 6G stopped being a scoping paper and became a
work program. The FS_6G_Radio study item exploded to become the single largest
work item at any meeting in this entire series: larger than any Rel-19 work item
at any RAN4 meeting, larger than any RAN1 work item before or since. A
relatively flat company distribution replaced the usual RAN4 hierarchy of
Ericsson and HiSilicon at the top with Samsung, Nokia, Ericsson, Sanechips,
Qualcomm, and HiSilicon all within a single-digit margin. Rel-20 accounted for
nearly half the meeting's contributions. Everything about this meeting was
different from what RAN4 had been doing.

While RAN4 was still writing Rel-19 specifications in Wuhan three months earlier,
RAN1 had already pivoted. The lag between RAN1 design and RAN4 specification,
which post 01 identified as a structural feature of 3GPP, was playing out in
real time. RAN1#122 was where the physical layer concepts that RAN4 would later
have to specify conformance tests for were being invented.

# FS_6G_Radio: 6G Breaks Into the Open

FS_6G_Radio was RAN1's center of gravity at this meeting. With the volume of
an entire RAN4 meeting compressed into a single study item, it covered three
main technical areas: modulation and waveform design, numerology and frame
structure, and AI/ML integration into the 6G air interface.

## Modulation and Waveform: Beyond OFDM

The modulation debate for 6G was the most active single thread within FS_6G_Radio.
Companies proposed alternatives to OFDM that could handle the extreme
requirements of 6G: peak data rates above 100 Gbps, sub-millisecond latency, and
operation at frequencies up to 100 GHz where phase noise and power amplifier
nonlinearity degrade OFDM performance.

Rakuten Mobile contributed a discussion on modulation and joint channel coding
and modulation for the 6G air interface [R1-2506143], proposing that 6G should
evaluate a unified coding and modulation framework rather than treating them as
separate layers. The argument was that classical OFDM with separate channel
coding (LDPC) leaves performance on the table: joint optimization of the
modulation constellation and the error-correcting code can approach Shannon
capacity more closely than the concatenated approach used in NR. BUPT
contributed considerations on channel modeling and evaluation assumptions for
6G air interface [R1-2505767], emphasizing that any new waveform must be
evaluated against channel models that capture the spatial and temporal
characteristics of sub-THz propagation, which differ fundamentally from the
sub-6 GHz and mmWave models used for NR.

Spreadtrum discussed modulation and joint channel coding for 6G [R1-2505175],
OPPO contributed a parallel discussion [R1-2505760], and InterDigital weighed
in on modulation and coding [R1-2505839]. Qualcomm moderated the discussion with
two feature lead summaries [R1-2506445, R1-2506579], a role that positioned
Qualcomm as the neutral arbiter of the modulation debate. Lekha Wireless
Solutions, a smaller Indian company, contributed on modulation and coding
[R1-2505784], reflecting RAN1's broader participation base compared to RAN4.

The waveform candidates under discussion included OTFS (orthogonal time frequency
space) for high-Doppler scenarios where OFDM subcarrier orthogonality breaks
down, UFMC (universal filtered multi-carrier) for mixed-numerology deployments
where different subcarrier spacings coexist on adjacent frequency resources, and
variants of filtered OFDM that add spectral shaping to reduce out-of-band
emissions at the cost of increased transmitter complexity.

No single waveform had consensus support. The modulation debate was at an early
stage: companies were proposing candidates, defining evaluation criteria, and
agreeing on simulation assumptions. The decision on a 6G waveform would not be
made until later meetings, but the candidate set was taking shape.

## Numerology and Frame Structure

Lenovo contributed a discussion on numerology and frame structure for the 6G
air interface [R1-2505526], proposing that 6G should support a wider range of
subcarrier spacings than NR, including 480 kHz and 960 kHz for sub-THz
operation where phase noise makes narrower subcarrier spacings impractical. The
NR numerology set (15, 30, 60, 120, 240 kHz) was designed for frequencies up
to 52.6 GHz. At 100 GHz and above, the phase noise corner frequency increases,
and subcarrier spacings of 480 kHz or higher are needed to maintain reasonable
common phase error after phase tracking reference signal compensation.

The frame structure discussion addressed the tension between low latency and
spectral efficiency. NR's scalable numerology allows short slot durations (down
to 0.0625 ms at 240 kHz subcarrier spacing), but the control channel overhead
grows as slot duration shrinks. 6G's sub-millisecond user plane latency target
requires either shorter slots with higher overhead or a fundamentally different
frame structure that decouples control and data transmission.

## AI/ML for 6G: The Architecture War Reaches the Next Generation

Nokia contributed views on AI/ML operation and use cases for the 6G radio air
interface [R1-2505132], advocating for a clean-slate design where AI/ML is
integrated into the 6G physical layer from the start, rather than retrofitted
as in NR. Nokia's position was that 6G should define AI-native physical layer
components (channel estimation, beam management, resource allocation) where
the baseline specification assumes AI/ML implementation rather than classical
algorithms.

This was an escalation of the Rel-19 AI/ML architecture war. In Rel-19, the
fight was whether AI/ML should be optional (Ericsson, Qualcomm, Apple) or
mandatory with standardized architectures (Huawei, Nokia). In 6G, Nokia was
proposing to invert the paradigm: AI/ML should be the default, and classical
algorithms should be the fallback. The implication was that 6G UEs and gNBs
would require on-device AI inference engines as baseline capability, a
requirement that would add silicon cost and power consumption to every device.

The AI/ML for 6G discussion was at an even earlier stage than the modulation
debate. Nokia's contribution was a position paper, not a technical proposal.
But the framing was significant: it showed that the AI/ML architecture question
was not just a Rel-19 issue but would define the 6G physical layer design
philosophy.

NEC contributed a discussion on 6G energy efficiency and AI/ML evaluation
requirements [R1-2505639], raising the question of how to evaluate AI/ML
performance for 6G: should the KPI be throughput at a given complexity budget,
energy per bit at a given throughput, or a compound metric? The energy
efficiency question was particularly acute because AI/ML inference adds compute
energy that classical algorithms do not require, and 6G's sustainability
targets demand net energy reduction despite higher data rates.

# AI/ML Air Interface Phase 2: CSI Compression Goes Normative

While FS_6G_Radio was inventing the future, NR_AIML_air_Ph2-Core was specifying
the present. Phase 2 moved AI/ML for NR air interface from feasibility study to
normative specification, and the work converged on a single use case as the
first standardized AI/ML feature: CSI spatial/frequency compression, known as
Case 0.

## Case 0: The First Standardized AI/ML Use Case

CSI compression with a two-sided model, where the UE encodes channel state
information using a neural network and the gNB decodes it using a matching
neural network, was designated Case 0: the first AI/ML use case to be specified
normatively. The designation came with specific scope constraints: Case 0
covers CSI spatial/frequency compression without temporal aspects, meaning the
AI/ML model operates on a single CSI measurement instance rather than
predicting future CSI from past measurements. This limited scope was a political
compromise: temporal prediction was more controversial because it requires the
UE and gNB models to maintain state across CSI reporting instances, raising
interoperability concerns.

FUTUREWEI filed two TDocs on Case 0: inference-related aspects
[R1-2505148] and other aspects [R1-2505149]. MediaTek contributed parallel
discussions on inference-related aspects [R1-2506029] and other aspects
[R1-2506030], creating a structured debate where each company addressed the
same sub-topics from different positions. Xiaomi discussed inference-related
aspects of two-sided AI/ML model-based CSI feedback [R1-2505452], Apple
contributed on inference-related aspects for AI-based CSI compression
[R1-2505902], and CATT addressed specification support for inference-related
aspects [R1-2505299].

The inference-related aspects were the heart of the specification work: how does
the UE perform inference to compress CSI, and how does the gNB perform inference
to decompress it? The key specification decisions were the input/output tensor
formats (what data structures are passed between UE and gNB), the compression
ratio (how many bits the compressed CSI occupies compared to the uncompressed),
and the performance requirement (what reconstruction accuracy the gNB decoder
must achieve). The fight was over whether the specification should mandate
specific tensor formats and compression ratios, which would constrain model
architectures, or leave them as vendor implementation choices.

## Inter-Vendor Training: The Unresolved Frontier

NTT DOCOMO raised the inter-vendor training collaboration question
[R1-2506296], and FUTUREWEI contributed a parallel discussion [R1-2505150].
The problem: if a UE from Vendor A uses a model trained on Vendor A's data and a
gNB from Vendor B uses a model trained on Vendor B's data, the CSI compression
and decompression may be incompatible even if both vendors implement the same
standardized tensor formats. The encoding learned by Vendor A's model may
produce compressed representations that Vendor B's model cannot accurately
decode, because each model was trained to optimize its own loss function without
knowledge of the other.

The inter-vendor training problem was the practical manifestation of the
architecture war. Without standardized model architectures (Huawei, Nokia
position), inter-vendor interoperability requires joint training of UE and gNB
models, which means sharing training data or model gradients between competing
vendors. With standardized model architectures (Ericsson, Qualcomm, Apple
position), interoperability is guaranteed by design because every vendor
implements the same encoder and decoder structure.

The RAN1#122 discussion did not resolve this question. But the volume of
contributions on Case 0 and the specificity of the technical proposals indicated
that standardization was proceeding despite the open question. The working
assumption appeared to be that Case 0 would be specified with standardized
interfaces (tensor formats, compression ratios) and the inter-vendor training
problem would be addressed through an industry collaboration framework outside
the 3GPP specification.

# MIMO Phase 6: Distributed MIMO Takes Shape

MIMO Phase 6 (NR_MIMO_Ph6-Core) was the Rel-20 successor to the Rel-19 MIMO
Phase 5 work item that had consumed a significant share of RAN1 and RAN4
attention. While Phase 5 focused on coherent joint transmission from two TRPs,
Phase 6 expanded the scope to distributed MIMO with more than two TRPs, massive
MIMO arrays exceeding 128 elements, and AI/ML-assisted beam management at scale.

OPPO contributed a discussion on enhancement of SRS capacity and coverage for
MIMO Phase 6 [R1-2505749], addressing a fundamental scaling problem. In a
multi-TRP distributed MIMO system, each UE must transmit SRS to each TRP so
that each TRP can estimate the uplink channel. The number of SRS resources
required scales with the number of TRPs times the number of UE antennas. For a
4-TRP system with 4-antenna UEs, 16 SRS resources are needed per UE, consuming
a significant fraction of the uplink capacity. OPPO proposed SRS resource
pooling and spatial multiplexing techniques to reduce the overhead.

CATT moderated the SRS capacity and coverage discussion with four summary
documents [R1-2506461, R1-2506532, R1-2506571, R1-2506585], indicating an
active debate with multiple rounds of proposals and counter-proposals. KDDI
contributed views on SRS capacity and coverage [R1-2506381], and ETRI discussed
enhancing DL CSI acquisition for Phase 6 [R1-2506060]. MediaTek moderated the
DL CSI acquisition discussion [R1-2506450].

The MIMO Phase 6 work at this meeting was at the feature-scoping stage: defining
what the work item should cover and which technical problems should be
prioritized. The specification work would follow in later meetings.

# Ambient IoT Outdoor: Active Devices Enter the Frame

The ambient IoT study item split into indoor and outdoor tracks in Rel-20. The
outdoor active device study (FS_Ambient_IoT_Outdoor_Active) made its first
appearance at this meeting. Unlike the passive (tag-based) ambient IoT that
Rel-19 studied, outdoor active devices have an energy storage element (capacitor
or small battery) and an active transmitter, enabling longer range at the cost
of periodic recharging.

Xiaomi contributed a discussion on evaluation methodology for outdoor ambient
IoT [R1-2505457], proposing deployment scenarios, channel models, and
performance metrics for active ambient IoT devices operating at ranges up to
100 meters. The evaluation methodology was critical because ambient IoT is a
radically different device class from NR UEs: peak data rates are measured in
kbps, not Gbps, and the primary KPI is energy per bit rather than spectral
efficiency.

LG Electronics moderated the air interface study for Device 2b/C with five
summary documents [R1-2506506 through R1-2506509, R1-2506592]. Device 2b is
a self-powered active device with energy storage; Device 2c is a passive
backscatter device. The five summaries indicated a structured debate with
multiple rounds, similar to the MIMO Phase 6 SRS discussion.

Huawei moderated a draft liaison statement on BS transmit power assumptions for
coverage evaluation in ambient IoT [R1-2506574], coordinating with RAN4 on the
deployment assumptions that would be used for coverage and link budget analysis.
This LS was one of the first RAN1-to-RAN4 coordination points for Rel-20 ambient
IoT.

# GNSS-Resilient NTN: What If the Satellites Go Dark?

The GNSS-resilient NTN study item (FS_NR_NTN_GNSS_resilient) addressed a
scenario that satellite operators preferred not to discuss publicly but that
defense and emergency-service users demanded: what happens when GNSS is
unavailable? GNSS jamming and spoofing are well-documented threats. In military
contexts, GNSS denial is a standard electronic warfare tactic. In civilian
contexts, GNSS outages from solar weather, equipment failure, or accidental
interference occur regularly.

For NR NTN, GNSS is critical because the UE uses GNSS for timing and frequency
pre-compensation. A LEO satellite at 600 km moves at 7.5 km/s, creating a
Doppler shift that the UE must pre-compensate based on its own position and
velocity, which it determines from GNSS. Without GNSS, the UE cannot pre-
compensate, and the satellite cannot decode the UE's transmission.

THALES led the study with considerations on NR NTN resilience to GNSS
unavailability and degradation [R1-2505359], compiling company proposals on
alternative positioning methods [R1-2506449]. Nokia contributed initial views
on GNSS-resilient operation [R1-2506048], proposing network-based positioning
assistance where the gNB (or satellite gateway) provides the UE with its
approximate position based on the beam identity and timing advance. Eutelsat
Group contributed considerations from a satellite operator perspective
[R1-2505859], emphasizing that GNSS resilience must not increase UE complexity
to the point where direct-to-handset NTN becomes economically infeasible.
FUTUREWEI discussed Rel-20 GNSS-resilient operation [R1-2505153].

The GNSS-resilient study was at an early phase, with companies proposing
alternative positioning technologies: network-based assistance, inertial
sensors, LEO satellite-based positioning (using the same satellites the UE is
communicating with as positioning references), and terrestrial beacon
augmentation. No single approach had consensus, and the study was expected to
continue through Rel-20.

# 7-24 GHz Channel Modeling: Building the 6G Propagation Map

The 7-24 GHz channel modeling study (FS_NR_7_24GHz_CHmod) was a maintenance
item from Rel-19 that carried into Rel-20. The study aimed to define channel
models for the frequency range that 6G would occupy, filling the gap between
the FR1 (sub-7 GHz) and FR2 (above 24 GHz) channel models that were defined in
NR.

Nokia contributed updated calibration simulation results for channel modeling
enhancements for 7-24 GHz [R1-2506382], providing measurement-calibrated
channel parameters for indoor hotspot, urban micro, and urban macro scenarios
at 7, 15, and 24 GHz. HiSilicon contributed calibration results and maintenance
issues [R1-2505225], and Samsung discussed remaining issues on channel modeling
enhancements [R1-2505549].

InterDigital moderated the maintenance discussions with three summaries
[R1-2506403, R1-2506404, R1-2506405], indicating that the study was nearing
completion with only calibration and parameter refinement remaining. The 7-24
GHz channel models were foundational for all subsequent 6G work: waveform
evaluation, link budget analysis, MIMO performance assessment, and eventually
RAN4 conformance testing all depend on accurate channel models.

# Sensing: From Feasibility to Specification

The sensing study item (FS_Sensing_NR_bis) continued the Rel-19 feasibility
study into Rel-20, moving toward specification. The Rel-19 study had established
that NR signals (CSI-RS, SRS) could be reused for sensing with sub-meter range
resolution. The Rel-20 bis study was defining the specific sensing service types,
signal designs, and RAN-CN interface procedures.

Sensing was not a top-five work item at this meeting, but it was a top-five
topic by company interest. The sensing discussion was less visible in the TDoc
counts because much of the work was in the FS_6G_Radio study item, where
sensing was treated as a 6G use case rather than a separate work item. The
integration of sensing into 6G was one of the design principles that
distinguished 6G from 5G: in 5G, sensing is an add-on feature; in 6G, sensing
is expected to be a native capability of the air interface.

# Company Dynamics: The Flat Distribution

RAN1#122 had the flattest company distribution of any meeting in this series.
Samsung, Nokia, Ericsson, Sanechips, Qualcomm, and HiSilicon all fell within a
single-digit margin at the top. No company dominated. This flat distribution was
characteristic of RAN1: the physical layer design phase involves many companies
contributing competing proposals, and no single vendor can push a design through
without coalition-building. By contrast, RAN4's specification-writing phase
concentrates contributions among the infrastructure vendors and UE chipset
companies that will implement the requirements.

Samsung's top position was notable because Samsung is typically a mid-tier
contributor in RAN4. In RAN1, Samsung is a major PHY design contributor with
strong positions on MIMO, beam management, and AI/ML. The RAN1#122 result
reflected Samsung's investment in 6G research and its strategy of influencing
the 6G physical layer design early.

OPPO, vivo, CATT, and Xiaomi all had significant presence, continuing the
pattern of strong Chinese participation. NTT DOCOMO was the most active
operator, reflecting Japan's strategic interest in 6G standardization. Google,
Sharp, InterDigital, Ofinno, and Lenovo all had non-trivial contributions,
reflecting RAN1's broader company base compared to RAN4.

The session had multiple moderators: Qualcomm for 6G modulation, CATT for MIMO
Phase 6 SRS, MediaTek for MIMO DL CSI, LG Electronics for ambient IoT, THALES
for GNSS-resilient NTN, and InterDigital for 7-24 GHz channel modeling. The
distribution of moderator roles across companies was both a coordination
mechanism and a power-sharing arrangement: companies that moderate a topic
control the agenda and the summary documents, which influence how decisions are
recorded.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#116

RAN1#122 was the meeting that RAN4#113-115 had been waiting for. The FS_6G_Radio
study item was now active. The AI/ML air interface Phase 2 was going normative.
MIMO Phase 6 was scoped. Ambient IoT outdoor was under study. GNSS-resilient NTN
was evaluating alternative positioning technologies. The 7-24 GHz channel models
were being calibrated.

The next meeting in the series is RAN4#116, back in Bangalore one month after
this RAN1 meeting. RAN4#116 would be the last big Rel-19 RAN4 meeting, with the
Rel-20 work still a trickle. The contrast between RAN1#122 and RAN4#116, held in
the same city a month apart, would crystallize the RAN1-RAN4 lag: RAN1 was
already deep in 6G while RAN4 was still writing Rel-19 specifications. The
question for RAN4#116 was whether any of the RAN1 work from this meeting would
appear as liaison statements or scope requests at RAN4.
