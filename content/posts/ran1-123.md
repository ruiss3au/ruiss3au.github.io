# RAN1#123 - October 2025, Arlington, VA

RAN1#123 was the consolidation meeting. Three months after the RAN1#122
explosion in Bangalore, where FS_6G_Radio debuted at a volume that dwarfed
every other work item, the Arlington meeting saw the 6G study item settle into a
more focused rhythm. The raw volume dropped by two-thirds, but the content
became sharper: companies were no longer proposing candidate technologies, they
were narrowing the field.

The meeting also saw the first signs of Rel-19 wind-down in RAN1. SBFD had moved
from -Core to maintenance, with contributions focused on corrections:
NTT DOCOMO filed maintenance on SBFD [R1-2509258](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509258.zip), Samsung contributed
corrections [R1-2509706](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509706.zip), Nokia filed two correction sets [R1-2509656](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509656.zip), [R1-2509662](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509662.zip), and Huawei contributed a Rel-19 correction [R1-2509696](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509696.zip).
LP-WUS and MIMO Phase 5 had effectively closed in RAN1.

# FS_6G_Radio: From Brainstorming to Down-Select

The FS_6G_Radio study item at RAN1#123 was qualitatively different from
Bangalore. The modulation and waveform debate that had generated dozens of
proposals had progressed to a structured evaluation phase, with companies
submitting simulation results against agreed channel models rather than position
papers. The moderator summaries from Bangalore had been consolidated into a set
of evaluation assumptions, and companies were now running the simulations.

The 6G modulation discussion converged on a narrower set of candidates. OPPO's
waveform and multiple access discussion [R1-2508727](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508727.zip) and modulation and joint
channel coding paper [ R1-2508730](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508730.zip) included simulation parameters for the
waveform candidates under evaluation. OTFS emerged as the leading candidate for
high-Doppler scenarios above 500 km/h [R1-2508471](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508471.zip), [R1-2509133](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509133.zip), but its spectral
efficiency penalty relative to OFDM at low Doppler (below 30 km/h) meant it
would likely be an optional waveform rather than a replacement for OFDM
[R1-2509110](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509110.zip), [R1-2508631](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508631.zip). UFMC showed promise
for mixed-numerology scenarios but CATT's discussion on DL DFT-s-OFDM
[R1-2509303](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509303.zip) raised questions about implementation complexity at sub-THz
frequencies.

The numerology discussion made more concrete progress. The candidate subcarrier
spacings were narrowed to 120, 240, 480, and 960 kHz, with 480 kHz expected to
be the baseline for the 7-24 GHz range and 960 kHz for sub-THz operation above
100 GHz. The 15, 30, and 60 kHz spacings used in NR would be retained for
backward compatibility. NTT DOCOMO maintained the TR 38.760-1 skeleton for 6G
Radio RAN1 aspects, advancing it to version 0.0.3 [R1-2509569](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509569.zip).

LG Electronics contributed views on overall design and techniques for the 6G
air interface [R1-2508908](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508908.zip), advocating a balanced approach that integrates
AI/ML where classical algorithms hit diminishing returns but preserves
deterministic fallbacks. Sanechips contributed on evaluation assumptions
[R1-2508524](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508524.zip), Xiaomi contributed parallel evaluation assumptions [R1-2508683](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508683.zip),
and LG Electronics filed additional evaluation methodology [R1-2508909](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508909.zip).

# AI/ML Air Interface: The Architecture War

The architecture question crystallized around two competing visions at this
meeting. Nokia's AI-native approach (first presented at RAN1#122 [R1-2505132](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_122/Docs/R1-2505132.zip))
argued that AI/ML should be the default processing paradigm, not an optional
enhancement: the 6G baseline should assume AI-based channel estimation with
sparse DMRS, AI-based beam prediction across cells, and AI-based CSI compression
as the primary feedback mechanism. Classical algorithms would be the fallback,
not the baseline. This required every 6G device to include an AI inference
engine, adding silicon area and power consumption to the baseband chipset.

The cautious camp, led by Ericsson, Qualcomm, and Apple, argued that AI/ML
should remain a UE capability negotiated with the network, not a mandate. The
6G baseline physical layer should work without AI using classical OFDM channel
estimation, classical beam management, and classical CSI codebooks. AI/ML
enhancements would be available for premium device tiers but not required for
all devices. The argument was both practical (adding mandatory AI inference to
low-cost IoT and RedCap devices would defeat their cost and power targets) and
strategic (premature standardization of AI models would lock the industry into
suboptimal designs before deployment data existed).

OPPO's modular design proposal [R1-2505757](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_122/Docs/R1-2505757.zip) offered a middle path: a mandatory
baseline functionality set serving both 6G MBB and 6G IoT, with AI/ML as a
layer on top without changing the baseline waveform or channel estimation
framework. This preserved vendor differentiation while allowing the
specification to proceed without resolving the architecture question.

The Case 0 CSI compression work proceeded within its limited scope
(spatial/frequency compression without temporal prediction). But the
architecture question was deferred to RAN#112, the plenary that would make
binding scope decisions for Rel-20. The deferral created an awkward situation:
the normative specification work for Case 0 was progressing, but the
fundamental architecture decision had not been made. The specification was being
written in a conditional voice.

# Ambient IoT Outdoor: Evaluation Framework Established

The outdoor ambient IoT study item became the second-largest work item at this
meeting. Xiaomi led the evaluation methodology discussion with contributions on
methodology [R1-2508675](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508675.zip), [R1-2509453](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509453.zip), R2D (reader-to-device) signals and
waveforms [R1-2508676](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508676.zip), and D2R (device-to-reader) signals and waveforms
[R1-2508677](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508677.zip). Huawei coordinated the inclusion of outdoor active device study
outcomes [R1-2509718](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509718.zip) into the broader ambient IoT specification framework.

# GNSS-Resilient NTN: Positioning Alternatives Narrow

Three primary candidates emerged: network-based assistance, LEO satellite-based
positioning, and inertial sensor fusion. THALES, as rapporteur, maintained the
TR 38.742 skeleton for the study [R1-2508479](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508479.zip), [R1-2509611](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509611.zip) and filed
considerations on GNSS resilience [R1-2508465](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508465.zip). Nokia contributed a discussion
on GNSS-resilient operation [R1-2509007](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509007.zip). ESA contributed from a space
infrastructure perspective [R1-2508626](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508626.zip), emphasizing that GNSS resilience
solutions must work with existing satellite constellations.

# Sensing Bis: Service Types Defined

Service types agreed at this meeting included object detection, velocity
measurement, and environmental imaging. China Telecom drove the ISAC
(Integrated Sensing and Communication) technical report with draft TR 38.765
versions [R1-2509237](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509237.zip), [R1-2509522](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509522.zip), [R1-2509632](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509632.zip). LG Electronics contributed
evaluation assumptions and performance evaluation for NR ISAC [R1-2509305](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2509305.zip).
ETSI ISG ISAC provided a liaison statement on their draft group report
[R1-2508858](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_123/Docs/R1-2508858.zip), coordinating between 3GPP and the European ISAC standardization
effort.

# MIMO: Phase 5 Closes, Phase 6 Continues

MIMO Phase 5 was winding down in RAN1, with the normative work moving to RAN4
for conformance specification. Phase 6 continued with SRS capacity and coverage
work from Bangalore and new contributions on multi-TRP CSI acquisition. The
multi-TRP CSI question was the most technically challenging: in a distributed
MIMO system with three or more TRPs, the UE must report CSI for each TRP, but
the CSI feedback overhead scales with the number of TRPs.

# Company Dynamics: Nokia Edges Ahead

Nokia took the top position by a small margin, followed closely by Ericsson.
The two infrastructure vendors had effectively traded places with the Samsung/
Sanechips/Qualcomm/HiSilicon pack from Bangalore. The moderator roles continued
to be distributed across companies: Huawei for ambient IoT, THALES for
GNSS-resilient NTN.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#117

RAN1#123 was the last RAN1 meeting before RAN4 would feel the full force of
Rel-20. The next meeting in the series is RAN4#117 in Dallas, where Rel-20 would
finally break out in RAN4. The FS_6G_Radio study item would appear on RAN4's
agenda for the first time. The 6G channel models, waveform candidates, and
numerology decisions from RAN1 would cascade into RAN4 as RF and RRM
requirements that needed conformance specifications.
