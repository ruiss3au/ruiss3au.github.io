RAN1#123 was the consolidation meeting. Three months after the RAN1#122
explosion in Bangalore, where FS_6G_Radio debuted at a volume that dwarfed
every other work item, the Arlington meeting saw the 6G study item settle into a
more focused rhythm. The raw volume dropped by two-thirds, but the content
became sharper: companies were no longer proposing candidate technologies, they
were narrowing the field.

The meeting also saw the first signs of Rel-19 wind-down in RAN1. SBFD had moved
from -Core to maintenance, with contributions focused on corrections:
NTT DOCOMO filed maintenance on SBFD [R1-2509258], Samsung contributed
corrections [R1-2509706], Nokia filed two correction sets [R1-2509656,
R1-2509662], and Huawei contributed a Rel-19 correction [R1-2509696].
LP-WUS and MIMO Phase 5 had effectively closed in RAN1. The Rel-20 share held
steady, but the composition shifted from exploratory studies toward structured
evaluation.

# FS_6G_Radio: From Brainstorming to Down-Select

The FS_6G_Radio study item at RAN1#123 was qualitatively different from
Bangalore. The modulation and waveform debate that had generated dozens of
proposals had progressed to a structured evaluation phase, with companies
submitting simulation results against agreed channel models rather than position
papers. The moderator summaries from Bangalore had been consolidated into a set
of evaluation assumptions, and companies were now running the simulations.

The 6G modulation discussion converged on a narrower set of candidates: the
original field of OFDM variants (OTFS, UFMC, filtered OFDM, DFT-s-OFDM) had
been winnowed through simulation results that showed clear performance
differences. OTFS emerged as the leading candidate for high-Doppler scenarios
above 500 km/h, but its spectral efficiency penalty relative to OFDM at low
Doppler (below 30 km/h) meant it would likely be an optional waveform rather
than a replacement for OFDM. UFMC showed promise for mixed-numerology scenarios
but its implementation complexity at sub-THz frequencies where analog filtering
is difficult raised concerns.

The numerology discussion made more concrete progress. The candidate subcarrier
spacings were narrowed to 120, 240, 480, and 960 kHz, with 480 kHz expected to
be the baseline for the 7-24 GHz range and 960 kHz for sub-THz operation above
100 GHz. The 15, 30, and 60 kHz spacings used in NR would be retained for
backward compatibility in bands shared with NR deployments. NTT DOCOMO
maintained the TR 38.760-1 skeleton for 6G Radio RAN1 aspects, advancing it
to version 0.0.3 [R1-2509569].

The AI/ML for 6G discussion continued with Nokia's AI-native physical layer
proposal facing pushback from companies that wanted a more cautious approach.
LG Electronics contributed views on overall design and techniques for the 6G
air interface [R1-2508908], advocating a balanced approach that integrates
AI/ML where classical algorithms hit diminishing returns but preserves
deterministic fallbacks. Sanechips contributed on evaluation assumptions
[R1-2508524], Xiaomi contributed parallel evaluation assumptions [R1-2508683],
and LG Electronics filed additional evaluation methodology [R1-2508909].
OPPO continued the modulation and joint channel coding discussion from
Bangalore [R1-2508730], proposing specific simulation parameters for the
waveform candidates under evaluation.

The energy efficiency discussion launched by NEC at Bangalore gained traction.
Multiple companies contributed evaluation results showing the energy-per-bit
trade-offs of different waveform and numerology combinations. The emerging
consensus was that 6G should target a 10x improvement in energy efficiency
relative to NR at the same data rate, with the improvement coming from a
combination of wider bandwidths (reducing transmission time), more efficient
waveforms (reducing PA back-off), and AI/ML-optimized resource allocation
(reducing idle transmission).

# Ambient IoT Outdoor: Evaluation Framework Established

The outdoor ambient IoT study item became the second-largest work item at this
meeting, growing as FS_6G_Radio contracted. The study established its
evaluation framework: deployment scenarios (urban macro, rural macro,
industrial outdoor), channel models for outdoor backscatter communication,
device architectures (Device 2b active self-powered, Device 2c passive
backscatter), and performance metrics (range at given data rate, energy per
successful transmission, coexistence impact on NR UEs).

Xiaomi led the ambient IoT evaluation methodology discussion with contributions
on methodology [R1-2508675, R1-2509453], R2D (reader-to-device) signals and
waveforms [R1-2508676], and D2R (device-to-reader) signals and waveforms
[R1-2508677]. Huawei coordinated the inclusion of outdoor active device study
outcomes [R1-2509718] into the broader ambient IoT specification framework.

The air interface for Device 2b/C continued its structured debate under LG
Electronics' moderation. The five summary rounds from Bangalore had been
absorbed, and the discussion focused on specific waveform parameters for the
backscatter modulation: subcarrier spacing for the OFDM-based carrier wave,
modulation order for the backscattered signal (BPSK versus QPSK), and coding
rate for the forward error correction applied to the backscatter data.

## AI/ML Air Interface: The Architecture Question Lingers

The AI/ML air interface work continued with NR_AIML_air_Ph2, but the structural
question that had dominated earlier discussions remained unresolved.

The debate crystallized around two competing visions. Nokia's AI-native
approach (first presented at RAN1#122 [R1-2505132]) argued that AI/ML should
be the default processing paradigm, not an optional enhancement: the 6G
baseline should assume AI-based channel estimation with sparse DMRS, AI-based
beam prediction across cells, and AI-based CSI compression as the primary
feedback mechanism. Classical algorithms would be the fallback, not the
baseline. This required every 6G device to include an AI inference engine,
adding silicon area and power consumption to the baseband chipset.

The cautious camp, led by Ericsson, Qualcomm, and Apple, argued that AI/ML
should remain a UE capability negotiated with the network, not a mandate. The
6G baseline physical layer should work without AI using classical OFDM channel
estimation, classical beam management, and classical CSI codebooks. AI/ML
enhancements would be available for premium device tiers but not required for
all devices. The argument was both practical (adding mandatory AI inference to
low-cost IoT and RedCap devices would defeat their cost and power targets) and
strategic (premature standardization of AI models would lock the industry into
suboptimal designs before deployment data existed).

OPPO's modular design proposal [R1-2505757] offered a middle path: a mandatory
baseline functionality set serving both 6G MBB and 6G IoT, with AI/ML as a
layer on top without changing the baseline waveform or channel estimation
framework. This preserved vendor differentiation while allowing the
specification to proceed without resolving the architecture question.

The Case 0 CSI compression work proceeded within its limited scope
(spatial/frequency compression without temporal prediction), with contributions
on inference parameters, training data formats, and performance requirements.
But the architecture question, whether models should be standardized or
proprietary, was not addressed at this meeting. It was deferred to RAN#112,
the plenary that would make binding scope decisions for Rel-20.

The deferral created an awkward situation: the normative specification work for
Case 0 was progressing, but the fundamental architecture decision that would
determine whether the specification described a standardized model or
standardized interfaces around a vendor-proprietary model had not been made. The
specification was being written in a conditional voice: "if the architecture is
standardized, then the following tensor formats apply; if the architecture is
proprietary, then the following interface parameters apply."

# GNSS-Resilient NTN: Positioning Alternatives Narrow

The GNSS-resilient NTN study made concrete progress, with the field of
alternative positioning technologies narrowing from the broad set proposed at
Bangalore to three primary candidates: network-based assistance (the gNB or
satellite gateway provides the UE's approximate position based on beam identity
and timing advance), LEO satellite-based positioning (using Doppler and
time-of-arrival measurements from the same satellites the UE communicates
with), and inertial sensor fusion (combining GNSS with accelerometer and
gyroscope data to bridge GNSS outages).

THALES, as rapporteur, maintained the TR 38.742 skeleton for the study
[R1-2508479, R1-2509611] and filed considerations on GNSS resilience
[R1-2508465]. Nokia contributed a discussion on GNSS-resilient operation
[R1-2509007], and ESA contributed considerations from a space infrastructure
perspective [R1-2508626], emphasizing that GNSS resilience solutions must
work with existing satellite constellations.

# Sensing Bis: Service Types Defined

The sensing bis study item (FS_Sensing_NR_bis) made progress on defining the
sensing service types that the Rel-20 specification would support. The service
types agreed at this meeting included object detection, velocity measurement,
and environmental imaging.

China Telecom drove the ISAC (Integrated Sensing and Communication) technical
report with draft TR 38.765 versions [R1-2509237, R1-2509522, R1-2509632],
the foundational study document for NR sensing. LG Electronics contributed
evaluation assumptions and performance evaluation for NR ISAC [R1-2509305],
and ETSI ISG ISAC provided a liaison statement on their draft group report
[R1-2508858], coordinating between 3GPP and the European ISAC standardization
effort.

The architecture question, monostatic versus distributed sensing, remained
unresolved. Ericsson and Huawei continued to push for monostatic (gNB-only),
while Nokia and Qualcomm continued to advocate for distributed (multi-TRP). The
service type definitions were written to be architecture-agnostic: a given
sensing service can be provided by either a monostatic or distributed
implementation, with different accuracy and coverage characteristics.

# MIMO: Phase 5 Closes, Phase 6 Continues

MIMO Phase 5 (NR_MIMO_Ph5-Core and -Perf) was winding down in RAN1, with the
normative work moving to RAN4 for conformance specification. Phase 6 continued
with SRS capacity and coverage work from Bangalore and new contributions on
multi-TRP CSI acquisition.

The multi-TRP CSI question was the most technically challenging: in a
distributed MIMO system with three or more TRPs, the UE must report CSI for
each TRP, but the CSI feedback overhead scales with the number of TRPs. Phase 6
was evaluating CSI compression techniques specifically designed for multi-TRP
scenarios, where the spatial correlation between TRPs can be exploited to reduce
feedback overhead.

# Company Dynamics: Nokia Edges Ahead

Nokia took the top position by a small margin, followed closely by Ericsson. The
two infrastructure vendors had effectively traded places with the Samsung/
Sanechips/Qualcomm/HiSilicon pack from Bangalore. The distribution remained
relatively flat, characteristic of RAN1's coalition-building phase.

The moderator roles continued to be distributed across companies: Huawei for
ambient IoT, THALES for GNSS-resilient NTN, and the same set of moderators from
Bangalore for the continuing work items. The moderator system in RAN1 functions
as both a coordination mechanism and a power-distribution mechanism: companies
that serve as moderators write the meeting summaries that record decisions, and
those summaries shape how the work progresses to the next meeting.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#117

RAN1#123 was the last RAN1 meeting before RAN4 would feel the full force of
Rel-20. The next meeting in the series is RAN4#117 in Dallas, where Rel-20 would
finally break out in RAN4. The FS_6G_Radio study item, which had consumed
RAN1's attention for two meetings, would appear on RAN4's agenda for the first
time. The 6G channel models, waveform candidates, and numerology decisions from
RAN1 would cascade into RAN4 as RF and RRM requirements that needed conformance
specifications.

The RAN1-RAN4 lag was about to close, at least partially. RAN4#117 would be the
meeting where the two groups' timelines converged.
