# RAN1#121 - April 2025, Wuhan, China

RAN1#121 was the meeting where Rel-19 stopped being a debate and started being
a specification. Every major Rel-19 work item that RAN1 owned arrived in Wuhan
with the same instruction: stop arguing about scope, start writing the text that
goes into 38.211, 38.212, 38.213, 38.214, and 38.215. The change registers were
open, the editors were appointed, and the moderator summaries that had once
collected open issues were now collecting closing decisions. AI/ML for the air
interface, subband full duplex, MIMO Phase 5, NTN Phase 3, mobility Phase 4,
ambient IoT, low-power wake-up, and network energy saving were all converging on
final form at the same time. It was the last big Rel-19 RAN1 meeting, and it
showed: the contributions were dense, technical, and almost entirely about
parameters rather than principles.

Wuhan also set up one of the cleaner geographic coincidences in this series.
Two weeks after RAN1 left the city, RAN4 would arrive in the same Wuhan venue
for RAN4#115 to begin turning these physical-layer decisions into radio
requirements and conformance tests. The RAN1-to-RAN4 lag that the introduction
described as a structural feature of 3GPP was, for once, compressed into a
fortnight and a change of meeting badge. What RAN1 decided here, RAN4 would
inherit almost immediately. That made RAN1#121 the cleanest possible snapshot of
what Rel-19 actually was, before the requirements work began to reshape it.

The company hierarchy at this meeting was the classic RAN1 split between two
ecosystems and a long tail. Nokia and Ericsson tied at the top, with Samsung
close behind. HiSilicon led the Chinese contingent, with Sanechips and Qualcomm
clustered just behind it, and NTT DOCOMO, Apple, CATT, and vivo filling out the
upper tier. The pattern that runs through almost every topic below is the same:
Ericsson and Nokia pulling the design one way, HiSilicon pulling it another, and
the moderator role rotating between them as a way of sharing control of the
agenda.

# AI/ML for the Air Interface: The Final Rel-19 Round

The AI/ML for NR air interface work item (NR_AIML_air-Core) was the single most
active item at the meeting, and it carried the most unresolved tension. Rel-18
had produced a study; Rel-19 was supposed to produce normative specification for
three use cases (CSI feedback, beam management, positioning) plus an additional
study on CSI compression. By RAN1#121 the beam management and positioning work
was mostly converging on procedure text, but CSI compression remained the open
wound. It was the only use case that required a two-sided model, where the UE
runs an encoder and the network runs a matching decoder, and that two-sidedness
is what turned a signal-processing problem into a standardization fight.

## CSI Compression: NW-Side Versus UE-Side Training

Ericsson's contribution on AI/ML for CSI compression
[R1-2503245](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503245.zip)
pressed the inter-vendor training collaboration question that had hung over the
study since Rel-18. The core issue: if the UE encoder and the network decoder are
trained separately by different vendors, the two halves of the model have to
agree on a shared latent representation, or the decompressed CSI is garbage.
Ericsson favored a network-first training flow, in effect Option 4-1 in the
study's taxonomy, where the network side defines the dataset and the reference
encoder behavior, and UE vendors train their encoders to match. The advantage is
that the network keeps control of decoder design and can update it without
coordinating a UE recertification; the cost is that UE vendors lose freedom to
optimize their encoders against their own hardware.

HiSilicon came at the same problem from the opposite direction
[R1-2503254](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503254.zip),
arguing for extensions that fold more channel information into the encoder input,
including time-domain extension for TDD deployments where the uplink SRS can be
fused with downlink CSI to improve the compression. The SRS-fusion idea is
attractive precisely because TDD channel reciprocity gives the network a free
look at the channel through the uplink, so a UE encoder that conditions on both
the downlink measurement and the uplink sounding can compress more aggressively
for the same reconstruction quality. The catch is that it deepens the coupling
between the two model halves and makes the inter-vendor training problem worse,
not better, which is exactly the friction with Ericsson's network-first flow.

## Model Representation: ONNX or 3GPP-Defined

Underneath the training-flow argument sat a quieter but more consequential
question: how do you even describe a model in a 3GPP specification? Two camps
formed. One wanted to lean on an existing interchange format such as ONNX, so
that a model could be exported, transferred, and loaded across vendors with
tooling that already exists. The other wanted a 3GPP-defined representation,
narrower and more rigid, that constrains the model to a form the working group
can actually test and require. The trade is stark: ONNX is expressive enough to
carry almost any neural network, which is exactly why it is hard to bound a
conformance test around it; a 3GPP-defined format is testable precisely because
it is restrictive.

HiSilicon's broader AI/ML contributions, including its position on UE features
for the air interface
[R1-2503255](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503255.zip)
and its beam management view
[R1-2503251](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503251.zip),
treated model transfer and representation as a feature that has to be specified,
consistent with the broader Huawei and HiSilicon position that AI/ML belongs in
the standard with defined interfaces. The positioning accuracy track, moderated
by Ericsson across five summaries
[R1-2504691](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2504691.zip),
[R1-2504695](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2504695.zip),
was the most settled of the three use cases, with the argument largely reduced
to which spec sections carry the parameters. The change-register work was
captured in the editor and email-discussion summaries from the Huawei
[R1-2505028](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2505028.zip)
and Nokia
[R1-2503479](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503479.zip)
moderators, with Intel handling the 38.215 draft change request
[R1-2503451](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503451.zip).
The CSI compression representation question did not fully close in Wuhan, but the
framing had hardened: a restricted, testable representation for anything that
gets a conformance requirement, with the more expressive formats pushed toward
optional or implementation-defined territory.

# Subband Full Duplex and Cross-Link Interference

Subband full duplex (SBFD) was the marquee item of the duplex evolution work item
(NR_duplex_evo-Core), and HiSilicon owned the physical-layer design more
completely than any single company owned any other topic at the meeting. SBFD
lets a TDD cell transmit downlink and receive uplink in different subbands of the
same slot, which recovers uplink coverage and latency that pure TDD throws away.
The price is self-interference at the gNB and a new class of interference between
neighboring cells, and the Wuhan job was to pin down how the physical layer
signals the subband configuration and how the UE handles the resulting
interference.

## Semi-Static Subband Indication and PRG Size

HiSilicon's SBFD design contribution
[R1-2503261](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503261.zip)
laid out the core of the indication framework: the subband layout is
cell-specific and indicated semi-statically rather than dynamically, so that all
UEs in a cell share a common picture of which resource blocks are downlink,
which are uplink, and where the guard band sits. The semi-static choice matters
because a dynamically changing subband pattern would force the UE to re-derive
its rate-matching and measurement assumptions slot by slot, and it would make the
interference environment impossible to predict for neighbors. HiSilicon also
addressed the random access operation that has to coexist with the subband
structure
[R1-2503262](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503262.zip)
and the higher-layer parameters needed to carry the configuration
[R1-2503266](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503266.zip).

The PRG, the precoding resource block group, became the unit of the argument for
PDSCH scheduled across the subband boundary. With the downlink subband split into
non-contiguous regions, the precoder granularity has to be small enough that a
single PRG does not straddle the guard band, which pushed the design toward a PRG
size of 2 resource blocks for the affected transmissions. A smaller PRG means
finer precoding granularity at the cost of more demodulation reference signal
overhead, and that overhead trade was the heart of the negotiation over how SBFD
PDSCH gets scheduled.

# MIMO Phase 5: Finalizing UE-Initiated Beam Management

MIMO Phase 5 (NR_MIMO_Ph5-Core) was the second-largest item at the meeting and,
like SBFD, it was dominated at the design level by HiSilicon. Phase 5 was the
maintenance-and-completion release for the multi-TRP and beam management work
that had been building across Rel-17 and Rel-18, and the centerpiece in Wuhan
was UE-initiated and event-driven beam management, where the UE, rather than
waiting for a network-triggered report, can decide on its own that a beam has
degraded and ask for a new one.

## The Beam Management Endgame

The reason UE-initiated beam management is hard is that the moment you let the UE
trigger reporting, you have to define exactly when it is allowed to do so,
otherwise a poorly behaved UE floods the uplink with reports. HiSilicon's
contribution
[R1-2503275](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503275.zip)
worked through the mechanics that bound this behavior: the counter reset
conditions that determine when a degradation event is considered cleared, the
prohibit timer that prevents the UE from re-triggering too soon after a report,
the exact reporting content the UE sends, the collision handling when a
UE-initiated report runs into a network-triggered one, and the CPU occupancy
accounting that decides how much of the UE's CSI processing budget the new
behavior consumes. Each of these is a small parameter, and each one was a place
where UE vendors and network vendors disagreed about who pays the cost.

# NTN Phase 3: Squeezing Capacity and Coverage from Satellites

The non-terrestrial network work split into two work items, NR NTN Phase 3
(NR_NTN_Ph3-Core) and IoT NTN Phase 3 (IoT_NTN_Ph3-Core), and both arrived in
Wuhan focused on two practical problems: how to fit more users onto a satellite's
constrained uplink, and how to keep the downlink reaching devices that have no
business closing a link budget to orbit.

## Uplink Capacity and Orthogonal Cover Codes

Ericsson led the uplink capacity work for NR NTN
[R1-2503240](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503240.zip)
and for IoT NTN
[R1-2503338](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503338.zip).
The capacity problem in NTN is acute because the satellite beam covers a huge
geographic footprint with a fixed pool of uplink resources, so multiplexing more
PUSCH transmissions into the same resources directly multiplies the number of
users a beam can serve. Orthogonal cover codes are the lever: by spreading
multiple users' PUSCH with mutually orthogonal codes, the network can let several
devices share the same time-frequency resource and separate them at the receiver.
The design questions were which cover-code lengths to support and how to keep the
orthogonality intact across the long, frequency-shifted satellite channel, where
residual Doppler and timing error erode the very orthogonality the scheme depends
on.

HiSilicon contributed its own uplink capacity views for FR1-NTN
[R1-2503282](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503282.zip)
and for IoT NTN
[R1-2503283](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503283.zip),
and Ericsson, as moderator, carried the editor summaries for both the 38.211 NR
NTN change request
[R1-2505026](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2505026.zip)
and the 36.211 IoT NTN change request
[R1-2505020](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2505020.zip).
A coexisting liaison on contention-based Msg3 early data transmission drew a
discussion from Ericsson
[R1-2503339](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503339.zip)
and a reply from HiSilicon
[R1-2504292](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2504292.zip),
tying the random-access procedure into the capacity story.

## Downlink Coverage and HD-FDD RedCap

Downlink coverage was the other half of the NTN agenda, and it was the more
contested. The basic tool is repetition: send PDCCH and PDSCH multiple times so a
device with a weak link can accumulate energy across repetitions. THALES
moderated the downlink coverage enhancement thread through four successive
summaries
[R1-2503689](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503689.zip),
[R1-2503692](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503692.zip),
which is a heavy moderation load and a sign of how many open threads there were.

# Mobility Phase 4: LTM Measurements Mature

NR mobility enhancements Phase 4 (NR_Mob_Ph4-Core) was the meeting's quietest
big-ticket item, which is itself notable: L1/L2-triggered mobility, known as LTM,
had been the disruptive idea of the previous release, and by RAN1#121 it was
mature enough that the work was about measurement bookkeeping rather than
architecture. LTM moves the cell-change decision down from RRC to the physical
and MAC layers, which slashes handover latency but only works if the UE has fresh,
trustworthy measurements of the candidate cells before the switch is triggered.

HiSilicon's contribution on measurement-related enhancements for LTM
[R1-2503259](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503259.zip)
worked the candidate-cell measurement framework: how the UE measures cells it has
not yet connected to, including CSI-based measurements rather than relying solely
on the synchronization signal block, so that the network has L1-quality
information for the candidates it might switch to. The CSI-based path is faster
and finer-grained than SSB-based measurement, but it requires the candidate cell
to configure measurement resources for a UE that is not yet its own, which is the
coordination wrinkle the framework had to solve.

# Ambient IoT: The Modulation Decision

Ambient IoT (Ambient_IoT_Solutions-Core) was the most genuinely new physical
layer in Rel-19, a clean-sheet design for devices so power-constrained that they
may have no oscillator and no conventional transmitter. By RAN1#121 the work had
narrowed to the physical-channel design, and the sharpest single decision in
front of the group was the modulation choice for the device-to-reader direction.

The candidate that the room kept circling was on-off keying with a four-level
extension, OOK-4, and the argument was about how to generate it on the reader and
device sides. One school favored generating the OOK waveform through a CP-OFDM
transmitter, reusing the existing NR downlink chain so that ambient IoT signals
can be multiplexed cleanly into an NR carrier. Ericsson's modulation contribution
[R1-2503220](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503220.zip)
took a hard line against the CP-handling approach embodied in what the discussion
called Method Type 2, on the grounds that the cyclic prefix machinery designed for
OFDM does not map cleanly onto a backscatter or envelope-detection device that has
no concept of a CP. Ericsson's broader set of contributions on coding
[R1-2503221](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503221.zip),
timing and synchronization
[R1-2503222](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503222.zip),
and other physical-channel aspects
[R1-2503223](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503223.zip)
reinforced the same theme: keep the device side as dumb as physically possible.

The counterweight came from the camp that wanted to keep the generation flexible,
with HiSilicon
[R1-2503294](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503294.zip),
Nokia
[R1-2503299](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503299.zip),
Samsung
[R1-2503566](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503566.zip),
vivo
[R1-2503358](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503358.zip),
Sanechips
[R1-2503311](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503311.zip),
CATT
[R1-2503793](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503793.zip),
CMCC
[R1-2503831](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503831.zip),
and FUTUREWEI
[R1-2503224](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG1_RL1/TSGR1_121/Docs/R1-2503224.zip)
all filing modulation positions. The split was less along the usual east-west
ecosystem lines than along a device-complexity axis: how much waveform-shaping
burden the reader can carry to keep the device simple, versus how cleanly the
ambient IoT transmission has to coexist with the host NR carrier. The
DFT-S-OFDM-versus-CP-OFDM framing of how to generate OOK was the technical proxy
for that whole argument, and it did not fully resolve in Wuhan.

# Network Energy Saving: On-Demand SSB and SIB1

The network energy saving enhancement work item (Netw_Energy_NR_enh-Core) drew
some of the broadest company interest at the meeting, and for a reason that has
nothing to do with radio performance and everything to do with operating cost: a
base station that can switch off its always-on signals saves real money and real
carbon. The Rel-19 lever was making the cell's mandatory broadcast transmissions
optional, transmitted only when a device actually needs them.

The two centerpieces were on-demand SSB and on-demand SIB1. On-demand SSB lets a
secondary cell skip transmitting its synchronization signal block until a UE
requests it, which means a lightly loaded cell can go nearly dark. HiSilicon

# Company Dynamics

The signature of RAN1#121 was the two-ecosystem structure operating at full
intensity. Nokia and Ericsson tied at the top of the contribution count, and the
two companies divided the editor and moderator roles between them across the major
items: Ericsson held the pen on NTN Phase 3 and MIMO Phase 5's 38.211 register,
Nokia held the CSI and uplink change requests in MIMO Phase 5 and the mobility
Phase 4 change request. HiSilicon, leading the Chinese contingent, was the single
most influential design voice across SBFD, MIMO Phase 5 beam management, NTN
coverage, network energy saving, and LP-WUS, filing the anchor contribution in
each. Sanechips and Qualcomm rounded out the upper tier, with Qualcomm's most
visible role being the moderator chair on the AI/ML CSI compression study rather
than a flood of its own positions.

The moderator distribution was a map of who controlled which agenda. Qualcomm ran
AI/ML CSI compression, Ericsson ran AI/ML positioning and NTN, THALES ran NTN
downlink coverage, Huawei ran SBFD CLI and several email discussions, Nokia ran
the MIMO and mobility change requests, and Intel handled the 38.215 registers for
both AI/ML and MIMO. Handing a topic's moderator pen to a company with a stake in
the outcome, Qualcomm on a UE-side compression question, THALES on satellite
coverage, is the normal way 3GPP works, and the summaries those moderators
produced are where the meeting's real decisions were recorded.

Below the top tier, the long tail did the heavy lifting of legitimizing the
agreements. NTT DOCOMO, Apple, CATT, vivo, OPPO, NEC, CMCC, MediaTek, and Xiaomi
all carried substantial loads, and smaller players such as FUTUREWEI, Tejas
Network, EURECOM, SageRAN, and TCL filed targeted contributions on the topics
they cared most about. The breadth is the point: a Rel-19 work item only closes
when the proposal survives contact with this many independent reviewers, and the
density of remaining-issue contributions at this meeting was the clearest sign
that almost everything was about to close.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#115

RAN1#121 was the meeting that handed Rel-19 to RAN4. AI/ML for the air interface
had its three normative use cases on track and its CSI compression study narrowing
toward a representation and training flow. SBFD had a cell-specific semi-static
subband structure, a PRG size of 2 for the boundary-spanning PDSCH, and SRS-RSRP
as the CLI workhorse. MIMO Phase 5 had closed in on the UE-initiated beam
management timers and counters. NTN Phase 3 had orthogonal cover codes for uplink
capacity and a repetition framework for downlink coverage. Mobility Phase 4 had
its LTM candidate-cell measurement framework. Ambient IoT had its modulation
choice down to how OOK gets generated. Network energy saving had on-demand SSB and
SIB1 taking shape.

Two weeks later, RAN4 would convene in the same Wuhan venue for RAN4#115 and begin
turning every one of those decisions into radio requirements and conformance
tests. The SBFD self-interference numbers, the LP-WUS wake-up receiver RF
assumptions that Sanechips had already started coordinating, the NTN coverage link
budgets, the low-band carrier aggregation switching gaps: all of it now belonged
to RAN4. The fortnight between the two Wuhan meetings was the shortest the
RAN1-to-RAN4 lag would get in this entire series, and it makes RAN4#115 the
cleanest possible test of how RAN1's last big Rel-19 design meeting survived
contact with the requirements engineers.
