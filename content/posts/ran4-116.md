Bangalore was the meeting where Rel-19 closed. Four weeks after RAN1#122 had
erupted with 6G studies in the same city, RAN4 convened in Bangalore for the
largest meeting of the series and the last one where Rel-19 would dominate. The
meeting produced the final Big CRs for LP-WUS, SBFD, and Ambient IoT. Three
work items that had consumed RAN4's attention since Orlando reached their
specification conclusion. And for the first time, RAN4 began organizing for its
own Rel-20 future.

The Rel-20 signal jumped from 7 TDocs at Wuhan to 29 at Bangalore. More
importantly, the character of the Rel-20 contributions changed. The scoping
papers from earlier meetings were joined by draft work item descriptions,
new WID proposals for Rel-20 features, and the first organizational steps:
Apple, as RAN4 Chair, filed guidance on Rel-19/Rel-20 basket work items and a
plan for 6G discussion and meeting arrangement [R4-2511652, R4-2511653]. RAN4
was not just talking about Rel-20. It was planning how to work on it.

# LP-WUS: The Final Specification

LP-WUS at RAN4#116 was about completion. After three meetings of framework
setting, simulation campaigns, and incremental CR drafting, the work item
produced its final Big CRs covering the full UE RF requirements and the
complete measurement and RRM specification stack.

## vivo's Big CR Completes the UE Requirements

vivo, which had taken the specification lead for LP-WUS UE RF requirements at
Athens, filed the definitive Big CR for LP-WUS UE RF requirements in Rel-19
[R4-2510293]. This CR consolidated all the UE RF clauses that had been
discussed across Orlando, Athens, and Wuhan into a single specification change:
reference sensitivity (REFSENS), adjacent channel selectivity (ACS), adjacent
channel selectivity with simultaneous transmission (ASCS), in-band blocking
(IBB), out-of-band blocking (OBB), receiver intermodulation, spurious
emissions, and the full set of RF testability clauses. A companion draft CR
[R4-2510296] addressed REFSENS, ACS, and ASCS specifically.

The Big CR represented the end of the LP-WUS specification-writing phase. The
parameter values had converged: -97 dBm reference sensitivity at 10 MHz
bandwidth, 9 dB noise figure, the 3-4 dB margin between wake-up receiver and
main receiver sensitivity that Apple had proposed in Athens. The mandate fight
was resolved by data: the simulation results showed that the relaxed sensitivity
was sufficient for operator coverage requirements, and LP-WUR became mandatory
for premium device categories and optional for reduced-capability devices.

## The Full Measurement Stack

The measurement and RRM specification for LP-WUS reached completion with CRs
covering every UE state and measurement type. CATT filed draft CRs on
definitions, symbols, and abbreviations [R4-2509285], inter-RAT E-UTRAN
measurements for UE with LP-WUR [R4-2509286], and UE channel bandwidth
[R4-2509379]. CATT also filed CRs on base station declaration of LP-WUS support
[R4-2509383, R4-2509384], the BS-side conformance equivalent of the UE
requirements.

OPPO filed a draft CR on intra-frequency NR cell measurements for UE with LP-WUR
in IDLE and INACTIVE states [R4-2509678]. LG Electronics filed a draft CR on
inter-frequency NR cell measurements [R4-2509739]. Xiaomi filed a draft CR on
serving cell measurement and evaluation requirements [R4-2509776]. CMCC filed
draft CRs on inter-frequency and inter-RAT cell reselection [R4-2510031]. vivo
filed draft CRs on inactive-state LP-WUR serving cell measurements
[R4-2510197].

The measurement stack covered every combination of UE state (IDLE, INACTIVE,
connected), frequency relationship (intra-frequency, inter-frequency,
inter-RAT), and measurement purpose (cell selection, cell reselection, serving
cell evaluation). The completeness of the stack was the signal that LP-WUS was
done: when every clause has a CR, the work item is ready for approval.

# SBFD: Closing the Specification

SBFD followed LP-WUS's trajectory from framework to specification, reaching its
own CR completion at RAN4#116. The CRs covered the full set of new and modified
requirements that SBFD operation demanded.

## L1-CLI-RSSI: The Last Measurement Object

Samsung filed a draft CR on general L1-CLI-RSSI measurements [R4-2509239],
and CATT filed draft CRs to update those measurement requirements
[R4-2509305]. L1-CLI-RSSI (Cross-Link Interference Received Signal Strength
Indicator) was the last measurement object needed for SBFD. It measures the
total received power from interfering UEs, as opposed to L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP which
measures specific SRS resources. RSSI-based measurement is simpler and faster
but less accurate. RSRP-based measurement is more accurate but requires SRS
configuration and scheduling. The specification included both, with RSSI as
the default measurement for basic CLI awareness and RSRP as the enhanced
measurement for scheduling optimization.

Nokia filed draft CRs on core requirements for UE-to-UE CLI handling
[R4-2509617] and L1 CLI performance requirements [R4-2509621], establishing
the measurement accuracy targets: plus or minus 3 dB for L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP and
plus or minus 6 dB for L1-CLI-RSSI, at the 90th percentile confidence level.

## SCell Activation and CSI-RS Reporting

Nokia filed a draft CR on SCell activation for SBFD SCell [R4-2509619],
addressing a deployment scenario where a secondary cell operates in SBFD mode
while the primary cell operates in conventional TDD. The SCell activation
procedure must account for the different reference signal configuration of the
SBFD cell.

OPPO filed a draft CR on CSI-RS based L1-RSRP reporting with SBFD
[R4-2509691], and China Telecom filed a draft CR on CSI-RS based CBD
evaluation period requirements with SBFD [R4-2510087]. The CSI-RS reporting
chain was the most affected by SBFD because the gNB's CSI-RS transmission on a
DL sub-band may be adjacent to the UE's SRS transmission on a UL sub-band,
creating a new interference scenario that the UE must handle in its measurement
reporting.

Apple filed a draft CR on clarification of DL and UL slots in SBFD
[R4-2509991], addressing an editorial issue that had practical consequences:
the slot format definitions in TS 38.213 used the terms "DL slot" and "UL slot"
assuming TDD operation. In SBFD, a slot can be simultaneously DL on one
sub-band and UL on another, making the existing terminology ambiguous. Apple's
CR introduced the concept of "SBFD symbols" to distinguish them from
conventional DL and UL symbols.

CATT filed draft CRs for TS 38.104 on conducted and OTA out-of-band blocking
for SBFD [R4-2509326] and for TS 38.101-1 on updating duty cycle evaluation
[R4-2509386], the BS and UE complements to the RRM requirements.

## STxMP: Multi-Panel Power Control Gets Specified

Simultaneous Transmission from Multiple Panels was the quiet but consequential
Rel-19 MIMO feature that reached its specification peak at Bangalore. After
Wuhan's initial OTA test method discussions, the feature produced concrete CRs
covering maximum transmission power, test tolerance, and the liaison exchange
between RAN1 and RAN4.

The key specification challenge was maximum transmission power for a UE
transmitting from multiple panels simultaneously. In single-panel operation,
maximum power is straightforward: the UE's power amplifier is set to its rated
output, and the total radiated power is measured. In STxMP, each panel has its
own power amplifier, and the UE must manage the total power across panels to
stay within regulatory limits while maximizing throughput.

RAN1 sent a liaison statement on maximum transmission power for STxMP
[R4-2509008], specifying the power allocation rules: the UE may distribute its
total power budget across panels according to a configured power ratio, and
the per-panel power must not exceed the panel's individual maximum. vivo replied
to the LS on behalf of RAN4 [R4-2510273], confirming that the RF requirements
are testable with the two-step OTA method proposed at Wuhan.

Samsung filed CRs to TS 38.101-2 on maximum transmission power for STxMP
[R4-2511070, R4-2511071], the normative text that defines the power measurement
procedure and conformance limits. HiSilicon filed three updates to the maximum
transmission power test procedure [R4-2511098, R4-2511099, R4-2511699],
refining the test configuration and measurement uncertainty budget. Nokia filed
a reply LS to RAN1 [R4-2511272] confirming RAN4 agreement on the power
allocation framework.

The test equipment industry weighed in. Keysight contributed a technical report
proposal to TR 38.771 on FR2 STxMP test tolerance [R4-2512595], providing the
test vendor's assessment of achievable measurement accuracy for multi-panel
OTA. Samsung discussed test tolerance for multi-panel transmission
[R4-2510226], and vivo contributed the TR 38.771 update on sTxMP test
tolerance [R4-2510245].

The STxMP specification completed at Bangalore was a milestone for FR2 MIMO.
Multi-panel UEs would become the standard architecture for mmWave devices, where
a single panel cannot cover the full sphere around the device due to the body
blocking effect at mmWave frequencies. By enabling simultaneous transmission
from multiple panels, STxMP turned a coverage workaround (multiple panels for
beam coverage) into a throughput feature (multiple panels for spatial
multiplexing).

# Ambient IoT: TS 38.194 Takes Shape

Ambient IoT at RAN4#116 was where the specification became real. After Wuhan's
initial specification push, Bangalore saw the Big CRs that would define the
ambient IoT conformance framework.

## HiSilicon's Big CR and the OTA Framework

HiSilicon filed the foundational specification contribution for ambient IoT at
this meeting: a big draft CR for TS 38.194 [R4-2509896]. TS 38.194 is the
base station conformance specification for ambient IoT, and HiSilicon's Big CR
covered the full set of BS transmitter and receiver requirements: output power,
transmit ON/OFF power, frequency error, unwanted emissions, transmitter
intermodulation, reference sensitivity, in-band blocking, out-of-band blocking,
and receiver intermodulation. The Big CR was the ambient IoT equivalent of
vivo's LP-WUS Big CR: the consolidated specification text that would define the
feature's conformance framework.

LG Electronics UK filed a draft CR on ambient IoT BS general requirements
[R4-2510079], CMCC filed a draft CR on device-to-reader timing requirements
[R4-2510178], Xiaomi filed a draft CR on contention-based random access
behavior [R4-2509804], and Ericsson filed a draft CR on ambient IoT random
access [R4-2511263]. HiSilicon filed a draft CR on carrier frequency
acquisition RRM requirements [R4-2510606].

## Test Methods: The Apple-vivo Split

The OTA test method question that CMCC had raised at Wuhan produced competing
proposals at Bangalore. Apple filed a Big CR to TR 38.870 for Rel-19 OTA test
methodologies [R4-2510286], proposing a comprehensive OTA test framework based
on anechoic chamber measurements with a reference reader. vivo filed a CR to TS
38.870 on ambient IoT test methods [R4-2510254], proposing a simpler conducted
test approach where the device's antenna is replaced with a cable connection
for test purposes.

The Apple-vivo split on test methodology reflected a deeper disagreement about
what ambient IoT conformance testing should verify. Apple's OTA approach tested
the complete device including the antenna, which is integral to an ambient IoT
device since the antenna both harvests energy and communicates. vivo's conducted
approach tested only the chipset, which is simpler and cheaper but does not
verify the antenna's energy harvesting efficiency or backscatter modulation
performance.

The debate was unresolved at Bangalore. The test method question would carry
into Rel-20, where the indoor and outdoor ambient IoT work items would need
separate test frameworks for their different deployment scenarios.

# Rel-20: From Scoping to WID Proposals

The Rel-20 contributions at RAN4#116 were qualitatively different from the
scoping papers that had characterized earlier meetings. Companies were now
submitting draft work item descriptions, concrete new WID proposals, and
technical contributions to specific Rel-20 features.

## New Work Item Descriptions Proliferate

Nokia submitted a draft work item description for NR Demodulation Performance
Phase 6 [R4-2509152], the Rel-20 successor to the demodulation Phase 5 work
item that was closing in Rel-19. The Phase 6 WID proposed to extend demodulation
requirements to 200 MHz channel bandwidth, MIMO layers beyond 4 for FR1, and
spatial channel model-based testing for distributed MIMO.

Nokia also submitted a new WID for DC_R20_xBLTE_yBNR [R4-2510907], continuing
the EN-DC and NR-DC dual connectivity work with new band combinations for
Rel-20 carriers. MediaTek contributed draft WIDs for NR FR1 DL Fragmented
Carriers [R4-2510786] and provided a draft CR with a time mask for LB-LB
switching [R4-2510788], building on the low-band CA switching study that had
been progressing through Rel-19.

MediaTek also pitched NTN work for Rel-20: a discussion on Rel-20 NTN WI about
power class 1.5 HPUE or DL CA [R4-2510098], and NTN HD-FDD VSAT for Ka/Ku
bands work scope in Rel-20 [R4-2510183]. The VSAT work was the natural
progression of the Ku-band NTN work that had started in Athens: now that
Ku-band system parameters were defined, the VSAT device requirements could be
specified.

## The 4.9 GHz US Band: American Operators Engage

A new thread appeared at Bangalore: the 4.9 GHz band (4940-4990 MHz) for US
public safety operation. APCO International, representing US public safety
communications officials, filed a discussion on coexistence requirements
[R4-2509944]. T-Mobile USA contributed on 4.9 GHz band coexistence
[R4-2509988]. Ericsson filed two TDocs on the new 4.9 GHz US band coexistence
with adjacent services [R4-2510514] and with other users of the band
[R4-2510515]. AT&T Services contributed a coexistence discussion for the
North American 4.9 GHz band [R4-2511346].

The 4.9 GHz band contributions were significant because they represented
American operators engaging directly in RAN4 for the first time in the series.
Previous meetings had been dominated by European and Chinese companies. The US
4.9 GHz band, allocated for public safety and critical communications, had
specific coexistence requirements with adjacent federal and commercial users
that required 3GPP specification support. The American operator engagement was
a reminder that RAN4 specifications have real-world spectrum deployment
consequences.

KDDI proposed a draft new WID on EESS (Earth Exploration-Satellite Service)
protection and power class 1 requirements for 40 GHz [R4-2510373], another
frequency-range-specific work item driven by operator deployment needs.

## Apple's Chair Role: Organizing the 6G Transition

Apple, in its role as RAN4 Chair, filed two organizational contributions that
were unlike any previous Rel-20 TDoc. A plan on RAN4 6G discussion and meeting
arrangement in Rel-20 [R4-2511652] laid out how RAN4 would organize its 6G
work: which sessions would discuss 6G topics, how 6G would be coordinated with
ongoing Rel-19 and Rel-20 work, and what the meeting schedule would look like
through the Rel-20 timeline. A guidance and plan on Rel-19/Rel-20 basket WI
[R4-2511653] defined how work items that span both releases would be managed.

The chair contributions were procedural documents, but they were the first
indication that RAN4 leadership was actively planning the Rel-20 transition. The
meeting arrangement plan was particularly significant because it meant that
RAN4's 6G work would no longer be scattered across scoping papers and ad-hoc
discussions: it would have dedicated session time, assigned rapporteurs, and
a structured work plan.

Skyworks Solutions contributed inputs to RAN4 Rel-20 5GA and 6G work
[R4-2511645], providing a component vendor's perspective on what Rel-20 RF
requirements would mean for the RF front-end supply chain. vivo replied to a
liaison statement on maximum transmission power for STxMP [R4-2511781], an
early 6G multi-TRP transmission topic.

# NTN Phase 3: Final Ku-Band and IoT Specifications

NTN work at RAN4#116 continued at high volume but with a shift toward
performance (Perf) work items. NR_NTN_Ph3-Perf and NR_IoT_NTN_req_test_enh-Perf
indicated that NTN conformance testing was now the primary activity, replacing
the requirement definition work of earlier meetings.

The Ku-band NTN work item grew to 75 contributions, its peak in the series. The
work was now focused on specific RF parameter values rather than system
parameter frameworks. The VSAT terminal requirements that Ericsson had proposed
in Athens were being specified with concrete power levels, antenna gain
assumptions, and adjacent channel selectivity targets.

IoT NTN testing continued at high volume with the 3 MHz channel bandwidth work
now in its CR finalization phase. The less-than-5 MHz NTN work that Apple had
driven through eight TDocs at Athens was now mature enough for normative CRs
across multiple specifications.

# Company Dynamics: Ericsson Regains the Lead

Ericsson retook the top position with a slight edge over HiSilicon, reversing
the Wuhan tie and Athens's HiSilicon lead. The infrastructure vendors (Ericsson,
Nokia) gained share in the specification-writing phase, while UE chipset vendors
(HiSilicon, Sanechips) maintained their strong presence.

Apple consolidated its fourth-place position and expanded its role from
contributor to chair, filing both technical CRs and organizational guidance.
Qualcomm rose further, driven by EN-DC RF Phase 4, SBFD, and carrier aggregation
contributions. T-Mobile USA and AT&T Services appeared as new contributors,
reflecting the US 4.9 GHz band topic.

The Rel-20 contributions came from a broader set of companies than in previous
meetings: Apple (chair), Nokia, MediaTek, vivo, KDDI, AT&T, Intel, Skyworks,
Qualcomm, and Ericsson all filed Rel-20 TDocs. The Rel-20 conversation was
spreading beyond the early movers (Apple, Intel, BT) to engage the full RAN4
community.

# Looking Ahead to RAN1#123

RAN4#116 was the closing chapter of Rel-19. The LP-WUS Big CR, the SBFD
measurement stack, the Ambient IoT TS 38.194 Big CR, and the NTN Ku-band
specifications represented the completion of the work items that had defined
RAN4's Rel-19 effort since Orlando.

But the meeting also opened the Rel-20 chapter. The draft WIDs for demodulation
Phase 6, fragmented carriers, DC R20, and NTN VSAT were concrete proposals for
what RAN4 would specify in Rel-20. Apple's chair contributions established the
organizational framework for 6G work. The Rel-20 signal was no longer a scoping
exercise: it was a work plan.

The next meeting in the series is RAN1#123, back in Arlington, where RAN1 would
continue the 6G physical layer design that had launched at RAN1#122. The RAN1
meeting would define features that RAN4, now organized for Rel-20, would
eventually have to specify conformance tests for. The RAN1-RAN4 cascade was
entering its most active phase.
