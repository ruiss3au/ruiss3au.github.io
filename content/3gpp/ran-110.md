When the Technical Specification Group for the Radio Access Network gathers for its plenary, the room does not debate the shape of a beamforming codebook or the bit width of a feedback report. That work happens in the working groups. The plenary is where the group decides what the working groups are allowed to spend the next year arguing about. It approves study items and work items, it sets their scope and their deadlines, it blesses the technical reports the groups have produced, and occasionally it tells a group to slow down or to coordinate with a neighbor before charging ahead. Baltimore in December 2025 was exactly that kind of meeting: short, dense, administrative, and quietly decisive about the direction of Release 20 and the first concrete contours of 6G.

## How a Plenary Shapes a Release

The mechanics matter, because they explain why a plenary reads so differently from a working group meeting. A working group produces change requests, simulation results, and detailed text proposals. A plenary produces decisions: this study is approved, this work item is revised, this report is taken to the next version. The currency here is the work item description and the study item description, and the verbs are approve, revise, and defer. Status reports flow upward from the working group chairs, and the plenary checks whether the schedule still holds. The RAN1 chair, for instance, brought the WG1 status report to Baltimore for the group to note its progress and remaining bottlenecks ([RP-252972](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-252972.zip)). Most of the real fights at a plenary are about scope and timing rather than technique, and Baltimore had several of those.

## 6G Takes Center Stage: Requirements and the Race to TR 38.914

The center of gravity in Baltimore was the 6G scenarios and requirements study, captured in TR 38.914. CMCC arrived as one of the most prolific contributors to the requirements track, bringing a text proposal that set out the deployment scenarios the report would recognize ([RP-253115](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253115.zip)) and a companion proposed change to the report covering the key performance indicators for 6G ([RP-253116](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253116.zip)). The KPI proposal is where the ambition becomes numeric: peak spectral efficiency targets reaching into the tens of bits per second per hertz, single-digit-millisecond latency budgets for immersive traffic, and a still tighter bound for high-reliability low-latency communication. CMCC also pressed the report to commit to a coherent set of deployment scenarios spanning urban, rural, and extreme-distance coverage, alongside discussions of energy efficiency requirements ([RP-253117](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253117.zip)) and coverage evaluation methodology ([RP-253118](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253118.zip)).

This was not a one-company show. A large operator bloc led by Deutsche Telekom fed the report parallel text proposals on architecture and migration requirements ([RP-253079](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253079.zip)) and on diverse device types ([RP-253080](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253080.zip)), keeping the requirements grounded in what carriers will actually deploy. The net result of all this input was a new version of the report: TR 38.914 advanced to v0.3.0, capturing the agreements reached in Baltimore ([RP-253750](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253750.zip)). A report at v0.3.0 is still soft clay, but the scenarios and KPI skeleton it now carries are the frame every later 6G work item will have to fit inside.

## The Architecture Fight: Service-Based RAN and Day-One 6GC

The most consequential disagreement in Baltimore was not about numbers but about the basic shape of the 6G radio network. CMCC pushed hard for a service-based RAN architecture, arguing in a dedicated contribution that the radio side should adopt the kind of service-oriented interfaces that the core network already uses ([RP-253130](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253130.zip)), and laying out broader views on how the 6G RAN architecture should be organized ([RP-253120](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253120.zip)). This is a structural bet: a service-based RAN would make functions more modular and cloud-friendly, at the cost of more interface machinery.

ZTE came at the architecture question from a different angle, pressing for a clean, standalone 6G radio network connecting to a 6G core as a day-one priority rather than an evolution bolted onto existing 5G deployments. Its views on architecture and migration requirements made the case for treating the new-radio-to-new-core path as the primary design target ([RP-253195](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253195.zip)), and it reinforced that position with its broader views on how the 6G RAN working-group study should be framed ([RP-253702](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253702.zip)) and on the services the radio network must support from the outset ([RP-253191](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253191.zip)). The plenary did not crown a winner here, and it was never going to. What it did was keep both visions inside the study scope, which guarantees that the service-based-versus-standalone debate will be reopened, in sharper form, in the working groups.

## Device Types: XR Wearables Enter the 6G Conversation

One of the quieter but telling moves came from Meta, which argued that the 6G device type taxonomy should explicitly recognize extended-reality wearables as a first-class category ([RP-253172](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253172.zip)). Glasses and headsets behave unlike phones: they are power-constrained, latency-sensitive, and often tethered to a companion device. Getting them written into the requirements as a distinct device type early shapes how power, bandwidth, and latency targets get apportioned later. Meta's push sat alongside the operator bloc's own diverse-device-types text proposal, signaling that the device taxonomy for 6G is being negotiated now rather than left to chance.

## Ambient IoT Phase 2: Going Outdoors and Active

Ambient IoT, the batteryless and ultra-low-power device track, moved into its more ambitious phase. Huawei, as rapporteur, brought the status report for the Phase 2 work on Ambient IoT solutions in the radio, confirming the work item remains on its planned trajectory ([RP-253109](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253109.zip)). The more interesting development was the revised study item, carried by LG, that pushes the enhancement work toward outdoor scenarios and active devices ([RP-253394](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253394.zip)). The earlier Ambient IoT work leaned on passive, backscatter-style devices in benign indoor settings. Extending to outdoor coverage and to devices with active amplification, the kind sometimes labeled Device 2b and Device C in the working-group discussions, is a substantial range and reliability ask. The plenary kept the study moving, which sets up the working groups to wrestle with the harder link budgets that active outdoor operation demands.

## GNSS-Resilient NTN: A Study Survives

Non-terrestrial networks brought one of the cleaner outcomes of the meeting. THALES carried a revised study item description for GNSS-resilient operation of satellite radio links, addressing what happens when a device cannot rely on a clean satellite-positioning fix to time and steer its transmissions ([RP-253137](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253137.zip)), and brought the matching status report to show the study's progress ([RP-253138](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253138.zip)). The technical community weighed in through a multi-company views paper led by Sharp on how GNSS-less and GNSS-resilient operation should be studied ([RP-253443](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253443.zip)). The plenary approved the revised study, with the understanding that the next plenary becomes a checkpoint: the group will decide then whether the findings justify opening normative work, the stage at which study conclusions become binding specification text.

## Base Station RF Evolution: Living With Self-Interference

On the radio-hardware side, ZTE drove the base station RF requirement evolution work into a second phase. It brought the revised work item description for the phase-two effort ([RP-253210](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253210.zip)), alongside the status report ([RP-253516](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253516.zip)) and revised description for the ongoing phase ([RP-253517](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253517.zip)). The headline item is subband full-duplex coexistence: when a base station transmits and receives in the same band at once, it has to define how much interference it can throw at a neighboring base station and tolerate from one. That means new base-station-to-base-station coexistence requirements and tighter rules for co-locating equipment, with particular attention to bands above 3.3 GHz where the duplexing pressure is sharpest. Approving the phase-two scope commits RAN4 to producing those coexistence numbers, which are the unglamorous prerequisite for any operator that wants to deploy full-duplex without wrecking its own adjacent sites.

## A Security Problem That Crosses Group Lines

Not every contribution in Baltimore asked the plenary to approve scope. Some asked it to fix a coordination problem. Ericsson, ZTE, and ZTE's Sanechips arm jointly raised a flag that the 6G security framework cannot be designed in a single group's silo ([RP-253197](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253197.zip)). Their concern is that lower-layer security and the protection of system information broadcast in the SIBs straddle the boundary between the security group, SA3, and the radio protocol group, RAN2. Designing one without the other invites either gaps or duplicated, incompatible mechanisms. The request was for an explicit joint session so the two groups settle the security framework together rather than discovering the seams later. This is the kind of plumbing decision that never makes a press release but quietly determines whether 6G security is coherent or bolted together.

## The IoDT Standoff

The sharpest single-company stance in Baltimore came from OPPO over interoperability and device testing, the IoDT issues that decide how cleanly multivendor 6G equipment will actually interwork. OPPO argued against baking vendor-specific differentiation mechanisms into the 6G design as a way to paper over interoperability problems ([RP-253048](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253048.zip)). Its worry is that letting each vendor solve interworking its own way fragments the ecosystem and undermines the whole point of a global standard. The counter-pressure was visible in the room: Qualcomm framed part of the problem as a question of UE capability signaling procedures ([RP-253491](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253491.zip)), and a broad cross-vendor group including Apple, OPPO, and Huawei circulated views on restricting which radio access technologies a device may use ([RP-253747](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_110/Docs/RP-253747.zip)). OPPO's position was a warning shot rather than a settled outcome, and the interoperability-versus-differentiation tension it exposed is one the working groups will inherit.

## What Baltimore Settled, and What It Left Open

Read as a whole, RAN#110 advanced the 6G frame without resolving its central arguments. The requirements report gained a real version and a numeric skeleton, the GNSS-resilient satellite study was approved with a clear checkpoint ahead, the base station full-duplex coexistence work got its phase-two mandate, and Ambient IoT was pointed outdoors toward active devices. What stayed open was everything that matters most: whether the 6G RAN is service-based or standalone-first, how device types like XR wearables reshape the requirements, whether security is designed jointly or in pieces, and whether interoperability survives the pull toward vendor-specific differentiation. A plenary is good at setting the table. It is deliberately bad at eating the meal, and Baltimore left a full plate for the groups.

The next plenary, RAN#111, would take place in March 2026 in Fukuoka where the GNSS-resilient NTN study reaches its normative-work checkpoint, the 6G architecture debate between service-based and standalone-first designs returns with the working groups' first technical findings in hand, and the requirements report moves another version closer to the locked-down frame that will govern the rest of Release 20.
