Dalian was the meeting where RAN4 tipped. Rel-20 contributions outnumbered
Rel-19 for the first time in the series. The margin, 41 percent to 13 percent,
was not the landslide of RAN1#125's 93 percent a week earlier in the same city.
But the crossing of the threshold, after eight meetings where Rel-19 had
dominated, was the structural event that the entire series had been marching
toward.

The context was the approaching RAN#112 plenary in Singapore, four weeks away.
That plenary would freeze the Rel-20 Stage-2 scope. All feature decisions had to
be locked. The Dalian RAN4 meeting was the last chance for the group to submit
its RF feasibility assessments, its RRM measurement frameworks, and its
conformance test plans to the plenary. The meeting had the urgency of a
deadline.

## FS_6G_Radio: RAN4's 6G Specification Framework

FS_6G_Radio at Dalian was the culmination of RAN4's 6G work that had started at
Dallas five months earlier. The operation efficiency discussions from Dallas and
Gothenburg had produced concrete specification framework proposals. The RAN1
waveform, numerology, and MIMO decisions from Gothenburg and Dalian had been
absorbed into RAN4's RF requirement work.

The 6G specification framework defined at this meeting included: a waveform-
agnostic transmitter requirement structure where MPR, ACLR, and EVM are
functions of waveform parameters rather than hard-coded for OFDM, a channel-
bandwidth-scalable receiver requirement framework where REFSENS, ACS, and
blocking limits are continuous functions of channel bandwidth, and an RRM
measurement framework that accommodates the shorter slot durations and wider
subcarrier spacings of 6G.

This framework consolidated proposals that had been building since Dallas
[R4-2606297](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606297.zip), [R4-2605226](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605226.zip).

Sanechips contributed views on 6G demodulation aspects of receiver assumptions,
interference modeling, and transmitter EVM assumptions [R4-2606297](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606297.zip). Nokia
contributed on 6G SI demodulation covering receiver assumptions, interference
profile modeling, and TxEVM assumptions [R4-2605226](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605226.zip). CATT discussed receiver
assumptions and interference modeling for 6G demodulation [R4-2606352](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606352.zip).
These contributions addressed the practical measurement framework: how should
conformance tests model the interference environment at 7-24 GHz where
interference characteristics differ from sub-7 GHz?

RAN4 also provided its RF feasibility assessment for the RAN#112 plenary. The
assessment confirmed that the 6G KPI targets were achievable with the agreed
waveform and MIMO configurations, but with specific caveats: the 100 Gbps peak
rate requires at least 8 MIMO layers and 256QAM modulation, which demands
high-SINR conditions only achievable at short range with line-of-sight; the 0.1
ms latency requires integrated access and backhaul or fiber fronthaul; and the
10x energy efficiency improvement assumes network-level sleep modes that reduce
always-on transmission, not just PHY-level efficiency gains
[R4-2605279](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605279.zip).

## Ambient IoT Phase 2: RAN4's Conformance Framework

Ambient IoT Phase 2 became the second-largest Rel-20 work item at RAN4#119, the
RAN4 counterpart to the RAN1 normative work. RAN4's work defined the
conformance testing framework for ambient IoT devices and readers: OTA test
methods for passive and active devices, reader sensitivity and transmit power
conformance tests, and coexistence testing with NR and LTE in shared spectrum.

CATT contributed on coexistence between active device 2b and NR/LTE
[R4-2606250](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606250.zip) and coexistence between active device C and NR/LTE [R4-2606247](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606247.zip),
addressing the practical deployment question of whether ambient IoT devices
interfere with existing NR and LTE UEs. LG Electronics filed CRs to TS 38.191
for correcting D2R transmission bandwidth [R4-2607193](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2607193.zip), [R4-2607195](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2607195.zip). HiSilicon
filed a CR for TS 38.195 to correct ACLR requirements [R4-2607275](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2607275.zip).

## Rel-20 Carrier Aggregation Expands

NR_CADC_SUL_R20_HPUE grew to become the third-largest work item. New band
combinations were added for 7-24 GHz carriers, new MPR and A-MPR values were
defined, and the HPUE framework was extended to support power class 1.5 (29
dBm) for fixed wireless access devices in the 7-24 GHz range.

Ericsson filed CRs to TS 38.101-1, 38.101-2, and 38.101-3 to remove
configuration tables and introduce a band combination database
[R4-2606379](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606379.zip), [R4-2606380](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606380.zip), [R4-2606381](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606381.zip), a specification structure improvement
that addresses the combinatorial explosion of band combinations. CHTTL filed
a draft CR for power class 1.5 uplink transmission with three transmit chains
[R4-2606554](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606554.zip). T-Mobile USA submitted a further revised WID for Rel-20 NR
carrier aggregation and dual connectivity [R4-2607184](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2607184.zip).

## AI/ML: Mobility and Air Interface Convergence

NR_AIML_Mob-Core and NR_AIML_air_Ph2-Core appeared together, reflecting
convergence of mobility and air interface AI/ML work. The mobility AI/ML
specification defined RRM measurement configurations for AI/ML-assisted cell
reselection and handover. The air interface AI/ML work from RAN1 was beginning
to produce RAN4 conformance requirements, though the full conformance framework
would be developed after RAN#112.

The convergence was driven by the AI-RRM work stream, where CMCC's Sub-Case 1
and Sub-Case 2 contributions bridged mobility prediction and air interface AI/ML
[R4-2606058](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606058.zip), [R4-2606059](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606059.zip).

## RRM Phase 6: Measurement Gaps for 7-24 GHz

NR_RRM_Ph6-Core, the work item that Apple had pitched since Athens in January
2025, was now an active normative work item at the final meeting of the series.
The Dalian contributions focused on measurement gap design for the 7-24 GHz
range. The 6G frame structure with 480 kHz subcarrier spacing and 0.125 ms slot
duration means that measurement gaps must be shorter and more frequent than in
NR. The Dalian RRM Phase 6 work defined measurement gap patterns with 0.5 ms
periodicity and 0.0625 ms duration, allowing RRM measurements to be interleaved
with data transmission at the slot level
[R4-2602666](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2602666.zip), [R4-2605329](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605329.zip).

A distinct sub-thread within RRM Phase 6 was AI-assisted RRM. CMCC contributed
on AI-RRM Sub-Case 1 covering FR2-1 L3 spatial domain beam-level prediction
for intra-cell transmission [R4-2606058](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606058.zip), and Sub-Case 2 covering FR1 L3
frequency domain cell-level prediction for inter-cell non-collocated scenarios
[R4-2606059](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606059.zip). CATT filed parallel contributions for both sub-cases
[R4-2605842](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605842.zip), [R4-2605843](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605843.zip). Apple contributed on AI-RRM same-FR non-collocated
cell prediction with channel models, cross-frequency bounds, and per-site
specialization [R4-2605329](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605329.zip). Samsung discussed FR1 L3 frequency domain
cell-level prediction for non-collocated inter-cell scenarios [R4-2606158](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606158.zip).
HiSilicon contributed on the framework for measurement data collection for
parameterized out-of-band correlation models [R4-2605736](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605736.zip), a foundational
piece for the AI-RRM training data framework.

## SCMC: The Gothia-Cell Debate Intensifies

The Single Cell Multi-Carrier debate reached RAN4#119 with sharply opposing
positions. vivo filed a comprehensive analysis of SCMC impacts on 6G RF
requirements, arguing that while SCMC does not require a fundamentally new UE RF
architecture, it imposes specific constraints: cross-carrier measurement
validity rules must be defined and the anchor carrier must provide coverage at
least as good as the data carriers [R4-2605978](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605978.zip).

Nokia took the hardest opposition line, strongly recommending against RAN4
engaging in the Gothia-cell concept at all [R4-2605529](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605529.zip). Nokia argued that
existing carrier aggregation and dual connectivity specifications already cover
the technical scenario. If CC1 carries control and CC2 carries data, that is
functionally equivalent to a primary cell and secondary cell in CA, and no new
specification work is needed. By denying SCMC a dedicated work item, Nokia
ensured the concept would be implemented through existing CA specifications
rather than requiring new RF requirement definitions.

Ericsson filed a comprehensive analysis of UE RF spectrum aggregation for 6G
[R4-2606660](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2606660.zip) that included SCMC as one of several spectrum aggregation
scenarios to be addressed within the existing framework. CATT proposed spectrum
aggregation as a foundational 6G feature [R4-2605794](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605794.zip), with SCMC as one
implementation option. The debate split three ways and the resolution would
wait for RAN#112.

### Spectrum Aggregation: The Bigger Frame

SCMC was one implementation option within a broader debate at RAN4#119: how
should 6G handle spectrum aggregation in general? Apple opened the discussion
on 6G spectrum aggregation topics [R4-2605279](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605279.zip), framing the question as a
choice between three models: keep 5G's carrier aggregation framework with
enhancements, adopt SCMC as a new aggregation paradigm, or define a
generalized spectrum aggregation framework that subsumes both.

Apple also contributed on 6G multi-RAT spectrum sharing [R4-2605330](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605330.zip). KDDI
contributed views on 6G UE RF for spectrum aggregation [R4-2605357](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605357.zip),
Spreadtrum filed its views [R4-2605363](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605363.zip), Qualcomm contributed its position
[R4-2605550](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605550.zip), MediaTek added views on 6G spectrum aggregation [R4-2605581](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605581.zip),
and Nokia filed a comprehensive general and UE RF spectrum aggregation
contribution [R4-2605529](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_119/Docs/R4-2605529.zip).

The spectrum aggregation debate was important because it determined how 6G
would use the fragmented spectrum that operators own: a mix of low-band
(sub-1 GHz), mid-band (1-7 GHz), and the new 7-24 GHz allocations. The choice
between CA and SCMC affected every aspect of the UE RF design: how many RF
chains the UE needs, whether it can share RF components across carriers, how
RRM measurements work across frequencies, and how power is managed when
transmitting on multiple carriers simultaneously.

## The Last of Rel-19

The remaining Rel-19 contributions at Dalian were final corrections and
editorial CRs. SBFD, once the flagship Rel-19 feature that had generated
passionate debate across the industry, was present only in -Perf form at modest
volume. MIMO Phase 5 had essentially closed. LP-WUS was absent. The Rel-19 work
items that had consumed RAN4's attention from Orlando to Bangalore were either
complete or represented by final maintenance contributions.

The transition that had begun with a single Rel-20 TDoc at RAN4#113,
introducing Band 111, was complete. The Band 111 CR was a reminder of how far
the work had come: from routine band additions to a comprehensive 6G
specification framework, from scoping papers to normative work items, from zero
FS_6G_Radio TDocs to 622.

## Company Dynamics: The Pre-Plenary Positioning

HiSilicon retook the top position at Dalian, reversing Ericsson's Gothenburg
lead. Ericsson and Nokia held the next two positions. Apple, now serving as
RAN4 Chair, was solidly established in the upper tier. The RRM Phase 6 work
item that Apple had championed was an active normative work item.

CATT, Sanechips, OPPO, vivo, Qualcomm, Samsung, and MediaTek rounded out the
top contributor list. The distribution was less flat than RAN1#125 but showed
the same pattern of broad participation as the plenary deadline approached.

## The Series So Far

RAN4#119 was the last meeting covered so far. Eleven meetings, seventeen months,
from November 2024 to May 2026. The series began with RAN4#113 in Orlando, a
meeting where Rel-20 existed as a single Band 111 CR and Rel-19 consumed all of
RAN4's energy. It ended with RAN4#119 in Dalian, where Rel-20 edged past
Rel-19 for the first time, FS_6G_Radio was the dominant work item, and the 6G
specification framework was taking shape.

Between those endpoints, the series traced the structural RAN1-RAN4 lag that
defines how 3GPP works. RAN1 designed the features. RAN4 specified the
conformance tests. The six-month gap between RAN1#122's 6G explosion and
RAN4#117's Rel-20 breakout was not a delay: it was the time required for
physical layer concepts to mature into RF requirements.

In the introduction to this series, I said I wanted to watch closely the
standardization work of the new 6G system, and to understand what happened in
the previous releases before that. These eleven meetings did that: from a single
Band 111 CR in Orlando to FS_6G_Radio consuming two-thirds of RAN1 in Dalian,
from Rel-19's final specification push to Rel-20's first draft CRs. Each post
tracked what topics dominated, which companies drove the discussion, and how
each meeting moved the work forward.

I will continue writing new posts as new meetings take place. RAN#112, RAN1#126,
RAN4#120, and every meeting through Rel-21 will add new chapters as they happen.
The work continues.
