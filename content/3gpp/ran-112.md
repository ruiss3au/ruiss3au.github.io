# RAN#112 - June 2026, Singapore

A freeze meeting does not look like much. By the time the Technical Specification Group for the Radio Access Network gathered in Singapore in June 2026, the arguing was over. The scope of Release 20 had been negotiated at RAN#110 in December, where the work items were proposed, and assembled at RAN#111 in March, where the feature packs were locked and the deferrals to Rel-21 were made. Singapore was the formality that followed: the plenary where Rel-20 freezes, where the answer is already known, and where the room mostly confirms what the working groups decided when there was still time to change it. The clearest evidence of how thin the meeting was sits in the document numbering itself. The secretariat reserved a long block of slots, RP-260848 through RP-260869, that were never filled. Two dozen placeholders for contributions that never came. That is what a feature-freeze plenary looks like from the inside: a calendar event with the substance already settled.

# The Shape of a Freeze Meeting

The agenda set by the RAN chair from Samsung framed Singapore as housekeeping ([RP-260830](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260830.zip)), opening with the draft report of the previous plenary in Fukuoka ([RP-260831](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260831.zip)) and the standing review of the 3GPP work plan ([RP-260845](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260845.zip)). A work plan review at a freeze plenary is not a planning exercise. It is a stocktake: which work items completed on schedule, which slipped, and what the released specification set actually contains. Around it sat the formal machinery of a plenary, the support team report ([RP-260843](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260843.zip)) and the ITU-R ad hoc status ([RP-260844](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260844.zip)). None of it moves a technical parameter. All of it closes a release.

# The Status Reports Carry the Weight

When a plenary has nothing to decide, the working group status reports become the main event, because they are how each group tells the plenary that its Rel-20 deliverables are on track to freeze. Every radio group reported in turn: RAN1 from CMCC ([RP-260832](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260832.zip)), RAN2 from InterDigital ([RP-260834](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260834.zip)), RAN3 from Nokia ([RP-260836](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260836.zip)), RAN4 from Apple ([RP-260838](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260838.zip)), and RAN5 from Motorola Mobility ([RP-260840](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260840.zip)). Each arrived paired with its list of change requests for approval, the routine batch of corrections that keeps a frozen specification honest.

The individual work item status reports tell the same story, that the active features are mid-flight rather than up for debate. ZTE and NEC, as rapporteurs, reported on AI and machine learning for NG-RAN Phase 3 ([RP-260899](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260899.zip)), where the work has moved to multi-hop UE trajectory prediction and AI-assisted intra-CU mobility under the lower-layer triggered framework, with baseline change requests now assigned to specific companies to draft. OPPO carried the status report for AI mobility in NR alongside a revised work item description ([RP-260892](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260892.zip), [RP-260893](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260893.zip)), and Meta reported on the core part of XR for NR Phase 4 ([RP-260888](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260888.zip)). On the satellite side, THALES brought the status report for the study on GNSS-resilient NR-NTN operation ([RP-260872](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260872.zip)), tracking the connected-mode solutions of open-loop timing pre-compensation and timing-advance control, the initial-access approach built on multiple PRACH transmissions, and the RAN4 analysis of the specification impacts. The companion liaison traffic was equally procedural: RAN3 replied to RAN2 on power-warning support for eMTC over NTN ([RP-260846](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260846.zip)), and RAN4 replied to the European regulator CEPT on active-antenna base station operation in bands below 1 GHz ([RP-260847](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260847.zip)). Liaison statements are the connective tissue of a release that is closing, not opening.

# 6G Requirements Get Their Last Polish

The one piece of genuine technical content was the final polish of the 6G scenarios and requirements study. CMCC arrived as rapporteur with TR 38.914 at version 0.4.1 ([RP-260870](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260870.zip)) and a proposed change carrying only editorials and cleanups ([RP-260902](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260902.zip)). The version jump from 0.4.0 to 0.4.1 says it all: the substance was settled in Fukuoka, and Singapore was for tidying. Ericsson contributed the finalization of the deployment scenarios ([RP-260878](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260878.zip)), the document that takes the last brackets out of the UE density values and pins down the geostationary satellite antenna characteristics for the S-band GEO case at a 30 metre reflector diameter and 55 dBi of gain. THALES fed the satellite parameters from the other direction with a text proposal on NTN deployment scenarios ([RP-260873](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260873.zip)), nailing down beam hopping with roughly 15 of 1000 beams active at once and the channel bandwidth assumed per band, plus companion proposals on NTN spectrum ([RP-260874](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260874.zip)) and services ([RP-260875](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260875.zip)). CMCC rounded out the 6G NTN scenarios ([RP-260901](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260901.zip)). Removing brackets is the unglamorous closing act of any study: it converts a document full of options into a document full of decisions.

# The Rel-21 Questions Arrive Early

Singapore could not freeze what it had not finished, and a cluster of contributions was already pointing past Rel-20 into the studies that open next. OPPO offered an interim assessment of the 6G study against the milestones the plenary had set for itself ([RP-260898](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260898.zip)), which is the polite way of asking which parts of 6G are genuinely ready and which are aspiration. The architecture question that has shadowed every recent plenary returned in CMCC's discussion of the 6G RAN architecture ([RP-260904](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260904.zip)), the service-based RAN proposal that would carry the 5G core's design principles, control and user plane separation, modularization, stateless network functions, and capability exposure, into the radio network itself. The migration debate sharpened too: Xiaomi proposed that 6G should exclude dual-stack, the non-standalone dual-connectivity variants, and the eLTE-style options from day one ([RP-260900](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260900.zip)), while CMCC weighed the coexistence of 6G and 4G ([RP-260906](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260906.zip)).

The device-and-spectrum questions surfaced the usual operator and vendor split. OPPO argued for diverse device types beyond the eMBB and IoT split, reaching toward XR wearables and AI glasses as distinct categories ([RP-260879](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260879.zip)). Deutsche Telekom brought the operator view on channel bandwidth for low-tier IoT ([RP-260886](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260886.zip)), opposing the narrowest alternative of a 10 or 20 MHz RF front end paired with a 5 MHz baseband and favouring a mandatory 20 MHz at both RF and baseband for full-duplex 6G IoT. T-Mobile staked out the early 6G security and privacy requirements ([RP-260880](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260880.zip)) and the RAN architecture aspects of integrated sensing data collection and fusion for 6G ([RP-260887](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260887.zip)). On satellite spectrum, AWS pressed for a new 16.5 MHz channel bandwidth in band n254 for LEO direct-to-device operation in S-band ([RP-260891](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260891.zip)), and THALES set out how the GNSS-resilience work splits across Rel-20 and Rel-21 ([RP-260876](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_112/Docs/RP-260876.zip)). None of this freezes in Rel-20. All of it is the agenda for what comes after.

# What Singapore Locked In

Read as a whole, RAN#112 was the meeting where Rel-20 stopped being negotiable. It did not resolve the service-based RAN architecture, the 6G migration options, the low-tier IoT bandwidth, or the satellite spectrum questions, because none of those belonged to Rel-20 any longer. They belong to Rel-21, and the contributions that raised them in Singapore were staking claims for studies that have not formally opened. What the plenary did was confirm: it accepted the working group status reports, approved the change request lists, took TR 38.914 to its tidied 0.4.1, and let the satellite antenna and beam parameters settle out of brackets. The drama lived at RAN#110 and RAN#111. Singapore was the receipt.

The series continues. RAN1 and RAN4 will keep meeting, Rel-21 studies will begin, and new posts will follow.
