# RAN4#113 - November 2024, Orlando, FL

Rel-20 didn't start at RAN4#113. You'd expect the first meeting of the blog series
to kick off the Release 20 narrative, but RAN4 was still deep in Rel-19 delivery
when it convened in Orlando. A search of the database turned up a single Rel-20
contribution: a draft CR to TS 36.141 introducing Band 111 [R4-2417580](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417580.zip), a
routine LTE band addition with no bearing on 6G or any of the technology shifts
discussed in the introduction. The rest of the meeting was entirely Rel-19 specification completion. This is not a bug in the data: Rel-20 was
still being scoped in RAN1 and the SA groups in November 2024. RAN4, as the
performance and conformance group, receives work after the feature definition
happens upstream. Its Rel-20 clock hadn't started ticking yet.

What makes RAN4#113 worth studying is that it captures RAN4 at the peak of its
Rel-19 effort. SBFD requirements were being finalized. AI/ML for the air
interface was raising foundational questions about conformance testing. RAN4 was
grappling with what it means to test a neural network. NTN Phase 3 was adding
Doppler compensation and satellite handover requirements. LP-WUS was splitting
the industry on mandatory versus optional wake-up receiver support. This meeting
shows what RAN4 looks like when it's delivering, not studying, and the
decisions made in Orlando shaped the Rel-19 specifications that operators would
deploy.

# SBFD: Ericsson and Nokia Take Control

Sub-band full duplex was the signature Rel-19 feature, and by RAN4#113 the
specification work was in full swing. The introduction described how Ericsson
and Nokia pivoted from opposing SBFD during the Rel-18 study phase to leading the
BS RF conformance framework once it became a work item. Orlando was where that
pivot produced concrete requirements.

## The Pivot

Nokia's contributions at this meeting were a textbook case of the strategy. A set
of contribution filings systematically addressed every category of BS RF
requirement for SBFD operation: existing transmit requirements
[R4-2418022](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418022.zip), existing receive requirements [R4-2418020](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418020.zip), potentially new
requirements [R4-2418023](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418023.zip), in-band blocking with simulation results
[R4-2418021](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418021.zip), and system parameters [R4-2418024](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418024.zip). The structure was
deliberate. Rather than proposing a single architecture and defending it, Nokia
laid out the complete conformance framework and let the group fill in the
parameters. The effect was to frame the debate: SBFD is happening, here is the
testing architecture, now let us argue about the numbers.

Samsung mirrored the same structured approach covering system
parameters [R4-2418097](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418097.zip), new requirements [R4-2418098](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418098.zip), existing TX
modifications [R4-2418099](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418099.zip), and existing RX modifications [R4-2418100](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418100.zip). CATT
filed five TDocs in the same pattern [R4-2417845](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417845.zip), [R4-2417852](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417852.zip), [R4-2417853](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417853.zip), [R4-2417854](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417854.zip). The meeting produced alignment on the framework, even though the
parameter values were still under debate. The framework itself was the victory:
once the conformance architecture is agreed, the feature is locked into the
release.

## BS RF Requirements: What Got Specified

The key technical challenge in SBFD BS RF is simultaneous transmission and
reception on adjacent sub-bands within the same carrier. A gNB transmitting at
200 W per antenna port creates self-interference that leaks into its own
receiver. The BS receiver must maintain sensitivity while its own transmitter
is active on an adjacent sub-band, a requirement that does not exist in
conventional TDD where transmission and reception are separated in time.

Nokia's simulation results [R4-2418021](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418021.zip) showed that in-band blocking for SBFD
requires an additional 15-20 dB of receiver filtering compared to conventional
TDD, depending on sub-band guard-band width and transmit power. The implication:
SBFD-capable base stations need redesigned RF front-ends with sharper analog
filters and more aggressive digital self-interference cancellation, adding cost
and power consumption to the gNB hardware. This was precisely what Ericsson and
Nokia had warned about during the study phase, and by leading the specification
they ensured the requirements reflected realistic hardware constraints rather
than optimistic assumptions.

vivo contributed to the sub-band configuration debate, proposing specific
guard-band widths between DL and UL sub-bands [R4-2418192](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418192.zip). LG Electronics
addressed the RRM implications: how does the UE measure serving and neighbor
cells when the gNB is operating in SBFD mode, where reference signal
configurations differ from conventional TDD [R4-2418106](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418106.zip)?

## UE-to-UE CLI: The Operator Problem

Cross-link interference between UEs became a separate work strand within SBFD.
When one UE transmits UL on a sub-band while a neighboring UE receives DL on an
adjacent sub-band, the transmitting UE can desense the receiving UE even if both
are correctly scheduled. This is not a gNB problem: it is a UE-to-UE
interference problem that neither the scheduler nor the gNB receiver can fully
resolve.

CATT raised the issue explicitly [R4-2417696](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417696.zip), noting that UE-to-UE CLI handling
requires new RRM measurement configurations. Nokia followed with RRM
requirements for CLI handling [R4-2418068](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418068.zip) and SBFD-specific RRM
[R4-2418069](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418069.zip). LG Electronics contributed additional RRM impact analysis
[R4-2418105](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418105.zip). The outcome: the group agreed that UE-to-UE CLI would require new
RRM measurement objects and reporting configurations. The actual measurement
accuracy targets, reporting periodicities, and the specific RRM parameters (SINR
thresholds, filtering coefficients, validity timers) were deferred. No CRs were
filed for SBFD in Orlando. The entire work item produced discussion papers only, a
pattern that held across all the headline Rel-19 features at this meeting.

A broader structural fact about this meeting: the major Rel-19 work items (SBFD, AI/ML,
LP-WUS, NTN Phase 3, Network Energy Savings) produced zero CRs. Every contribution
title began with "Discussion on" or "On the topic of." Meanwhile, mature work items that
had been in specification for multiple releases (HPUE carrier aggregation, NR demodulation
corrections, band additions, EESS protection) filed concrete CRs with spec text. The
distinction is exactly what you would expect: RAN4#113 was the framework-setting meeting
for the signature Rel-19 features. The CRs, the normative text, the actual spec
changes would come in later meetings, starting with RAN4#114 in Athens. At this meeting,
companies agreed on what the problems were and how to structure the solutions. The
numbers - the measurement accuracy targets, the in-band blocking limits, the reference
sensitivity values - those were left for Athens and beyond.

# AI/ML Conformance: Can You Test a Neural Network?

The Rel-19 AI/ML work item (NR_AIML_air-Core) was a RAN1-led effort, but RAN4
owned the conformance testing question. And the conformance question was
fundamentally different from anything RAN4 had handled before. For conventional
NR features, RAN4 defines pass/fail criteria: the UE must detect a PDCCH with 1%
BLER at a given reference sensitivity, the BS must meet ACLR limits at a given
output power. These are deterministic, repeatable, and testable with standard
equipment.

A neural network-based CSI compressor or beam predictor does not behave like a
fixed algorithm. The same model trained on different data produces different
outputs. Two vendors can implement the same architecture with different training
procedures and get different performance. RAN4's job is to ensure
interoperability across vendor implementations, and the AI/ML use cases
challenged all the group's established testing methodologies.

## The Testability Question

OPPO opened the testability debate with TDocs covering each AI/ML use case
systematically: CSI compression [R4-2417618](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417618.zip), beam management [R4-2417620](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417620.zip),
beam management RRM requirements [R4-2417619](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417619.zip), and positioning [R4-2417621](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417621.zip).
The central question across all four was the same: how do you define a
conformance test when the device under test contains a learned model whose
behavior depends on training data that the test lab cannot access?

CATT filed AI/ML TDocs [R4-2417700](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417700.zip) through [R4-2417706](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417706.zip), covering general
aspects, testability for each use case, and RRM core requirements for beam
management and positioning. MediaTek contributed on CSI compression
testability and two-sided model logistics [R4-2417803](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417803.zip), [R4-2417804](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417804.zip), [R4-2417805](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417805.zip)
plus beam management core requirements and testability
[R4-2417893](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417893.zip), [R4-2417894](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417894.zip). Xiaomi weighed in on general AI aspects and beam
management [R4-2417947](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417947.zip), [R4-2417948](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417948.zip), [R4-2417949](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417949.zip).

The consensus from this wave of contributions was that RAN4 could only define
test procedures, not test limits, for AI/ML features in Rel-19. The limits
would require large-scale simulation campaigns across multiple vendors, and the
Rel-19 timeline did not allow for it. The practical outcome: Rel-19 AI/ML
features would ship with conformance test procedures but without mandatory
performance requirements, a significant limitation that operators would later
push to close in Rel-20.

Anritsu contributed a practical FR2 test setup for AI/ML beam management
evaluation [R4-2417673](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417673.zip), demonstrating that the measurement infrastructure
exists even if the pass/fail criteria remain undefined.

## Two-Sided Model Logistics

MediaTek raised the two-sided model problem [R4-2417804](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417804.zip): if the UE encodes CSI
using a neural network and the gNB decodes it using a matching neural network,
the two models must be compatible. A UE trained with one loss function will
produce compressed CSI that a gNB trained with a different loss function cannot
reconstruct. This is the interoperability problem that Huawei and Nokia had
flagged in RAN1: without standardized model architectures, vendors cannot
guarantee interoperation.

OPPO and CATT echoed the concern, but the Rel-19 scope did not include model
architecture standardization. The RAN1 debate described in the introduction,
between standardized architectures (Huawei, Nokia) and proprietary models with
standardized interfaces (Ericsson, Qualcomm, Apple), was unresolved. RAN4's
position in Orlando was clear: until RAN1 resolves the architecture question,
RAN4 cannot define meaningful conformance requirements. The AI/ML conformance
work was effectively paused, waiting for RAN1 direction. Like SBFD, AI/ML
produced only discussion papers in Orlando, no CRs. The concrete outcome was the
testability framework: RAN4 agreed that AI/ML conformance would require (a) defined
test procedures for each use case, (b) vendor-declared performance levels rather than
mandatory pass/fail thresholds, and (c) standardized test datasets for model
evaluation, though the datasets themselves were not agreed. Everything else was
deferred to Rel-20.

# NTN Phase 3: Doppler, Timing, and Resilience

Non-terrestrial network work at RAN4#113 focused on Phase 3 enhancements to the
NR NTN specifications. Three threads dominated: RedCap NTN for narrowband
satellite IoT, FR2-NTN for mmWave satellite links, and Doppler compensation for
LEO constellations.

## RedCap NTN: Narrowband Satellites

RedCap (reduced capability) UEs are designed for IoT and wearable applications
with fewer antennas, narrower bandwidth, and lower power consumption than
full-spec NR devices. Applying RedCap to NTN creates a device class that can
connect to satellites with the complexity of a terrestrial IoT modem, enabling
direct-to-satellite IoT at scale.

CATT led the RedCap NTN contributions with RRM requirements for both RedCap and
non-RedCap NTN Phase 3 scenarios [R4-2417720](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417720.zip), [R4-2417721](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417721.zip). Apple contributed
the RedCap HD-FDD reference sensitivity analysis [R4-2417765](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417765.zip), proposing
REF SENS targets for half-duplex FDD RedCap devices over satellite links.
Xiaomi filed additional RedCap NTN RRM requirements [R4-2417945](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417945.zip), [R4-2417946](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417946.zip),
filling in the measurement configurations.

The RedCap NTN discussion revealed a fundamental tension: satellite operators
wanted RedCap devices with satellite capability to be as cheap and simple as
terrestrial RedCap devices, but the Doppler pre-compensation, timing advance
management, and higher path loss of satellite links required additional
processing that pushed against the RedCap design goals. Airbus contributed
corrections to UE transmit timing for satellite access [R4-2417512](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417512.zip), a subtle
but critical element: the UE must adjust its transmit timing to account for the
propagation delay to a satellite moving at 7.5 km/s, and RedCap devices with
simplified timing circuits struggle with the required accuracy.

## FR2-NTN: mmWave from Orbit

FR2-NTN, operating satellite links at mmWave frequencies, entered specification
work at this meeting. CATT proposed the FR2-NTN frequency range definition
[R4-2417834](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417834.zip) and drafted a liaison statement to other groups on the range
boundaries [R4-2417835](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417835.zip), paired with CRs to TS 38.101-5 [R4-2417836](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417836.zip) and TS
38.108 [R4-2417837](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417837.zip) to add the FR2-NTN frequency range to the respective
specifications. Apple contributed a position paper on FR2-NTN inclusion
[R4-2417766](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417766.zip), advocating for a conservative scope limited to Ka-band downlink
for initial deployments.

The FR2-NTN discussion was tied to the broader WRC-27 agenda: the ITU World
Radiocommunication Conference in 2027 will allocate new mmWave bands for
satellite services, and 3GPP needs FR2-NTN specifications ready to claim those
bands for IMT. The timeline was aggressive: RAN4 needed to complete FR2-NTN
conformance specifications by mid-2025 to feed into the ITU process. Orlando was
the starting gun for that work.

Doppler emerged as a thread across multiple NTN TDocs. HiSilicon proposed
solutions for Doppler shift issues in guard band and transmission bandwidth
configuration [R4-2419057](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2419057.zip), addressing the problem that Doppler shift at
Ka-band (up to 480 kHz for a LEO satellite at 30 GHz) can push the received
signal outside the configured bandwidth, causing demodulation failures. The
proposed solution added guard band margins that scale with the satellite's
Doppler profile, trading spectral efficiency for link reliability. MediaTek
contributed RRM requirements for the Phase 3 scenarios [R4-2417627](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417627.zip), and CATT
filed system parameters for Ka-band co-existence with CRs to TR 38.863
[R4-2417840](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417840.zip), [R4-2417841](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417841.zip). Apple pushed back on FR2-NTN inclusion
[R4-2417766](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417766.zip), specifically on adding FR2-NTN band definitions to the main NR
specifications, arguing that the channel models and propagation characteristics
above 24 GHz for satellite links were insufficiently validated. Apple's
position was that FR2-NTN should remain a study item until the WRC-27 band
allocations were confirmed.

# LP-WUS: The Mandate Fight

Low-power wake-up signal work at RAN4#113 split into two camps: BS RF
requirements driven by Nokia, and UE RF requirements where Apple pushed back on
mandatory support. The fight described in the introduction, between
infrastructure vendors wanting every UE to support LP-WUR for network efficiency
and device vendors wanting to segment the market, played out concretely in the
Orlando contributions.

## BS RF for Wake-Up Signals

Nokia opened the BS RF discussion [R4-2417543](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417543.zip), proposing transmit power,
ACLR, and EVM requirements for the LP-WUS signal from the gNB perspective. The
LP-WUS waveform is deliberately simple (OOK modulation) compared to standard NR
signals, which means the BS transmitter must support a second modulation scheme
alongside its normal NR transmissions. The coexistence question was whether LP-WUS
transmission degrades the normal NR signal on adjacent resources, and Nokia's
proposal set conservative limits to guarantee backward compatibility with
existing NR UEs.

HiSilicon filed a comprehensive set of five LP-WUS TDocs covering general
aspects [R4-2417885](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417885.zip), UE reference sensitivity and ACS [R4-2417886](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417886.zip), other
Rx requirements [R4-2417887](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417887.zip), testability [R4-2417888](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417888.zip), and BS RF
[R4-2417889](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417889.zip). CATT added system parameter proposals with draft CRs to TS
38.101-1 and TS 38.104 [R4-2417862](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417862.zip), [R4-2417863](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417863.zip), plus TR 38.774 updates for
system parameters and simulation assumptions [R4-2417867](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417867.zip), [R4-2417868](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417868.zip).

## UE Requirements: Apple Pushes Back

Apple's position [R4-2417759](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417759.zip) was the most consequential in this session. The
contribution addressed LP-WUR noise figure and reference sensitivity, proposing
values that would make LP-WUR feasible in low-cost device tiers: a noise figure
of 9 dB for the wake-up receiver, roughly 6 dB worse than the main NR receiver,
and a reference sensitivity of -97 dBm at 10 MHz bandwidth. Apple's argument was
that requiring the same sensitivity as the main receiver defeats the purpose of
a low-power wake-up radio: the WUR is supposed to be simple and cheap, and
mandating high-end RF performance would make the cost savings illusory.

The subtext was the mandate fight. If LP-WUR sensitivity requirements are set
at main-receiver levels, only premium devices can support it, and the feature
becomes optional by default. If set at relaxed levels, all device tiers can
support it, and the infrastructure vendors win the mandate argument. Apple's
position was calibrated to keep LP-WUR optional for low-end devices while
maintaining coverage parity for premium tiers. CATT filed further RF
requirement discussions [R4-2417864](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417864.zip), [R4-2417865](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417865.zip) and testability analysis
[R4-2417866](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417866.zip), supporting the tiered approach. Xiaomi contributed simulation
results [R4-2417942](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417942.zip) and RRM requirements [R4-2417943](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417943.zip). LG Electronics added
RRM core requirements [R4-2418107](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418107.zip).

The mandate question was not resolved in Orlando. It would return at subsequent
meetings, and the compromise described in the introduction, mandatory for
premium devices, optional for reduced-capability, was still months away.

# Network Energy Savings: RRM Meets Power Budgets

Network energy saving work at this meeting focused on RRM requirements for the
on-demand SSB and signal adaptation features described in the introduction.
Operators had set aggressive power reduction targets (30% at medium load, 50% at
low load), and RAN4 needed to define the measurement and reporting framework
that would allow gNBs to enter energy-saving modes without degrading UE
performance.

Nokia led with RRM requirements for on-demand SSB SCell operation
[R4-2418067](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418067.zip), proposing measurement configurations that allow a UE to maintain
RRM measurements on a carrier while the SCell SSB is transmitted only on demand
rather than continuously. MediaTek contributed two TDocs on OD-SSB and signal
adaptation RRM [R4-2417625](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417625.zip), [R4-2417626](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417626.zip). CATT filed corresponding requirements
[R4-2417726](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417726.zip), [R4-2417727](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417727.zip). CMCC, as the operator driving the energy efficiency
KPIs, contributed RRM requirement discussions for both OD-SSB [R4-2418430](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418430.zip) and
signal adaptation [R4-2418431](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418431.zip). China Telecom added OD-SSB SCell and signal
adaptation analysis [R4-2418471](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418471.zip), [R4-2418472](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418472.zip). NTT DOCOMO and Apple rounded out
the discussion [R4-2418297](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418297.zip), [R4-2418585](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418585.zip).

When the UE is measuring a cell that enters energy-saving mode, the challenge
is that the reference signals the UE relies on for measurement (SSB, CSI-RS) may
not be transmitted continuously. The UE must distinguish between a cell that is
in energy-saving mode (no SSB transmitted, so no measurement possible) and a
cell with poor coverage (SSB transmitted but weak). The RRM requirements defined
in Orlando introduced measurement validity timers and fallback procedures:
if a UE detects that a cell's SSB is absent, it declares the cell in
energy-saving mode and extends the measurement period, rather than immediately
reporting a radio link failure. This small specification change was critical to
making network-level energy savings deployable without triggering false
mobility events.

# 7-24 GHz: Early Signals from WRC-27 Preparation

The frequency range that would later dominate Rel-20 and 6G discussions made an
early appearance at this meeting through the FS_NR_IMT_4400_7125_14800MHz study
item. This study evaluated IMT parameters for three frequency ranges in
preparation for WRC-27: 4400-4800 MHz, 7125-8400 MHz, and 14800-15350 MHz. The
14.8-15.35 GHz range was the most interesting: it falls squarely in the 7-24
GHz range and was not previously studied for IMT.

Ericsson drove the study with a TR 38.922 draft on IMT parameters for all three
ranges [R4-2419346](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2419346.zip) and a contribution on co-existence spurious emission
requirements for bands in the upper FR1 region [R4-2418396](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2418396.zip), addressing
the practical question of whether existing BS RF requirements for spurious
emissions would protect the new bands. The answer was no: existing FR1 spurious
emission limits were defined to protect adjacent bands below 6 GHz, and the
14.8-15.35 GHz range requires tighter limits because it sits close to
radio-astronomy and passive sensing allocations.

Nokia contributed revisions to the TR 38.922 system-level simulation assumptions
for the 14.8-15.35 GHz range [R4-2417538](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417538.zip), proposing propagation models,
deployment scenarios, and interference assessment methodologies. Ericsson also
filed a liaison statement reply on 14.8-15.35 GHz parameters for sharing and
compatibility studies [R4-2420385](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2420385.zip), coordinating with the ITU-R working parties
that were preparing the WRC-27 agenda.

This work was procedural rather than contentious, but it laid the groundwork for
the 6G channel model studies that would become the dominant RAN4 topic a year
later. The propagation models and co-existence studies produced in this work
item were the first time RAN4 systematically evaluated spectrum above 7 GHz for
terrestrial IMT, and the assumptions made here would influence the 6G study item
scope.

# EN-DC RF Phase 4: 6Rx and HPUE

EN-DC RF Phase 4 continued the practical work of enabling multi-band NR
deployments. Two threads stood out: 6-receive-antenna (6Rx) support for 6 MIMO
layers, and high-power UE (HPUE) for inter-band carrier aggregation.

Nokia opened the 6Rx discussion [R4-2417559](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417559.zip), proposing to support 6 MIMO
layers on UEs with 6 receive antennas. The technical challenge was SRS antenna
switching: to sound up to 6 receive antennas with 2 or 4 transmit chains, the UE
must switch its transmit path across the receive antennas in a defined pattern.
Apple contributed the delta_TRXSRS values for SRS antenna switching
[R4-2417754](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417754.zip) and views on insertion loss imbalance solutions for 6Rx
[R4-2417755](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417755.zip). MediaTek provided feasibility evaluation of MIMO layer support
for 6Rx UEs [R4-2417561](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417561.zip). China Telecom weighed in on 6-layer performance
evaluation [R4-2417799](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417799.zip).

HPUE work addressed the intermodulation challenge of running two UL carriers at
power class 1.5 or 2 simultaneously. When a UE transmits on two bands, the
intermodulation products can desense its own receiver, and the maximum power
reduction (MPR) values must account for this. Apple contributed three HPUE
TDocs: SAR compliance for HPUE CA/DC [R4-2417761](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417761.zip), maximum sensitivity
degradation considerations [R4-2417762](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417762.zip), and intra-band non-contiguous UL CA
MPR [R4-2417763](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417763.zip), plus inter-band UL CA/DC HPUE [R4-2417764](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417764.zip). Murata
contributed HPUE MSD rules [R4-2417784](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417784.zip), and Skyworks proposed simplifications
to the 2UL IMD MSD framework [R4-2417793](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417793.zip), [R4-2417794](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417794.zip). CATT added power
domain enhancements [R4-2417882](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417882.zip) and MPR applicability for FR1 and FR2
[R4-2417883](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417883.zip), [R4-2417884](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_113/Docs/R4-2417884.zip).

The 6Rx and HPUE work was procedural specification completion, not contentious
debate. The parameters had been largely agreed in earlier meetings, and
Orlando was about CR finalization. But the sheer volume of ENDC RF contributions
showed how much specification detail a single RF feature requires in RAN4.

# Company Dynamics: The Chinese Ecosystem

The company distribution at RAN4#113 revealed a structural shift in 3GPP
participation. Ericsson and HiSilicon dominated the contribution volume, running
roughly even at the top. Nokia placed third with a significant gap behind the
leaders. But the aggregate Chinese presence was overwhelming: HiSilicon,
Sanechips, CATT, vivo, OPPO, CMCC, China Telecom, and Xiaomi combined accounted
for the majority of all contributions, collectively exceeding the combined
output of Ericsson, Nokia, Apple, Qualcomm, and Samsung.

Sanechips in particular was notable. A HiSilicon affiliate focused on UE
chipset implementation, Sanechips filed heavily on the detailed RF parameter
specifications that define how a UE chipset implements carrier aggregation, MPR,
and HPUE. This is the practical engineering layer where specifications become
silicon, and the volume of Sanechips contributions reflected the scale of
Chinese chipset investment in 5G.

CATT, the Chinese Academy of Telecommunications Technology, was the most broadly
distributed contributor, filing across almost every Rel-19 work item: SBFD,
AI/ML, NTN, LP-WUS, network energy savings, EN-DC RF. CATT's pattern was to
contribute to every topic rather than dominating any single one, a strategy of
broad participation that ensured Chinese positions were represented across the
entire specification scope.

Apple's contributions were overwhelmingly focused on UE RF requirements,
reflecting Apple's role as the primary device-side voice in RAN4. Apple
contributed to EN-DC RF, LP-WUS, NTN, and HPUE, always from the UE perspective,
always pushing for practical feasibility over aggressive performance targets.

The notable absence: Qualcomm was underrepresented compared to its RAN1
presence. Qualcomm's strength is in the PHY/MAC and higher-layer specification
work that happens in RAN1; RAN4 performance and conformance work tends to be
driven by infrastructure vendors and UE chipset companies.

# Looking Ahead to RAN4#114

RAN4#113 was the last meeting where the group's entire energy was directed at
Rel-19. The single Rel-20 contribution, a Band 111 CR, was a placeholder for a
transition that had not yet arrived. But the work done in Orlando was not a
sideshow to the Rel-20 narrative: it was the foundation. The SBFD BS RF
requirements, the NTN Doppler compensation framework, the LP-WUS mandate
structure, and the AI/ML testability approach defined here would carry forward
into Rel-20 as the baseline from which 6G features would be specified.

The next meeting, RAN4#114 in Athens, would begin the Rel-20 transition. The
FS_6G_Radio study item would appear for the first time in RAN4 contributions.
SBFD requirements would move from framework definition to parameter
finalization. The AI/ML architecture debate would intensify as Rel-20 scoping
decisions approached. The Orlando meeting closed Rel-19's peak effort. Athens
would open a new chapter.
