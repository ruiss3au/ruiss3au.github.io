Athens was not the start of the Rel-20 work. It was the meeting where RAN4 began
to talk about starting. Four Rel-20 TDocs appeared, not one of them a technical
contribution. They were positioning papers: Apple proposing RRM Phase 6
[R4-2500242](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500242.zip), Intel offering views on 5G-Advanced topics [R4-2501904](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501904.zip), MediaTek
laying out its RAN4 Rel-20 priorities [R4-2501625](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501625.zip), and BT proposing a way
forward on 200 MHz channel bandwidth [R4-2500214](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500214.zip). Companies were staking claims
on what RAN4 should work on before anyone had decided what RAN4 would work on.

The rest of the meeting was still overwhelmingly Rel-19, but the character had
shifted from Orlando. Where the flagship Rel-19 features at RAN4#113 had only
discussion papers establishing frameworks (the mature work items like HPUE and
demodulation corrections already had CRs), Athens saw the first draft CRs appear
for the headline Rel-19 features. LP-WUS got its first UE RF requirements CR.
SBFD moved from "should we add RRM measurements?" to specific SRS-RSRP
simulation results. Five new work items debuted, none of which existed in
November. The transition was not in volume but in direction: the group was
beginning to point toward Rel-20.

## The Rel-20 Scoping Papers

The four Rel-20 contributions in Athens were not about solving technical
problems. They were about defining the problems. This is the pre-standardization
phase where companies compete to frame the work.

### Apple's RRM Phase 6 Pitch

Apple's motivation paper for Rel-20 RRM Phase 6 [R4-2500242](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500242.zip) was the most
consequential of the four. RRM (Radio Resource Management) is the set of
measurement and reporting procedures that govern mobility, and RRM Phase 5 was
already delivering fast SCell activation and FR2-1 measurement optimizations for
Rel-19. Apple's pitch for Phase 6 framed Rel-20 RRM around three axes:
AI/ML-assisted mobility decisions, multi-TRP measurement frameworks for
distributed MIMO, and measurement gap enhancements for the 7-24 GHz range where
beam sweeping overhead becomes the dominant performance bottleneck.

The subtext was competitive positioning. Apple drives RRM specifications because
RRM directly affects the user experience of its devices. By framing Rel-20 RRM
early, Apple ensured that its device priorities would be baked into the work
item scope before infrastructure vendors could define it around gNB-centric
KPIs. MediaTek's views paper [R4-2501625](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501625.zip) aligned broadly with Apple's framing
while adding emphasis on power consumption metrics. Intel's paper
[R4-2501904](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501904.zip) supported the multi-TRP measurement direction.

### 200 MHz Bandwidth: The 7-24 GHz Enabler

BT's way forward paper on 200 MHz channel bandwidth [R4-2500214](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500214.zip) addressed a
specific technical gap. NR Release 15 through 19 supports maximum channel
bandwidths of 100 MHz for FR1 (below 7 GHz) and 400 MHz for FR2 (above 24 GHz).
The 7-24 GHz range sits between these two regimes: carrier bandwidths of 200 MHz
are a natural fit for the wider spectrum blocks available in this range, but no
NR specification currently supports it. BT argued that 200 MHz bandwidth is a
prerequisite for 6G in the 7-24 GHz range and should be a Rel-20 RAN4 work item.

## LP-WUS: The First Big CR

The low-power wake-up signal work item crossed a threshold in Athens. Orlando
had produced only system parameter draft CRs from CATT [R4-2417862](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2417862.zip), [R4-2417863](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2417863.zip),
adding LP-WUS entries to the specification tables without defining behavioral
requirements. In Athens, vivo submitted the first UE RF requirements CR.

### vivo Submits Draft Specification Text

vivo filed TR 38.774 v0.2.0 [R4-2500753](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500753.zip), the technical report consolidating
LP-WUS physical layer parameters, system parameters, and evaluation assumptions.
Alongside the TR update, vivo submitted a "Draft Big CR for LP-WUS UE RF
requirements in Rel-19" [R4-2500754](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500754.zip), the first attempt at UE RF requirement
text for LP-WUS.

HiSilicon continued its comprehensive approach from Orlando with five updated
TDocs [R4-2500157](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500157.zip) through [R4-2500161](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500161.zip). Nokia updated its BS RF
proposal [R4-2500075](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500075.zip), and Sanechips filed a full set of LP-WUS contributions
[R4-2500550](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500550.zip) through [R4-2500556](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500556.zip). Samsung entered the LP-WUS
discussion for the first time with UE RF requirements and testability
[R4-2500503](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500503.zip), [R4-2500504](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500504.zip).

### Apple Proposes Simulation Collection Framework

Apple's LP-WUS contributions in Athens were the most structurally significant.
Beyond the ongoing Rx requirements work [R4-2500307](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500307.zip) and RRM core requirements
[R4-2500238](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500238.zip), Apple submitted simulation assumptions and results for LP-WUR
[R4-2500276](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500276.zip) and, critically, a "Proposal for Simulation Results Collection for
LP-SS Performance" [R4-2500277](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500277.zip). The proposal established a formal process for
vendors to submit standardized simulation results for wake-up signal
performance. Once a simulation framework is agreed, every vendor must submit
results to the same format, making direct comparison possible.

Apple's position [R4-2507759](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2507759.zip), originally filed at RAN4#113 Orlando and cited here for its influence on the Athens debate, proposed wake-up
receiver noise figure of 9 dB, roughly 6 dB worse than the main NR receiver,
and reference sensitivity of -97 dBm at 10 MHz bandwidth. The mandate fight was
not resolved in Athens.

## SBFD: From Framework to Numbers

SBFD work in Athens shifted decisively from "what should we specify?" to "what
should the numbers be?"

### Nokia's SRS-RSRP Simulations

Nokia filed simulation results for L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP measurement
[R4-2500468](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500468.zip), the first quantitative performance data for UE-to-UE cross-link
interference measurement in SBFD. Nokia's results showed that L1-CLI-SRS-RSRP
measurement accuracy within plus or minus 3 dB is achievable with SRS
transmission periodicities of 10 ms or less. Nokia also updated its RRM
requirements for CLI handling [R4-2500466](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500466.zip) and SBFD
operation [R4-2500467](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500467.zip), now with specific measurement periods and accuracy
targets rather than the framework descriptions from Orlando.

## Ku-Band NTN: A New Spectrum Frontier

The appearance of a Ku-band NTN work item (NR_NTN_Ku_bands-Core) in Athens was a
surprise. Ku-band spans 12 to 18 GHz, well above the current NR NTN frequency
range which tops out at Ka-band (20-30 GHz) for the forward link. Ku-band is
interesting for NTN because it sits in a sweet spot: lower atmospheric
attenuation than Ka-band, wider available bandwidth than S-band, and existing
satellite infrastructure.

### System Parameters and Coexistence

Sanechips led the Ku-band charge with five initial TDocs covering coexistence
studies based on ITU regulations [R4-2500607](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500607.zip), system parameters
[R4-2500608](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500608.zip), UE RF requirements [R4-2500609](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500609.zip), SAN (satellite access node) RF
requirements [R4-2500610](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500610.zip), and a general discussion paper
[R4-2501175](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501175.zip).

Ericsson contributed a full set of five Ku-band TDocs: WI updates impact
[R4-2501354](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501354.zip), system parameters [R4-2501355](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501355.zip), UE RF requirements
[R4-2501356](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501356.zip), SAN requirements [R4-2501357](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501357.zip), a draft CR to TS 38.101-5 for
VSAT support [R4-2501358](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501358.zip), and coexistence simulation assumptions
[R4-2501398](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501398.zip). Ericsson's draft CR was the first concrete specification text for

## AI/ML Mobility: A Less Contentious Frontier

The AI/ML for mobility study item (FS_NR_AIML_Mob) debuted in Athens with
a notably different tone from the air interface AI/ML work that had split the
industry in Rel-19. The mobility AI/ML discussion was practical and
uncontroversial: can AI/ML improve handover decisions without requiring
standardized model architectures?

### Apple's Trio of Opening Contributions

Apple opened the mobility AI/ML discussion with three TDocs that framed the
entire work item. The first addressed testability and interoperability
[R4-2500231](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500231.zip), the second covered general aspects
[R4-2500232](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500232.zip), and the third studied impacts on RAN4 requirements
[R4-2500233](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500233.zip). A mobility prediction model can be tested against recorded
drive-test data: the model predicts a handover target, the test equipment
verifies whether the prediction matches the actual best cell. Apple's framing
emphasized this simplicity to argue that mobility AI/ML should not be held to
the same standardization requirements as air interface AI/ML.

### Broad Buy-In Across Companies

Every contributing company filed supporting papers. CMCC contributed general
aspects, requirement impacts, and testability [R4-2501099](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501099.zip) through
[R4-2501101](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501101.zip). NTT DOCOMO discussed requirement impacts
[R4-2500594](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500594.zip). Sanechips addressed requirements and testability
[R4-2500696](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500696.zip), [R4-2500697](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500697.zip). MediaTek contributed RRM impact and testability
[R4-2500890](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500890.zip), [R4-2500891](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500891.zip). Xiaomi filed requirement impacts and testability
[R4-2500619](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500619.zip), [R4-2500628](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500628.zip). CATT rounded out the discussion with RRM impacts
and testability [R4-2501040](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501040.zip), [R4-2501041](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2501041.zip). No one argued against studying AI/ML
mobility. This consensus was a preview of how AI/ML would likely be handled in
Rel-20: proprietary UE models for mobility, standardized interfaces, no
architecture mandates.

## Air-to-Ground Gets Serious

Air-to-ground communications (NR_ATG_enh-Core) provide cellular connectivity to
aircraft, enabling in-flight broadband without satellite links.

### CMCC Pushes MIMO and CA for ATG

CMCC was the primary driver of ATG enhancements at this meeting. The BS RF
requirements for ATG with DL MIMO [R4-2500858](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500858.zip) proposed 2x2 and 4x4 MIMO
configurations for ATG base stations. The UE RF requirements for ATG with
UL-MIMO [R4-2500859](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500859.zip) addressed the aircraft terminal side.

HiSilicon filed BS RF requirements for ATG enhancements [R4-2500783](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_114/Docs/R4-2500783.zip) with a

## Spatial Channel Modeling: The Hidden Foundation

## Company Dynamics: HiSilicon Takes the Lead

HiSilicon took the top position, narrowly ahead of Ericsson. Nokia held steady
at third. The most significant change was Apple's rise into the fourth position,
driven by the Rel-20 scoping papers and comprehensive IoT NTN testing
contributions. Qualcomm rose noticeably, doubling its output from Orlando.

The Chinese ecosystem remained dominant in aggregate: HiSilicon, Sanechips,
CATT, vivo, CMCC, OPPO, and Xiaomi together accounted for roughly half of all
contributions. Sanechips maintained its position as the leading UE chipset
contributor.

## Looking Ahead to RAN4#115

RAN4#114 was the meeting where Rel-20 stopped being an abstract concept and
became a set of specific proposals. The four scoping papers established the
initial boundaries of what RAN4 would work on in Rel-20. The five new work items
showed where the growth was happening. But Rel-20 still represented less than
one percent of the contributions. The question for the next meeting, RAN4#115 in
Wuhan, was whether Rel-20 would move from proposals to action.
