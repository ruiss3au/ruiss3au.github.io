A plenary is where the working groups stop arguing and the schedule starts deciding. By the time the Technical Specification Group for the Radio Access Network gathered in Fukuoka in March 2026, the calendar had become the most powerful participant in the room. Release 20 freezes at the June plenary, RAN#111's successor, and Fukuoka was the last full meeting with enough runway to change the answer. Everything brought to Japan was really one question wearing different costumes: does this make the Rel-20 cut, or does it slip to Rel-21? The feature packs were assembled here. Work items that arrived with momentum and a rapporteur willing to defend a timeline got blessed. Work items that arrived with open scope and a coordination problem got a polite nudge toward the next release. This is the story of what Fukuoka kept and what it let go.

## The Plenary as a Deadline Machine

The Rel-21 timeline was on the table in its own right, carried jointly by the RAN, SA, and CT chairs ([RP-260039](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260039.zip)), and that document framed the whole meeting. Once a group knows when the next release opens, it knows the cost of deferral, and deferral stops feeling like failure and starts feeling like triage. Ericsson made the point explicit with its own views on the Rel-21 timeline ([RP-260608](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260608.zip)). Fukuoka was less about technique than about which features had earned a seat in Rel-20 before the door closed in June.

## 6G Requirements: TR 38.914 Hardens

The gravitational center, as in Baltimore, was the 6G scenarios and requirements study captured in TR 38.914. CMCC arrived as rapporteur with the report at version 0.4.0 ([RP-260073](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260073.zip)) and the matching status report on the study's progress ([RP-260074](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260074.zip)). The substantive work was in the text proposals: CMCC pressed the report toward a settled set of deployment scenarios ([RP-260075](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260075.zip)) and a firmer table of key performance indicators ([RP-260076](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260076.zip)). Ericsson contributed its own view on the deployment scenarios ([RP-260041](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260041.zip)), and the deployment-scenario thread was consolidated through a moderator's proposed change to the report ([RP-260725](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260725.zip)). The significance is in the word "settled." A report at v0.4.0 with its scenario brackets coming out is no longer a wish list. It is the frame every later 6G work item will have to fit inside, and Fukuoka spent its requirements energy removing the optionality that earlier versions had carried.

## Service-Based RAN: The Architecture Bet Returns

The most consequential disagreement was again structural, and again CMCC was driving. Its contribution argued that the 6G RAN should adopt a service-based design, extending the service-based architecture principles of the 5G core, control and user plane separation, modularized network functions, stateless design, and API-based interactions, into the radio network itself ([RP-260089](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260089.zip)). CMCC paired this with broader views on how the 6G RAN architecture should be organized ([RP-260081](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260081.zip)). The bet is the same one it placed in Baltimore: trade interface machinery for cloud-friendly modularity. The plenary did not declare a winner, and it was never going to. What it did was keep the service-based vision inside the study scope, which guarantees the integrated-gNB-versus-service-based question reopens, in sharper form, once the working groups start drafting interfaces.

## Bands and Power Classes: The Great Consolidation

The least glamorous and most quietly important decision was a piece of housekeeping. Nokia brought a new basket work item, NR_Bands_BW_PC_BC_RedCap_R20, to fold together a sprawl of related additions: new channel bandwidths for existing NR bands, higher-power-UE operation in the lower frequency range, and power class 2 support for reduced-capability devices ([RP-260066](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260066.zip)). A parallel consolidation, led by Ericsson as rapporteur, merged several separate carrier aggregation, dual connectivity, and supplementary uplink band-combination efforts, including high-power-UE support, into one revised work item ([RP-260290](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260290.zip)). CATT pushed on the edges, arguing for high-power-UE support across band combinations with three or more downlink bands ([RP-260691](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260691.zip)) and weighing in on the smallest maximum UE bandwidth that should be specified ([RP-260360](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260360.zip)). Basket work items are how a standards body keeps a long tail of band-by-band requests from drowning the agenda. Approving them is the unglamorous prerequisite for any operator that wants its specific band, bandwidth, and power class supported without sponsoring a standalone work item for each.

## ISAC: A Study Reaches v1.0.0 and a Use-Case Fight Opens

Integrated sensing and communication had two distinct lives in Fukuoka. The first was the NR sensing study, where China Telecom, as co-rapporteur with Xiaomi, brought TR 38.765 to version 1.0.0 ([RP-260258](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260258.zip)) alongside the status report tracking its progress across the radio working groups ([RP-260257](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260257.zip)). A report at v1.0.0 is a study declaring itself substantially complete: the gNB-based sensing scenarios, the uncrewed-aerial-vehicle detection cases, and the evaluation results contributed by a wide span of companies have been folded into stable text.

The second life was the 6G ISAC requirements debate, and that one was contested. Xiaomi argued the 6G study should not try to swallow all of the sensing use cases inherited from the service requirements groups, and proposed focusing on a selected subset of object-detection and tracking cases ([RP-260260](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260260.zip)), with a companion paper on the deployment scenarios that should anchor them ([RP-260259](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260259.zip)). Huawei and HiSilicon pushed in a sharper direction still, proposing text that would remove the human-detection requirements from the sensing scope ([RP-260330](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260330.zip)). The fight over which use cases survive is the fight over how much sensing 6G actually commits to, and Fukuoka left it unresolved on purpose.

## 6G Security: The Cross-Group Clock

Not every problem in Fukuoka was a feature. One was a clock. RAN2 sent a liaison statement to SA3 on access-stratum security aspects for the 6G Radio study ([RP-260037](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260037.zip)), which is the polite, formal way of saying the radio side cannot finish designing lower-layer security and the protection of broadcast system information without the security group, and the security group is not going to conclude in time. SA3's work is not expected to land until the middle of 2026, which sits awkwardly against the Rel-20 freeze and the Rel-21 timeline the chairs had just circulated ([RP-260039](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260039.zip)). The 6G Radio study itself was carried forward in revised form by Ericsson ([RP-260282](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260282.zip)), but a study item description cannot manufacture time in a sister group. Security is the kind of dependency that, left unmanaged, produces either a gap or two incompatible mechanisms. Fukuoka acknowledged the seam without being able to close it, which means the security framework is a leading candidate for the work that gets shaped in Rel-20 but only fully specified later.

## Ambient IoT Phase 2: Outdoors and Active

Ambient IoT, the ultra-low-power and batteryless device track, moved toward its harder phase. Huawei and HiSilicon, as rapporteur, brought the revised work item description for Phase 2 of the Ambient IoT solutions in the radio ([RP-260222](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260222.zip)), backed by the status report confirming the work item's trajectory ([RP-260221](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260221.zip)). The ambition is visible in the device taxonomy: Phase 2 reaches toward Device 2b and Device C, the variants with active amplification rather than pure backscatter, and toward a topology in which an intermediate UE acts as the reader, plus outdoor deployment and a form of device localization that does not lean on a dedicated positioning architecture. The companion study item, carried by LG, frames exactly that outdoor-and-active extension of the indoor-focused Rel-19 baseline ([RP-260607](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260607.zip)). Ericsson contributed its views on scoping the outdoor active-device work ([RP-260501](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260501.zip)). Active outdoor operation is a real link-budget and reliability ask, and keeping it inside Rel-20 commits the working groups to the difficult range numbers rather than the easy ones.

## NR Mobility Phase 5: Fixing What LTM Left Behind

Apple arrived as rapporteur with a revised work item for NR mobility enhancements Phase 5 ([RP-260232](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260232.zip)) and a discussion paper laying out why the revision was needed ([RP-260231](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260231.zip)), with RAN2 carrying the matching status report ([RP-260230](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260230.zip)). The target is the lower-layer triggered mobility framework inherited from Rel-18 and Rel-19, which leans on heavy pre-configuration. Phase 5 reaches for secondary-cell activation improvements under triggered mobility and for dynamic configuration of L1 measurements signalled through a MAC control element, trading static configuration for something the network can steer in flight. This is incremental, but it is the kind of increment that decides whether triggered mobility is a demo feature or a deployable one.

## NTN: Scenarios for the Sky

Non-terrestrial networks fed the 6G requirements report from two directions. THALES, with a coalition of satellite players, brought a text proposal on NTN deployment scenarios ([RP-260068](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260068.zip)) covering channel bandwidth per band aggregation, beam hopping with only a fraction of beams active at once, and geostationary-satellite antenna characteristics defined per band. Ericsson and a broad operator and satellite group fed in the 3GPP-specific requirements on NTN, including the satellite antenna parameters the report needs ([RP-260279](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260279.zip)), and CATT with CSCN offered a parallel deployment-scenarios proposal ([RP-260361](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260361.zip)). The longer-running GNSS-free operation requirement also advanced through a multi-company text proposal led by THALES ([RP-260067](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260067.zip)). The net effect was to resolve a set of satellite antenna and beam parameters that had been left in brackets, so that the NTN portions of TR 38.914 stop being placeholders.

## Low-Tier IoT: How Narrow Is Too Narrow

The sharpest single-parameter fight was about the cheapest possible 6G device. AT&T, with Sony, Semtech, and Nordic Semiconductor, argued that the maximum channel bandwidth for the lowest tier of 6G devices should be capped, with an uplink limit narrow enough to allow surface-acoustic-wave-filterless implementation and minimal cost ([RP-260415](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260415.zip)), reinforcing the point with further considerations on the massive IoT requirements ([RP-260417](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260417.zip)). The counter-pressure was the broad massive-communication requirements paper led by Ericsson and a large operator and vendor bloc ([RP-260475](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260475.zip)), which wants the low-tier device to remain useful rather than be optimised down to a coverage liability. The operator camp, with Deutsche Telekom leading the diverse-device-types text proposal for the requirements report ([RP-260407](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/TSG_RAN/TSGR_111/Docs/RP-260407.zip)), is sensitive to the same trade: a marginal saving in UE complexity is not worth a real penalty in coverage. The bandwidth number for the lowest device tier is exactly the kind of value that looks trivial and quietly determines a market.

## What Fukuoka Locked In

Read as a whole, RAN#111 was a sorting meeting. It hardened the 6G requirements frame, taking TR 38.914 to v0.4.0 and pulling NTN antenna and beam parameters out of brackets. It approved the band and power-class consolidations that keep Rel-20's long tail manageable. It pushed Ambient IoT outdoors toward active devices and gave NR mobility Phase 5 a revised mandate to fix the triggered-mobility framework. It let the NR sensing study declare itself done at v1.0.0 while leaving the 6G sensing use-case scope deliberately open. And it named, without resolving, the two problems most likely to slip: the service-based-versus-integrated RAN architecture, which the working groups will reopen, and the lower-layer security framework, which is hostage to a security group that will not conclude before the freeze. The June plenary, RAN#111's successor, is where Rel-20 stops being negotiable. Fukuoka was the last meeting that could still move a feature across the line, and the companies that wanted to be in Rel-20 spent it making sure they were.
