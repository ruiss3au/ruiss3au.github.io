# RAN4#115 - April 2025, Wuhan, China

Wuhan was the meeting where Rel-19 stopped being debated and started being written.
The previous two meetings, Orlando and Athens, were about frameworks and scoping:
what should we specify, who should lead, which parts are mandatory. Wuhan was about
draft change requests: here is the spec text, here are the parameter values, here
are the test procedures. Seven different work items produced draft CRs for the
first time.

The Rel-20 future was still a faint signal. Seven Rel-20 TDocs appeared, five of
them scoping papers from the same companies that had filed scoping papers in
Athens: Apple, Intel, BT, AT&T, and KDDI. Apple repeated its RRM Phase 6 pitch
[R4-2505939](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505939.zip). BT repeated its 200 MHz bandwidth proposal [R4-2506912](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506912.zip). AT&T
pitched low-band CA switching enhancement [R4-2507343](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2507343.zip). The repetition was not
redundancy: companies that file the same scoping paper across multiple meetings
are signaling that they have not received an answer and want the work item
approved. The Rel-20 signal was growing louder, but RAN4 was still tuning to
Rel-19.

The structural story of Wuhan was the transition from -Core to -Perf across
multiple work items. In 3GPP parlance, a -Core work item defines the
requirements; a -Perf work item defines the conformance tests. When a work item
produces -Perf TDocs, it means the requirements are stable enough to test
against. Wuhan saw NR_duplex_evo-Perf, NR_LPWUS-Perf, NR_MIMO_Ph5-Perf,
NR_NTN_Ph3-Perf, NR_IoT_NTN_req_test_enh-Perf all active for the first time.
The group was shifting from "what should the UE do?" to "how do we prove the UE
does it?"

# LP-WUS: The Normative Phase Begins

LP-WUS was the largest work item at RAN4#115. In Athens it had been the
fourth-largest. In Orlando it had been the fifth. The jump reflected the
transition from discussion to specification: once a feature's parameters are
agreed, the number of specification clauses that need updating multiplies.

## Draft CRs Across the Specification Stack

The draft CRs filed at Wuhan covered the entire LP-WUS specification stack.
HiSilicon opened with a draft CR to TS 38.104 on the general part and power
boosting definition for LP-WUS [R4-2505524](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505524.zip), establishing the BS transmitter
framework: which clauses of TS 38.104 apply to the LP-WUS signal, which are
excluded because LP-WUS uses OOK modulation that does not map to standard NR
transmitter requirements. Nokia followed with draft CRs on unwanted emissions
and transmitter intermodulation [R4-2507087](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2507087.zip), [R4-2508772](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2508772.zip), two of the most
technically demanding BS RF clauses because they require measurements of the
LP-WUS signal's out-of-band energy when transmitted alongside normal NR signals.
The coexistence question from Orlando had a spec-text answer: the LP-WUS
transmitter must meet the same unwanted emission limits as the NR transmitter in
adjacent channels, but relaxed limits apply in channels farther than the LP-WUS
bandwidth from the carrier.

On the UE side, OPPO filed draft CRs on intra-frequency NR cell measurements for
UE with LP-WUR in IDLE and INACTIVE states [R4-2505708](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505708.zip), [R4-2508337](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2508337.zip). These
addressed the practical question of how a UE with its main receiver powered down
maintains the cell reselection measurements needed for mobility. A UE in RRC
Inactive with LP-WUR active cannot measure serving and neighbor cells using the
normal SSB-based measurement procedure because the main receiver is off. OPPO's
CR proposed that the UE perform measurements on the LP-WUS itself, using the
wake-up signal's received power as a proxy for cell quality. The measurement
accuracy would be lower than SSB-based measurements, roughly 3-4 dB worse
standard deviation, but sufficient for cell reselection decisions at low
mobility.

CMCC filed draft CRs introducing inter-frequency and inter-RAT cell reselection
requirements for UE with LP-WUR in RRC Inactive [R4-2506429](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506429.zip), [R4-2508343](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2508343.zip). The
inter-frequency case was harder: the LP-WUS is only transmitted on the serving
carrier, so the UE cannot measure neighbor frequencies using the wake-up signal.
CMCC's CR proposed periodic main-receiver wake-up events where the UE briefly
powers on the NR receiver, performs inter-frequency measurements, and returns to
LP-WUR monitoring. The periodicity was set to match the DRX cycle of the
serving cell, typically 1.28 seconds for connected-mode DRX, meaning the main
receiver wakes for roughly 5 ms every 1.28 seconds, a duty cycle of
approximately 0.4%, still achieving the 99% power savings target.

CMCC also filed a discussion paper on LP-WUS UE RF Rx requirements covering
in-band blocking, out-of-band blocking, intermodulation, and spurious emissions
[R4-2506370](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506370.zip), the full set of receiver performance clauses that would appear in
TS 38.101-1. The discussion paper was a precursor to a Big CR: laying out all
the clauses that need modification before attempting to write the text.

## The Simulation Campaign Intensifies

Apple's simulation collection framework, proposed in Athens, produced its first
fruits in Wuhan. Multiple companies filed simulation results for LP-WUR
performance, but the most significant was the convergence on reference
sensitivity values. The wake-up receiver sensitivity converged to approximately
-97 dBm at 10 MHz bandwidth with a 9 dB noise figure, matching Apple's Athens
proposal. The 3-4 dB margin between the wake-up receiver and the main receiver
sensitivity was accepted as the practical trade-off between power savings and
coverage.

HiSilicon continued its comprehensive approach from Athens with updated versions
of its LP-WUS TDocs covering general aspects, UE REFSENS and ACS, other Rx
requirements, testability, and BS RF. Sanechips maintained its chipset-level
contributions with TR updates on power boosting and spectrum requirements. vivo
continued as specification lead for the UE side, driving the Big CR
consolidation.

The mandate fight described in the introduction was not yet resolved, but the
simulation results were building a case. If the wake-up receiver could meet
-97 dBm sensitivity with 9 dB noise figure, the coverage gap between LP-WUR and
the main receiver was small enough that operators could deploy LP-WUS without
losing cell-edge users. This data would eventually support the mandatory LP-WUR
position for premium devices, the compromise that was still months away.

# SBFD: Spec Text Arrives

SBFD followed a similar arc to LP-WUS: framework setting in Orlando and Athens,
normative specification in Wuhan. The first CRs for SBFD BS and UE requirements
appeared at this meeting, signaling that the feature was entering its
specification-writing phase.

## Duty Cycle and MPR: Ericsson Raises the Hard Questions

Ericsson filed a discussion paper on SBFD symbol UL duty cycle evaluation and
MPR impact on SBFD UL resource muting [R4-2507397](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2507397.zip), the most technically
consequential SBFD contribution at this meeting. The problem: a UE operating
in SBFD mode is scheduled for UL on a sub-band while the gNB is simultaneously
transmitting DL on an adjacent sub-band. The UE's transmit power is limited by
MPR (maximum power reduction), which depends on the modulation scheme and
resource block allocation. In conventional TDD, UL transmission uses the entire
carrier bandwidth, and MPR is calculated for the allocated RBs. In SBFD, the
UL transmission uses only a sub-band, and the adjacent sub-band is occupied
by the gNB's DL transmission.

Ericsson's question was whether the MPR formula needed modification for SBFD.
When the UE transmits on a narrow sub-band surrounded by DL sub-bands, the
spectral regrowth from the UE's power amplifier falls into the adjacent DL
sub-bands, potentially degrading DL performance for UEs being served on those
sub-bands. The existing MPR formula assumes the adjacent spectrum is either
unused (guard band) or used by other UL transmissions (which are at lower power
and further away). Ericsson proposed an additional MPR allowance for SBFD UL
transmissions, effectively reducing the UE's maximum transmit power to protect
DL performance on adjacent sub-bands.

The implication was significant: if SBFD requires additional MPR, the UL link
budget suffers, potentially offsetting the duplexing gain that SBFD promises.
Ericsson's framing of this trade-off was consistent with its long-standing
position that SBFD's theoretical gains must be weighed against practical RF
constraints. CATT's draft CR on updating duty cycle evaluation with SBFD
[R4-2506048](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506048.zip) addressed the complementary problem: the UE's UL duty cycle
calculation, which determines average transmit power and SAR compliance, must
account for the fact that in SBFD the UE transmits only on a sub-band, not the
full carrier.

## CRs for Receiver Requirements

Qualcomm Germany filed a draft CR to TS 38.104 for introducing receiver spurious
and intermodulation requirements [R4-2506532](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506532.zip), marking the first time a
Qualcomm entity led an SBFD specification contribution. Qualcomm's entry into
SBFD BS RF specification was notable because Qualcomm had been largely absent
from the SBFD debate in RAN4, focusing instead on the RAN1 PHY design. The
German office's involvement suggested Qualcomm's European radio design team
taking a more active role in RAN4 work.

CATT filed draft CRs on CSI-RS based beam failure detection evaluation period
under SBFD [R4-2505991](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505991.zip), [R4-2508306](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2508306.zip), addressing how SBFD operation affects the
UE's ability to detect beam failure. When a gNB operates in SBFD mode, the
CSI-RS resources used for beam failure detection may be transmitted on DL
sub-bands that differ from the conventional TDD configuration. The UE must
adjust its measurement period to account for the changed resource mapping.

China Telecom contributed three SBFD discussion papers [R4-2506336](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506336.zip), [R4-2506337](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506337.zip), [R4-2506339](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506339.zip), covering CLI handling RRM requirements, general RRM
impacts, and RRM performance requirements. The operator perspective was
distinct: China Telecom wanted SBFD RRM requirements that were simple enough to
configure in a live network, with as few new parameters as possible.

# Ambient IoT: From Study to Specification

Ambient IoT (Ambient_IoT_Solutions-Core) was the most significant new Rel-19
work item to go normative at Wuhan. The Rel-19 study item (FS_Ambient_IoT_solutions)
had evaluated feasibility. The Wuhan meeting was where RAN4 began writing
specifications for ambient IoT devices and the reader/base station that
communicates with them.

## CATT Leads the Specification Charge

CATT filed the foundational specification contributions for Ambient IoT at this
meeting. A draft CR to TS 38.194 introduced base station output power and
transmit ON/OFF power requirements [R4-2506015](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506015.zip). A second draft CR introduced
transmitter intermodulation requirements [R4-2506016](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506016.zip). A discussion paper
addressed RRM impacts [R4-2506001](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506001.zip), and another addressed device RF
requirements [R4-2506033](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506033.zip).

TS 38.194 is a relatively new specification, created specifically for ambient
IoT and positioning. Adding base station output power and intermodulation
requirements to it was the first step in defining a complete BS conformance
framework for ambient IoT. The ambient IoT reader (the network device that
transmits the carrier wave that powers the tag and receives the backscattered
signal) is functionally different from a standard NR gNB: it transmits a
continuous-wave signal to power passive tags, and its receiver must detect the
very weak backscattered signal (typically -70 to -80 dBm) while its own
transmitter is active.

vivo contributed a draft technical report contribution to TS 38.194 on carrier
wave frequency error and unwanted emissions [R4-2506844](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506844.zip) and a draft CR to
TR 38.870 on test methods for ambient IoT devices [R4-2506845](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506845.zip). The test method
question was critical: how do you test a device that has no battery, no
transmitter, and no antenna connector? An ambient IoT tag harvests energy from
the carrier wave and modulates its reflection coefficient to communicate. You
cannot connect a cable to it and measure transmitter power because it has no
transmitter. The OTA (over-the-air) test methods were the subject of intense
discussion.

## OTA Testing: The Unresolved Question

CMCC filed a discussion on OTA test methods for ambient IoT devices
[R4-2505620](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505620.zip), proposing a reverberation chamber approach where the tag is placed
in a chamber with a known field distribution, the reader transmits the carrier
wave, and the backscattered signal is measured by a reference antenna. Ericsson
contributed RRM requirements for ambient IoT [R4-2506348](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506348.zip) and a TR 38.914
contribution on in-band blocking, out-of-band blocking, and spurious emissions
[R4-2507495](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2507495.zip). vivo discussed RRM impacts [R4-2505661](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505661.zip).

The OTA testing debate was unresolved at Wuhan. The fundamental problem was that
ambient IoT is the first 3GPP device class that is purely passive: no battery,
no oscillator, no active RF components. Every existing RAN4 test methodology
assumes an active device with a transmitter chain and a receiver chain that can
be accessed through a conducted test port. Ambient IoT breaks this assumption,
and the test methodology needed to be invented from scratch.

# NTN Phase 3: The Fragmentation Problem

NTN work at Wuhan was characterized by fragmentation across multiple frequency
bands and use cases. The monolithic NTN Phase 3 work item had splintered into
S-band, L-band, combined L-band, Ku-band, IoT-specific, and RedCap-specific
threads. This was both a sign of maturity (the work was detailed enough to
require separate tracks) and a coordination challenge (the same companies were
contributing to multiple threads, stretching delegation resources).

## Ku-Band NTN Matures

Ku-band NTN (NR_NTN_Ku_bands-Core) grew to its second meeting with significant
volume at Wuhan. The system parameters and coexistence studies from Athens had
been absorbed, and the work moved to specific RF requirements. The VSAT versus
portable terminal debate continued, with Ericsson and Sanechips pushing for
fixed VSAT deployments and Xiaomi advocating for portable terminal support. The
Ku-band specification was taking shape as a GEO-focused, fixed-terminal use
case, which was strategically important for operators wanting to use existing
Ku-band satellite capacity for NR backhaul.

## IoT NTN Testing: Performance Phase

IoT NTN testing (NR_IoT_NTN_req_test_enh-Core and -Perf) continued to be one of
the largest work items, now with a -Perf component indicating that conformance
test definitions were underway. The 3 MHz channel bandwidth work from Athens
was being consolidated into CRs. HiSilicon filed updated draft CRs for 3 MHz
channel bandwidth additions to multiple specifications.

The NTN fragmentation pattern revealed a structural challenge for RAN4: each
new frequency band, each new device class (handheld, VSAT, RedCap, IoT), each
new deployment scenario (GEO, LEO, MEO) multiplied the number of specification
clauses that needed to be written and tested. The work item count for NTN had
grown from essentially one (NR_NTN_Ph3) at the start of Rel-19 to more than ten
distinct work items at Wuhan.

## STxMP: Multi-Panel Transmission OTA Testing

Simultaneous Transmission from Multiple Panels (STxMP) slipped into the Wuhan
meeting almost invisibly: a handful of TDocs under MIMO Phase 5 and OTA test
methodology that addressed a specific and growing concern for FR2 devices. A
multi-panel UE has two or more antenna panels pointing in different directions,
each with its own power amplifier. In Rel-17 and Rel-18, only one panel could
transmit at a time. Rel-19 MIMO Phase 5 introduced simultaneous transmission
from multiple panels.

RAN4's job was to define the RF requirements, and this was where the difficulty
began. When a UE transmits from two panels simultaneously, the total radiated
power is the sum of the power from each panel, but the two beams point in
different directions. An over-the-air measurement must capture the combined
EIRP from both panels, which requires either a multi-probe anechoic chamber or
a two-step measurement procedure that measures each panel separately and
combines the results mathematically.

Qualcomm raised the FR2 OTA test method question for STxMP [R4-2505744](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505744.zip), noting
that existing single-panel OTA test frameworks cannot directly measure the
maximum transmission power of a multi-panel UE. vivo contributed a technical
report proposal to TR 38.771 on FR2 STxMP multi-user and test tolerance
[R4-2506848](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506848.zip), proposing specific test configurations and tolerance values.
Samsung discussed additional test tolerance for STxMP testing [R4-2506974](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506974.zip),
arguing that the measurement uncertainty for multi-panel OTA is inherently
higher than for single-panel, and that the specification must allow for this
additional uncertainty in the conformance limits.

The debate was quiet compared to SBFD or LP-WUS, but the challenge was
fundamental: STxMP extended the OTA testing problem from one spatial direction
to multiple directions, and the test equipment industry had not yet standardized
multi-probe OTA measurement procedures. The resolution was to define a two-step
test method where each panel is measured separately under the assumption that
their beams are sufficiently separated in angle that mutual coupling between
panels is negligible. This assumption would be debated at subsequent meetings.

# Rel-20: Seven Signals in a Sea of Rel-19

The seven Rel-20 TDocs at Wuhan fell into two categories: repeated scoping
papers and new technical proposals.

## 6 GHz MPR Relaxation: HiSilicon's Calculated Move

HiSilicon filed a technical Rel-20 proposal on MPR reduction for upper 6 GHz
through ACLR relaxation [R4-2506832](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506832.zip). This was the first non-scoping Rel-20
RAN4 contribution. The proposal was specific and technical: for devices
operating in the 6.425-7.125 GHz band (the upper part of the 6 GHz range
allocated for IMT at WRC-23), the adjacent channel leakage ratio (ACLR)
requirements could be relaxed by 2-3 dB compared to sub-6 GHz bands, allowing
MPR to be reduced correspondingly. The physics argument was that the wider
channel bandwidths available at 6 GHz (up to 100 MHz) provide more guard band
between channels, and the higher free-space path loss at 6 GHz reduces the
interference coupling between devices on adjacent channels.

The contribution was strategically important because it addressed a practical
deployment barrier for 6 GHz NR: if MPR requirements are too conservative, the
UE's maximum transmit power is unnecessarily reduced, and the coverage of 6 GHz
deployments suffers. By filing a technical Rel-20 proposal rather than a scoping
paper, HiSilicon was positioning itself as the specification lead for 6 GHz UE
RF requirements, a role that would become increasingly important as the 7-24
GHz range work expanded.

## Non-Collocated Deployment: KDDI's Niche Pitch

KDDI, the Japanese operator, filed a Rel-20 motivation paper on intra-band
non-collocated EN-DC and NR-CA deployment for handheld UEs
[R4-2506893](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506893.zip). Non-collocated deployment means the LTE anchor and NR secondary
cell are transmitted from different physical sites, which is common in Japanese
networks where LTE and NR equipment are deployed on different tower locations
due to site acquisition constraints. KDDI's proposal addressed a specific
Japanese deployment scenario, but the technical requirements (timing advance
management, power control across non-collocated carriers, MPR for inter-site
intermodulation) had broader applicability.

KDDI also filed an observation on UE simulation assumptions for A-MPR (additional
MPR) [R4-2506896](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506896.zip), providing operator measurement data on real-world UE MPR
behavior. Apple's repeated RRM Phase 6 pitch [R4-2505939](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2505939.zip), BT's 200 MHz way
forward [R4-2506912](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506912.zip), Intel's 5G-Advanced views [R4-2506889](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2506889.zip), and AT&T's
low-band CA switching enhancement [R4-2507343](https://www.3gpp.org/ftp/tsg_ran/WG4_Radio/TSGR4_115/Docs/R4-2507343.zip) rounded out the Rel-20
contributions. The pattern from Athens was repeating: companies were filing the
same scoping papers meeting after meeting.

# NR Mobility Phase 4: LTM Measurement Gaps

NR Mobility Phase 4 (NR_Mob_Ph4-Core) was the seventh-largest work item at
Wuhan with contributions spread across measurement gap design, LTM execution
phase measurements, and CHO (conditional handover) RRM requirements. The work
was procedural: the L1/L2 triggered mobility (LTM) framework had been agreed in
RAN1, and RAN4 was defining the RRM measurement configurations that would
support it.

The key technical question was measurement gap design for LTM execution. During
a conventional handover, the UE interrupts data transmission to measure the
target cell during a measurement gap. LTM aims to eliminate this interruption by
performing target cell measurements before the handover is triggered. But the
candidate cell list for LTM can be large (up to 8 cells), and the UE must
measure all candidates while maintaining the serving cell connection.

Apple and Qualcomm contributed competing measurement gap proposals. Apple's
approach favored longer measurement gaps with fewer candidate cells, optimizing
for measurement accuracy and battery life. Qualcomm's approach favored shorter
gaps with more candidates, optimizing for handover speed. The debate mirrored
the broader device-versus-network tension that runs through RAN4: device vendors
optimize for the UE experience, infrastructure vendors optimize for network
performance. The compromise, as in most RAN4 measurement gap debates, was a
configurable framework where the network chooses the trade-off.

# Company Dynamics: The Three-Way Tie at the Top

Ericsson and HiSilicon ran essentially tied at the top of RAN4#115, separated
by a margin small enough to be noise. Nokia held a strong third. The three-way
balance at the top was a change from Athens, where HiSilicon had a clear lead.
The rebalancing reflected the meeting's character: Wuhan was a specification-
writing meeting, and Ericsson and Nokia, as infrastructure vendors, are the
companies that write the BS RF specifications. HiSilicon and Sanechips, as UE
chipset vendors, write the UE RF specifications. When the meeting shifts from
framework discussions (where any company can contribute) to CR drafting (where
only companies that will implement the feature write the text), the
infrastructure vendors regain share.

Apple surged further, becoming a clear fourth-place contributor behind the
infrastructure trio. Apple's growth was driven by RRM requirements across
multiple work items (LP-WUS RRM, SBFD RRM, mobility RRM, NTN RRM) and the
Rel-20 RRM Phase 6 scoping paper. Apple's strategy of influencing RRM
specifications was paying off in contribution volume.

Qualcomm rose noticeably to mid-tier, driven by EN-DC RF Phase 4 and carrier
aggregation contributions plus the SBFD receiver CR. Samsung and MediaTek
maintained their mid-tier positions. The Chinese ecosystem (HiSilicon,
Sanechips, CATT, vivo, CMCC, OPPO, China Telecom, Xiaomi) collectively
remained the largest bloc.

Anritsu, Rohde & Schwarz, and Keysight Technologies all filed contributions,
reflecting the meeting's shift to conformance testing. Test equipment vendors
appear when specifications are stable enough to test.

# Looking Ahead to RAN1#122

RAN4#115 was the last meeting before RAN1 took over the narrative. The next
meeting in the series is RAN1#122 in Bangalore, the first RAN1 meeting where
